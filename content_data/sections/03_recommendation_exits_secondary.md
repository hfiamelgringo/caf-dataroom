## Exits and Secondary Markets

Across all of Latin America, only 6379 VC-backed exits were recorded in 20254, with an average exit value of USD 175 million. This stands in sharp contrast to Southeast Asia, where over 400 exits were recorded in the same period with an average exit value of USD 280 million. In the four target countries, the number of exits has been small. Over the past five years, Guatemala, Costa Rica, Dominican Republic, and Honduras combined have recorded fewer than a dozen VC-backed exits of meaningful scale. ([LAVCA, 2025](https://www.lavca.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/02/LAVCA_Trends-in-Tech-2025.pdf))

The absence of exit opportunities fundamentally undermines investor incentives. International investors are explicitly seeking scale potential and defined exit pathways. Without clear exit mechanisms, international capital will not flow into the region at the scale required to transform the ecosystem.

Costa Rica's Bolsa Nacional de Valores has taken preliminary steps toward establishing a secondary market for startup equity, including the creation of a special listing category for small and medium enterprises. However, the initiative remains nascent and has generated limited activity to date. More broadly, the regulatory frameworks across all four countries lack standardized, liquid secondary markets for private equity. ### Financing Secondary Liquidity and M&A

CAF could support the development of secondary liquidity mechanisms by:

* Providing technical assistance to securities regulators to design secondary market frameworks tailored to early-stage companies
* Co-investing in or guaranteeing returns for secondary market transactions to demonstrate viability and reduce perceived risk
* Facilitating cross-border secondary market transactions within the region
* Developing standardized valuation and pricing mechanisms for private equity transactions
* Provide technical assistance to securities regulators to design secondary market frameworks and provide liquidity mechanisms for private equity transactions.

The emergence of secondary markets is not a quick fix. However, a coordinated regional effort led by CAF could establish the regulatory and institutional infrastructure required to facilitate exits and secondary transactions over a 3-5 year timeframe. This is precisely the kind of structural, multi-year engagement that CAF is positioned to lead.
