## Guatemala

Guatemala is the largest economy among the four countries by GDP (USD 86 billion), but the venture ecosystem remains relatively nascent. The country is characterized by a large informal economy, concentrated wealth, and limited development of capital markets and financial infrastructure. Historically, capital flows have been dominated by remittances, foreign direct investment in agriculture and textiles, and private equity buyouts of family-owned businesses by international investors.

### Macroeconomic Context

Guatemala's GDP has grown at an average annual rate of 2.8 percent over the past decade, below regional averages and below the 3-4 percent growth rates required to generate meaningful poverty reduction. The economy is heavily dependent on agriculture (coffee, sugar, bananas) and textiles. Manufacturing and services account for roughly 30 percent and 55 percent of GDP, respectively. Foreign direct investment (FDI) has remained relatively stable at 1.5-2 percent of GDP annually. ([World Bank, 2024a](https://data.worldbank.org/indicator/NY.GDP.MKTP.CD?locations=GT))

### Venture Capital Market

Venture capital investment in Guatemala has been minimal historically. Available data from Cuántico VP and Startuplinks indicate that annual VC investment in Guatemala has ranged from USD 15-40 million over the past three years, concentrated in a small number of software and fintech companies. Most VC investment has been sourced from foreign investors, with limited domestic institutional investment. ([Cuántico VP & Startuplinks, 2026](https://www.startuplinks.world/reportes/latin-america-venture-capital-report-2025/))

### Entrepreneurial Ecosystem

Guatemala City hosts the majority of entrepreneurial activity and VC investment. The city is home to several accelerators and incubators, including INCUBADORA STARTUP GUATEMALA, EY Start, and others. Entrepreneurs and ecosystem participants report strong demand for networking and support services but limited availability of follow-on capital for promising startups.

*“Guatemala has entrepreneurial energy but limited capital. The disconnect is stark: accelerators are oversubscribed with quality entrepreneurs, but investors complain they cannot find enough good investment opportunities. This is a classic thick supply, thin demand problem.” — Interview, Guatemalan VC Investor, 2026*

### Regulatory Environment

Guatemala's regulatory environment for venture capital is underdeveloped. Securities regulations date to the 1960s and were not designed for modern venture capital structures. The country lacks clear regulatory guidance on venture capital fund formation, PIPE structures, and secondary market transactions. ### Pension Fund Landscape

Guatemala's pension fund system is private and mandatory, with approximately 2.5 million contributors and managed assets of approximately USD 20 billion. However, pension fund allocation to alternative investments including venture capital is minimal (less than 0.1 percent of assets). Regulatory constraints, fiduciary concerns, and limited capacity for alternative investment evaluation have restricted pension fund participation in VC. This represents a significant opportunity for ecosystem development.

### Capital Markets Infrastructure

Guatemala's Bolsa de Valores (stock exchange) is underdeveloped, with limited liquidity and only a handful of listed companies. Market capitalization is less than 10 percent of GDP, indicating very limited capital market infrastructure. The absence of a functioning secondary market for private equity creates challenges for exits and investor returns. ([World Bank, 2024e](https://data.worldbank.org/indicator/CM.MKT.LCAP.GD.ZS))
