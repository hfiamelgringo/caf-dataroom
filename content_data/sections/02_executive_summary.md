# Executive Summary

All four countries are "thin markets" where entrepreneurs complain there are not enough investors or investment capital and investors complain there are not enough credible VC opportunities, resulting in a double-sided market failure.

Foreign investors continue to prefer scale potential, predictability, and ease of exit. With some exceptions, domestic investors (corporates, family offices) prefer to invest in their own companies or offshore (higher returns, diversification), rather than in companies they do not control in their own country.

Accelerators and incubators have been part of the ecosystem strengthening efforts for many years, and both entrepreneurs and investors recognize their value, particularly for networking opportunities and for concrete, technical support tailored to their needs. They also continue to value targeted networking events and conferences such as Caricaco, FLII, Volcano Summit, IDB's GET Forum, and others.

With respect to CAF's role however, managing, coordinating and funding these efforts is both costly and time-intensive. Rather than competing with or replicating existing initiatives, CAF should remain closely engaged with accelerators and incubators, potentially through modest sponsorships, to ensure that its staff stay embedded in current thinking and networks, and can leverage these platforms as pipeline generation channels for its investments.

There are two benefits to ecosystem building: direct benefits to participants, and longer-term spillover effects to the broader economy and public sector through what we refer to as the Reinvestment Effect, discussed in detail later. The direct benefits lie in the dynamism and opportunities these ecosystems create for emerging entrepreneurs, exposing them to regional and global dynamics even when they operate within less developed environments.

The Reinvestment Effect, however, extends well beyond supporting the ambitions of a small group of founders and early investors. Evidence from the last 10 years on unicorn exits shows that such outcomes generate a cascading flow of capital, knowledge, and talent across the ecosystem. In many cases a significant share of this reinvestment remains geographically concentrated, allowing countries to compound benefits locally.

This dynamic underscores why fostering geographic concentration of investment should be a priority for the public sector, even if it is not typically a focus for private investors, whether domestic or international.

Despite the coordination, logistical, and institutional challenges inherent to joint efforts among multilateral development banks (MDBs), both investors and entrepreneurs consistently express frustration with the complexity of engaging each MDB under distinct policies and procedures. They would welcome a more unified approach. While this is neither a new nor particularly novel critique, it remains a persistent source of friction. Some degree of fragmentation is inevitable, and competition can be beneficial for ecosystem development. However, in areas such as regulatory guidance to governments, MDBs should align and act in concert wherever possible.

CAF's growth over the past five years positions it as an emerging and credible player in this space relative to peers such as Inter-American Development Bank and World Bank. This positioning creates an opportunity for CAF to take a more proactive leadership role in convening and coordinating joint efforts. For example, increase entrepreneurship themes in its annual conference in Panama or convene the MDB's in a joint "Startup Ecosystem Toolkit for Governments" akin to the product by IDB Invest's or IDB's for Investors or Entrepreneurs, or the MDB Joint Report.

Entrepreneurs are not "taught" at scale, *per se* but they emerge because the system rewards them. The same is true for Investors and Limited Partners (LPs). Their participation is shaped by incentives, market structure, and the perceived risk-return profile of the ecosystem. CAF should avoid narrowly focusing on supply-side human capital efforts to train entrepreneurs or support startups and instead focus CAF's resources on market formation. The objective is thus not to produce more entrepreneurs directly, but to construct a system in which entrepreneurship becomes a rational and scalable outcome. In other words, the emphasis should be to engineer markets that produce entrepreneurs.

**Systemic and Strategic Engagement:** Instead of "giving a person a fish,” or teaching someone to fish, CAF should help build the coastline where fishing is inevitable. In other words, beyond narrow programs for accelerators or incubators or one-off efforts that were previously supported by USAID, philanthropy and others, CAF's role should be more systematic, structural, and regional. Leveraging its scale, convening power, and close relationships with governments, CAF is uniquely positioned to advance coordinated, cross-border efforts rather than fragmented, individual programs. Within this framework, four potential opportunities emerge.
