# Four Macro Recommendations

## Public Resources

Entrepreneurs ask whycomplain that local businesses and family offices are not investing in local startupstheir peers. The simple answer is that many prefer to invest in their own companies, where they have control and information advantages, or invest offshore where returns are higher and diversification is greater. Absent meaningful regulatory changes or a substantial inflow of international capital, these incentives will not dramatically shift. However, there is one domestic source of capital that has been dramatically underutilized in the region: pension funds.

Pension funds in Mexico have been gradually shifted from a state-administered system to a private system with significant allocation to alternative investments, including VC and private equity. As a result, Mexican pension funds have become meaningful sources of capital for venture capital and private equity funds. In Chile, a similar process is underway and starting to show impact. The pension fund allocation to alternative investments is reshaping the venture ecosystem in both countries.

To address the same double-sided market failure in Guatemala, Costa Rica, the Dominican Republic, and Honduras, one high-potential area is pension fund reform. These countries have pension funds that are substantially more conservative in their allocation strategies than their Chilean or Mexican counterparts. In particular, the Dominican Republic and Honduras have pension funds with significant assets, but conservative allocation patterns. ([CONSAR, 2024; Fund Pro, 2024](https://fundpro.com/consar-updates-afore-investment-regime/))

*“Institutional investors like pension funds are often the linchpin of venture ecosystems. In emerging markets, government-backed efforts to allocate pension funds to VC and alternative investments have proven to be a more effective lever than direct government subsidies or grants.” — Interview, Global VC Policy Advisor, 2024*

\[PLACEHOLDER: PFA Pension Funds\] provides a detailed analysis of pension fund reform opportunities in the four countries, exploring the regulatory, governance, and incentive structures required to redirect a small but meaningful share of pension fund allocations toward VC and alternative investments. The analysis shows that modest reforms to allocation rules, fiduciary frameworks, and governance structures could unlock significant capital flows without creating undue risk.
