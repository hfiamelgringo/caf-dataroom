## Costa Rica

Costa Rica is the smallest of the four economies by GDP (USD 63 billion) but has the most developed venture ecosystem. The country has benefited from sustained ecosystem-building efforts, significant inflows of foreign direct investment, and a relatively strong institutional and regulatory framework. Costa Rica's venture ecosystem is more mature and internationally oriented than the other three countries, with regular exit activity and meaningful international investor participation.

### Macroeconomic Context

Costa Rica's economy has grown at an average annual rate of 2.5 percent over the past decade, slightly below Guatemala's rate, but with higher per capita income and more diversified economic structure. The economy is heavily dependent on tourism and services, which account for roughly 50 percent of GDP. Agriculture and agribusiness remain important but declining sectors. Costa Rica has attracted significant FDI, particularly from technology and electronics companies. ([World Bank, 2024b](https://data.worldbank.org/indicator/NY.GDP.MKTP.CD?locations=CR))

### Venture Capital Market

Costa Rica leads the region in VC investment. Annual VC investment in Costa Rica has ranged from USD 50-150 million over the past three years, with higher concentrations in software, fintech, and climate technology. The country has produced several successful exits, including acquisitions by international technology companies. Costa Rica's VC market is more mature and more internationally oriented than the other three countries, with significant investor participation from North America and Europe. ([Cuántico VP & Startuplinks, 2026](https://www.startuplinks.world/reportes/latin-america-venture-capital-report-2025/))

### Entrepreneurial Ecosystem

San José and its surrounding metropolitan area host the majority of entrepreneurial activity. The city has a well-developed accelerator and incubator ecosystem, with organizations including CAAS (Costa Rican Alliance of Acceleration Services), IYC, and others. Multiple networking events and conferences attract regional and international investors. The ecosystem benefits from historical connections to Silicon Valley and a relative abundance of software engineering talent.

*“Costa Rica has the pieces of a maturing ecosystem: capital, talent, accelerators, and deal flow. The challenge is scaling beyond the current base of 100-200 active angel and VC investors.” — Interview, Costa Rican Ecosystem Analyst, 2026*

### Regulatory Environment

Costa Rica's regulatory environment is the most developed among the four countries. Securities regulations were updated in the 1990s and have been refined over time. The Superintendencia General de Valores (SUGEVAL) has issued guidance on venture capital structures and provides clearer regulatory pathways for fund formation. However, gaps remain in secondary market regulation and tax treatment of carried interest. (*Ley No. 7732; Reglamento SUGEVAL 19-20*)

### Pension Fund Landscape

Costa Rica's pension fund system is mixed private and public, with approximately 2 million contributors and managed assets of approximately USD 22 billion. However, pension fund allocation to alternative investments is limited. Recent policy discussions have explored pension fund participation in venture capital, but regulatory changes have not been implemented at scale.

### Capital Markets Infrastructure

Costa Rica's Bolsa Nacional de Valores (BNV) is the most developed capital market in the region. The exchange has several hundred listed companies and reasonable liquidity in major indices. Recent efforts to create a secondary market for startup equity through a special listing category represent a significant step toward exit infrastructure development.

# SMALL IDEAS

In addition to the four big ideas presented above, PFA includes a few specific “small ideas” as part of what could be mobilized soon and with modest resources.

1) **Dedicated VC Fund Regulation Support:** CAF should designate a staff member or small team of experts to serve as a central expertise repository for the regulatory environment in all CAF member countries and work to incorporate insights and recommendations across CAF’s engagements in each country. Including development and promotion of standardized SAFE, convertible note, and equity investment templates adapted to each jurisdiction’s corporate law.
2) **Public Fund Foundational Work:** CAF can commission technical studies on pension fund allocation reform grounded in international precedents from Mexico, Chile, and Colombia. Early efforts could be with regulations in the Dominican Republic (Ley No. 87-01) and Costa Rica to permit limited allocation to regulated VC and PE vehicles. Specifically, CAF could explore weather it makes sense to promote a legislative amendment to the Dominican Republic’s Ley 87-01 to permit pension fund foreign investment in qualified regional structures.
3) **Governance Discipline and Industry Standards:** By establishing rigorous governance expectations in the funds it supports, consistent with ILPA Principles 3.0, CAF can contribute to the professionalization of the industry. This includes promoting independent investment committees, transparent reporting frameworks, standardized impact measurement methodologies, and the adoption of ESG standards consistent with global institutional investor expectations.
