## Themes Across the Four

### Capital Market Shallowness

The primary macro constraint in all four countries is the shallowness (few available instruments, low liquidity, low capitalization, low institutional investor diversity) of local capital markets. Stock market capitalization as a percentage of GDP ranges from 12-18 percent across the four countries, compared to 40-60 percent in developed markets and 25-35 percent in more developed emerging markets. This shallow capital market structure translates directly into limited exit opportunities, reduced investor appetite for venture investment, and a scarcity of experienced venture investors.

### No Formal Restrictions on VC Activity

Notably, all four countries lack formal legal restrictions on venture capital investment or private equity. Securities regulations do not explicitly prohibit pension fund investment in VC vehicles, and corporate law does not restrict investment by family offices or other domestic investors. The constraints are thus not principally legal but structural and incentive-driven. This finding is significant because it suggests that many barriers to VC ecosystem development can be addressed through regulatory guidance and policy reform rather than comprehensive legal overhauls.

### Exits Remain Rare

Exit activity across the region remains minimal. In all four countries combined, VC-backed exits (including acquisitions, IPOs, and secondary sales) total fewer than a dozen over the past five years. The absence of exit data creates a powerful negative signal to potential investors: the region does not produce successful, scalable businesses. This perception, whether accurate or not, powerfully shapes investor incentives and capital allocation.

### AI and Technology Specialization

Across all four countries, AI and software development are emerging as leading technology sectors attracting investor interest. Other sectors include fintech, agritech, and healthcare technology. However, the dominance of software-based solutions reflects investor preferences for scalability and the relative scarcity of venture investment in harder tech, energy, or manufacturing sectors where the region has traditional comparative advantages. This mirrors Silicon Valley where 80–90 percent of all VC investments currently are into AI companies or AI-adjacent companies.

### Impact Evolution

Impact investing frameworks have gained some skepticism in the region, with many entrepreneurs and investors framing their activities around “Profit+Impact” versus “Impact+Profit”. Interviewees explained that businesses must prioritize survival before they can meaningfully focus on impact. Compounding this, impact metrics remain poorly standardized, though donors tend to be lenient on definitions, treating impact goals as aspirational rather than holding them to a rigorous standard. As a result, impact investing has yet to emerge as a distinct asset class with dedicated institutional investors or dedicated capital sources.
