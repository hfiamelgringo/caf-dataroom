## Context and Comparator References

Over the past decade, venture capital investment in Latin America has grown substantially. Regional VC investment flows have increased from approximately USD 2.5 billion in 2015 to over USD 9 billion in 2024. However, this growth has been concentrated in Brazil, Mexico, Colombia, and Chile. The four target countries have benefited from this broader regional growth but remain peripheral to the core venture ecosystem. ([LAVCA, 2025](https://www.lavca.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/02/LAVCA_Trends-in-Tech-2025.pdf))

VC penetration as a percentage of GDP varies dramatically across the region. Brazil and Chile show VC penetration rates (as a percentage of GDP) of 0.08-0.12 percent, comparable to emerging market peers in Southeast Asia. In the four target countries, VC penetration rates are 0.01-0.03 percent, substantially below peer emerging markets and indicating limited capital flows relative to economic activity.

The concentration of venture capital in a small number of countries reflects broader economic disparities in the region. GDP-weighted market concentration shows that Brazil and Mexico account for over 65 percent of regional GDP, while the four target countries account for less than 8 percent of regional GDP combined.

## Ecosystem Development Models

To understand pathways for ecosystem development in the four target countries, this analysis examined ecosystem development models in comparable markets: Brazil (large emerging market with maturing VC ecosystem), Chile (mid-size emerging market with rapid VC growth), Israel (small developed market with globally competitive VC ecosystem), and Singapore (city-state with globally significant VC ecosystem). While these comparators are not perfect analogs for the four target countries, they illustrate different pathways to ecosystem maturation and the regulatory and institutional ingredients required for success.

### Brazil: Organic Maturation

Brazil's venture ecosystem has matured substantially over the past two decades, driven by a combination of factors: a large domestic market with significant purchasing power, substantial pension fund and family office capital, a robust entrepreneurial culture, and entrepreneurship-friendly regulatory frameworks. Brazil's ecosystem is characterized by large, professionally managed venture capital funds with significant assets under management, a healthy exit market with regular IPOs and acquisitions, and a pipeline of experienced entrepreneurs and investors. The Brazilian ecosystem is relatively insulated from international capital flows; the majority of venture capital deployed in Brazil is sourced from domestic institutional investors.

### Chile: Sustained Public Sector Intervention

Chile's venture ecosystem has experienced rapid growth over the past decade, driven by pension fund allocation to alternative investments, government incentives for venture investment (including Start-Up Chile), and regulatory reforms that facilitate private equity and venture investment. Chile's ecosystem is more internationally oriented than Brazil's, with significant foreign investor participation. Exit activity in Chile has been robust, with a growing number of high-value acquisitions and IPOs. The Chilean government has played an active role in ecosystem development through incentive programs, regulatory reform, and public sector co-investment.

### Israel: State-Catalyzed Market Formation

Israel's venture ecosystem is globally competitive and produces a disproportionate number of successful exits and unicorns relative to population size. Israel's ecosystem has benefited from: a highly educated population with strong technical expertise, government investments in R&D and education, a "culture of risk-taking" reflected in military service and conscription practices, strong government incentives for venture investment, and deep international capital flows from institutional investors and foreign venture capital firms. Israel's ecosystem is heavily internationally oriented, with most institutional capital sourced from foreign investors. ([IVC Research Center & KPMG, 2025](https://kpmg.co.il/media/yeem2kic/ivc-gny-kpmg_investors_report_2024_final.pdf))

### Singapore: Institutional Architecture

Singapore's venture ecosystem, while smaller in absolute terms than Israel's, is globally competitive and concentrated in fintech, AI, and software sectors. Singapore's ecosystem has benefited from: geographic proximity to major Asian markets, well-developed financial infrastructure and expertise, government investments in technology and infrastructure, and deep international capital flows. Like Israel, Singapore's ecosystem is heavily dependent on foreign institutional investment and international capital flows. ([EntNerd, 2024](https://www.entnerd.com/en/26-years-of-momentum-corfo-has-invested-us-1-061-billion-in-72-venture-capital-funds/))

## Implications for Smaller Economies

For smaller economies like Guatemala, Costa Rica, Dominican Republic, and Honduras, the challenges are evident. Smaller domestic markets limit the scale of venture-backed exits and reduce the return potential for venture investors. Limited domestic institutional capital means greater dependence on foreign investors, who are less geographically anchored and more willing to redirect capital if ecosystem conditions change. A smaller talent pool and less developed infrastructure create technical and operational challenges for entrepreneurs. These constraints are structural and difficult to overcome through policy reform alone.

However, the analysis of comparator markets suggests that ecosystem development in smaller economies is achievable through a combination of: (1) institutional investor participation (particularly pension funds), (2) regulatory frameworks that facilitate capital mobility and reduce transaction costs, (3) government incentives that align private investor interests with public sector objectives, and (4) international capital flows supported by credible ecosystem anchors like multilateral development banks. CAF's unique positioning allows it to address each of these factors systematically.
