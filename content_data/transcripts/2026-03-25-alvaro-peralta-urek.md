## Executive Summary
Álvaro Peralta offers a candid insider view of Costa Rica's innovation ecosystem as small, internationally connected, but structurally fragmented. He identifies a clear funding valley-of-death between early-stage public seed capital and meaningful scale-up investment, attributing it largely to startups arriving at later funding stages poorly prepared — lacking market validation, polished pitches, and regulatory literacy. In sectors like biopharma and ag-tech, regulatory misunderstanding early in the process has caused companies to lose years of work and revert to earlier development stages.

Peralta argues strongly for sector-focused prioritization — climate tech, ag-tech, human health, and animal health — pointing to Costa Rica's existing strengths in medical device manufacturing (over 120 companies) and its emerging biotech cluster. He flags a predatory capital dynamic in which desperate startups accept convertible notes at 18% interest in USD, and notes that family offices predominantly seek majority control rather than venture-style minority stakes. His overarching recommendation is that any CAF program must invest upstream in startup preparation and commit to sector specialization rather than broad-based support.

## Stakeholder Background
Álvaro Peralta has spent over a decade working at the intersection of green and environmental biotechnology, residual biomass valorization, and waste transformation in Costa Rica. He sits on the board of the Global Life Center Hub, a private-public effort focused on digital health and clinical research, and advises Ocean Oysis, an NGO running a blue economy accelerator with an aquaculture innovation center on Costa Rica's Pacific coast. His vantage point spans early-stage venture ecosystems, biotech clusters, public policy engagement, and regional Latin American comparisons, including work with the Inter-American Development Bank on research input supply-chain mapping.

## Key Findings

### Costa Rica's ecosystem is small, connected, but fragmented — with pockets of genuine strength
Costa Rica's innovation ecosystem covers roughly 5 million people concentrated in four cities in the Central Valley. While everyone knows everyone, coordination is weak outside organized clusters. The strongest sectors — cybersecurity/AI, biotech (organized through the national biotech cluster), medical devices, and advanced manufacturing for aerospace — are well-connected internally but poorly linked to each other, creating a fragmented rather than integrated national ecosystem.

### A 120-company medical device manufacturing base is an underutilized innovation anchor
Costa Rica is among the top two global exporters of medical devices, with over 120 manufacturing companies in-country. A growing subset is beginning to conduct local R&D: Boston Scientific is running cardiac valve trials on horses in partnership with the National University's veterinary facility, creating a shared testing infrastructure that other ecosystem actors can now access. This manufacturing-to-innovation pipeline represents a structural asset that remains underdeveloped.

### The funding gap is real, and startups are arriving at the gap underprepared
Public seed capital exists in multiple forms, and some pre-scaling funds are available, but scale-up funding is scarce. Conversations with multiple investment funds converge on the same diagnosis: startups are arriving at Series A-equivalent stages without market validation, refined pitches, or regulatory roadmaps. This is not just a capital supply problem — it reflects a systemic failure in earlier-stage preparation that must be addressed upstream before later-stage fund-building will succeed.

### Regulatory IP constraints actively push biotech companies out of Costa Rica
Costa Rica is a signatory to the Nagoya Protocol and has national legislation restricting patents on naturally occurring microorganisms and molecules. A researcher who discovers an extremophile microorganism with commercially valuable properties — for instance, drought-stress resistance — cannot patent it domestically, eliminating commercial viability. As a result, companies quietly take their research abroad to complete development, a workaround that is legally ambiguous and represents a significant ecosystem leakage problem.

### Family offices operate with a control-acquisition logic incompatible with venture-stage startups
Local family offices typically seek 51–60% equity stakes, take operational control, scale, and exit — a private equity-style model unsuited to early-stage ventures that need growth-friendly cap tables. Angel-style investors exist in loosely organized investment clubs (Carao Ventures is cited as having regional track record), but they are not well-coordinated. In the vacuum, desperate startups are accepting convertible debt instruments at 18% annual interest in USD — predatory terms that reflect a genuine lack of accessible early-growth capital.

### The most successful Costa Rican startups exit the local capital market entirely
Once startups achieve product-market fit, the dominant path is incorporation in Delaware and fundraising in the United States. This pattern holds across sectors — software, biotech, applications — and even among companies that retain R&D or product development activities in Costa Rica. The local ecosystem effectively serves as an incubation ground whose successful outputs are then captured by the US venture capital system.

### Sector specialization is essential — and politically difficult to implement
Peralta argues that CAF and national policymakers should concentrate on a small number of proven sectors: climate tech, ag-tech, human health, and animal health. He draws on Ireland's pharma/biotech bet and Spain's biotechnology association experience — where focused policy and regulatory clarity helped grow the sector from near-zero to over 500 companies in 20 years — as instructive models. He acknowledges the political difficulty of explicit sector prioritization in small countries, where broad-based support tends to be favored for optics reasons.

### Research input supply chains are a pan-regional bottleneck, not unique to Costa Rica
A reagent that arrives in a San Diego lab within hours can take weeks to reach a Costa Rican laboratory. This lead-time problem — documented in an Inter-American Development Bank mapping study Peralta contributed to — applies across Latin America with country-specific variations, but consistently undermines the competitiveness of local biotech and health research relative to US hubs. It is a structural cost that compounds other disadvantages in the innovation pipeline.

## Regulatory & Structural Observations
Costa Rica's Nagoya Protocol implementation is the most acute regulatory constraint Peralta identifies. While the existence of a legal framework is positive in principle, its practical effect is to block commercialization of naturally occurring biological materials discovered domestically — a direct barrier to biotech venture formation. He contrasts this with the Spanish model, where regulatory clarity (not regulatory laxity) enabled a rapid growth in biotech enterprises over two decades.

He also notes that in regulated industries — biopharma, animal health products, agricultural inputs — failure to map the regulatory pathway from the outset can be catastrophic. He cites a case where a company progressed to pre-clinical trials before discovering it had not met the in vitro requirements of its contract lab, forcing a four-year regression. This underlines the need for regulatory coaching as a core component of any early-stage acceleration program, not an afterthought.

## Recommendations & Insights
- CAF should concentrate investment and programming on Costa Rica's naturally strong sectors — climate tech, ag-tech, human health, animal health — rather than attempting broad ecosystem support.
- Any fund or accelerator strategy must invest heavily in upstream startup preparation: market validation discipline, pitch quality, and especially regulatory pathway literacy for life sciences ventures.
- The medical device manufacturing cluster (120+ companies) offers a concrete entry point for connecting multinational R&D needs with local startup and research capacity — Boston Scientific's partnership with the National University is a replicable model.
- Spain's biotechnology association (Asebio) and Ireland's biotech regulatory environment are worth studying as policy templates for Costa Rica and the broader region.
- CAF should be aware that capital deployed locally may largely benefit Delaware-incorporated entities — any program design should explicitly address whether and how to retain value in-country.
- The convertible note dynamic at 18% USD is a red flag: a bridge financing instrument or first-loss facility that provides patient, reasonably priced growth capital could be highly impactful given the current vacuum.
