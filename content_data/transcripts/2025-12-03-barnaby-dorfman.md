## Executive Summary

Barnaby Dorfman brings a rare combination of deep product-engineering experience — spanning Amazon's early marketplace, two PE-backed SaaS companies scaled from single-digit to over $100M ARR, and current AI strategy work across a 25-company portfolio — to a conversation about whether the US vertical SaaS private equity model can be transplanted to Latin America. His core thesis is that AI has dramatically compressed the time and capital required to build software, making it feasible to construct mobile-first, Latin America-native B2B SaaS platforms that leapfrog existing fragmented solutions. He points to specific underserved verticals — legal practice management, gas delivery logistics, insurance claims adjudication — where US incumbents exist but Latin American equivalents are either absent or built on decade-old web technology.

Dorfman also offers a candid structural critique: the LatAm venture ecosystem has abundant seed capital but a near-total absence of growth capital at the Series A and B stages, which creates a ceiling for talented founders who lack US banking networks. His preferred model is not venture but PE — buying bootstrapped profitable companies, modernizing them with AI, and growing them to $25–50M ARR before selling, essentially replicating the Mainsale playbook regionally. He sees WhatsApp as the dominant distribution channel and argues that voice- and text-based AI agents built natively into WhatsApp could unlock entire service verticals that currently run on informal radio networks, clipboard processes, or word of mouth.

For CAF and fund-of-funds strategy, Dorfman's implicit message is that the standard VC model of seeding many startups and hoping for unicorns is the wrong frame for smaller Central American and Caribbean markets. A PE-style buy-and-hold or buy-and-grow approach, targeting profitable but technology-primitive businesses, may be better suited to markets where liquidity events are rare.

## Stakeholder Background

Barnaby Dorfman spent seven years at Amazon — joining via the 1999 acquisition of Exchange.com — where he led product for the first version of Amazon Marketplace and worked in what is now called the AI group on core search and recommendations. He subsequently served as CTO of PayScale, scaling it from $8M to over $100M ARR through Warburg Pincus ownership, and then of Go One, an Australia-based employee e-learning platform that grew from single-digit millions to over $100M ARR in under two years during the pandemic. He is now a fractional CTO and AI strategy lead at Mainsale Partners, a private equity firm with approximately $4 billion under management that acquires founder-led, bootstrapped B2B SaaS companies in the $5–10M ARR range and scales them to $25–50M. He brings firsthand experience across the full PE transaction cycle and direct exposure to Latin America through extended family stays in Costa Rica and a home in San Miguel de Allende, Mexico.

## Key Findings

### Mainsale's Vertical SaaS Playbook Is Directly Exportable to Latin America
Mainsale Partners acquires bootstrapped, founder-led B2B SaaS companies in niche blue-collar industries — pool cleaning services, chiropractic practices, veterinary clinics, porta-potty logistics, machine shops — at the $5–10M ARR entry point and grows them to $25–50M before selling to larger PE buyers. The firm has approximately $4 billion under management and around 80 staff. Dorfman argues this playbook maps directly onto Latin American markets where entire industries still operate on clipboards, post-it notes, and informal radio networks, and where comparable software either does not exist or is built on obsolete web-one technology.

### AI Has Made Capital-Efficient Software Development Radically Faster — a Structural Advantage for LatAm
Dorfman estimates that AI-assisted development is approximately ten times faster than traditional approaches, citing his own experience building two functional, globally deployable applications in roughly three hours using tools like Replit. This compression of development time and cost means that a small team can now build a mobile-first, Latin America-native SaaS platform competitive with US incumbents in weeks, not years. He argues this is a genuine leapfrog moment: building with AI from day one, on mobile-first infrastructure, against entrenched manual processes is a more defensible position than trying to compete with established US players on their own turf.

### Latin America's Series A and B Gap Is the Defining Structural Bottleneck
Drawing on Jonathan Nelson's analysis, Dorfman identifies the near-total absence of growth capital as the ceiling that prevents talented LatAm founders from scaling. At the Series A stage, roughly a dozen funds can write the check; at Series B, the field narrows to General Atlantic and Kaszek, with IFC as an occasional third option. Founders who do break through typically have pre-existing US banking or network ties. Technically strong founders without those connections — Dorfman cites an Argentine AI founder whose enterprise sales cycles run three to six months versus eighteen months in the US — hit a structural ceiling regardless of product quality.

### Enterprise Sales Cycles in Latin America Are Dramatically Shorter Than in the US
Dorfman was surprised to learn from a portfolio AI company in Mexico — which built a computer-vision solution that identifies cash theft by bank tellers, winning a paid pilot with a major Mexican bank within six months, and a pharmacy upsell-monitoring solution in a similar timeframe — that enterprise sales cycles in Latin America run three to six months, compared to eighteen months in the US. He attributes this partly to less bureaucratic procurement and more centralized decision-making, contrasting it with the highly decentralized, risk-averse US municipal and county purchasing processes he experienced selling to 911 centers.

### WhatsApp Is the Single Most Important Distribution Platform for LatAm SaaS
Dorfman repeatedly returns to WhatsApp as the de facto operating system for business communication across Latin America, noting that bank operations, service coordination, and informal transport networks all run through it. He argues that building AI agents — capable of sending and receiving voice messages, text, and audio recordings natively within WhatsApp — to automate service industries such as insurance claims, gas delivery, bill payment, and logistics could unlock enormous markets. He is currently building AI-voice interpreting capabilities at Boost Lingo (a marketplace he sits on the board of) and sees direct applicability of that architecture to LatAm service verticals.

### Legal Practice Management Is a Concrete, Under-Served B2B SaaS Opportunity in LatAm
Dorfman identifies small law firm software as a specific, immediately actionable vertical. Having briefly served on the board of a US company providing software for small law firms, he notes there are tens of thousands of small law firms across Latin America currently served only by aging web-based solutions. He proposes that an AI-enabled legal practice management platform — combining document analysis, workflow automation, and billing — could be built rapidly, then scaled through a buy-and-roll-up strategy acquiring existing local incumbents before growing the combined entity. He frames this as a model for systematic sector-by-sector opportunity mapping.

### A Buy-and-Hold PE Model May Be Better Suited to LatAm Than Venture, Given Thin Exit Markets
Dorfman explicitly questions whether the traditional VC model — seeding many companies and targeting IPO or acquisition exits — makes sense in smaller LatAm markets where liquidity events are rare and the acquirer universe is thin. He suggests the PE buy-and-hold model, in which profitable growing companies simply throw off cash indefinitely without needing a near-term exit, may be the more appropriate structure for markets like Costa Rica, Honduras, and Guatemala. He contrasts this with the US venture model, where 85–90% of global tech M&A is concentrated in Silicon Valley, making exit planning for LatAm-based companies structurally disadvantaged.

## Regulatory & Structural Observations

Dorfman did not engage in detailed regulatory discussion, but made several structural observations relevant to market entry. He noted that Mexico's 911 system adopted video-capable emergency dispatch technology faster and more broadly than the US, where fragmented city- and county-level procurement creates 18–24 month sales cycles and extreme risk aversion. He contrasted this with US procurement processes, suggesting that centralized government purchasing in smaller LatAm markets can actually accelerate enterprise adoption. He also observed that informal workarounds — local ride-share radio networks in San Miguel de Allende, gas delivery apps that inconsistently accept foreign credit cards — indicate latent demand for reliable digital service platforms that formal technology providers have not yet satisfied.

## Recommendations & Insights

Dorfman recommends a systematic, AI-assisted market mapping exercise as a first step: using large language models to survey the full portfolio listings of US PE firms (which are publicly disclosed on their websites), identify which vertical SaaS categories have no credible LatAm equivalent, and produce a ranked opportunity list by market size and replicability. He would fund an analyst role to execute this research and sees it as the foundation of a credible PE fund pitch deck for a LatAm-focused B2B SaaS strategy.

He recommends that any LatAm-focused fund adopt a PE rather than VC structure, targeting profitable bootstrapped companies at $5–10M ARR, modernizing them with AI, and growing them to $25–50M ARR — accepting that exits may take longer and that buy-and-hold cash-flow strategies may need to substitute for traditional M&A or IPO liquidity.

He advocates building all new platforms as mobile-first, WhatsApp-native, and AI-enabled from inception rather than adapting US web-based architectures, arguing that the infrastructure — smartphone penetration, cloud computing, ubiquitous connectivity — is now in place across the region to support this approach.

He also suggests that multilateral development banks like CAF are natural co-investors in social-good applications of this model — citing English language learning as one domain where the gap between high-level app solutions like Duolingo and ground-level classroom realities remains wide and largely unaddressed by creative technology intermediaries.
