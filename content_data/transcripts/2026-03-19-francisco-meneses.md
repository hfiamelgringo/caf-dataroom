## Executive Summary

Francisco Meneses, a veteran of Corfo and Banco Estado with direct experience designing Chile's venture finance infrastructure, presented a detailed blueprint for a CAF-anchored regional fund of funds. His core argument is that pension funds — holding hundreds of billions of dollars across Colombia, Chile, and Peru — are the only pools of capital large enough to meaningfully finance Latin American venture ecosystems, but cultural and regulatory barriers prevent them from touching private markets directly. The solution is a two-step architecture: first, create regulated junior stock exchanges (modeled on Chile's Scalex) that give pension funds a publicly-traded, auditable entry point into growth-stage companies; second, build a regional fund of funds seeded by development banks (CAF, IDB, IFC) to generate a two- to three-year track record before approaching pension funds in a second generation.

Meneses also made a strong case for pairing equity investment with a Corfo-style leverage facility — emphasizing that lending draws far less regulatory capital from a development bank balance sheet than equity — and flagged venture debt as an underutilized and underappreciated instrument in the region. He identified Nuam (the combined Chile-Peru-Colombia exchange group), Banco Estado's startup guarantee program, and a prospective venture-debt asset manager as concrete, ready partners. He closed by endorsing an external private fund manager over an internal CAF structure, citing political-pressure risk, but insisted that governance guardrails (e.g., geographic allocation caps) must be hard-coded from day one.

## Stakeholder Background

Francisco Meneses spent years at Corfo (Chile's national development agency) as a lending officer and later as an institutional architect of its venture leverage programs, and at Banco Estado as a senior capital-raising executive. He subsequently moved to the private sector, where he advises companies on debt-raising and continues to work on expanding the Scalex junior exchange to Peru and Colombia through Nuam. His dual vantage point — having designed the public instruments that unlocked Chilean venture capital and now operating as a practitioner trying to replicate them regionally — makes him one of the most technically specific voices in this project.

## Key Findings

### Pension Funds Are the Only Scalable LP Base — But Require a Public-Market On-Ramp
Latin American pension systems hold transformative sums: Colombia's funds hold approximately $400 billion, Chile's approximately $250 billion, and Peru's approximately $50 billion. However, pension funds in the region will not invest in unregulated private vehicles because of reputational and fiduciary constraints that are culturally entrenched. The United States resolved this in the 1970s through the "prudent man rule," which allowed defined allocations (roughly 1%) to venture capital — a regulatory precedent Latin America has not yet adopted. A junior public exchange provides the regulated wrapper that makes pension fund participation legally and institutionally defensible.

### Scalex Is the Proof of Concept — and Already Expanding to Peru and Colombia
Chile's Scalex junior stock exchange, developed under Meneses's involvement, has already listed companies from cities across Chile — not just Santiago — and achieved two female-founded companies among its first five listings, demonstrating inclusion effects that private markets structurally cannot replicate. Nuam, the merged exchange group covering Chile, Peru, and Colombia, has already offered approximately $1 million in seed funding to expand the Scalex model, signaling institutional commitment beyond rhetoric. Peruvian companies are already in active discussions to list on the Chilean exchange as a first step.

### A Two-Phase Fund-of-Funds Architecture — Development Banks First, Pension Funds Second
The recommended structure launches with $100–200 million anchored by CAF, IDB, and IFC as primary LPs, deploying into funds that invest in regional growth-stage startups via the junior exchange infrastructure. After two to three years of exits and track record, the fund of funds approaches pension systems for a second-generation raise — at which point arm-twisting is minimal because performance data exists. Meneses cited a Toronto-based analog where pension funds were brought in at the first close but described it as "a lot of arm twisting," and said the second-generation raise was dramatically easier.

### Pair the Fund of Funds with a Corfo-Style Leverage Facility — Copied Almost Verbatim
Meneses recommended that CAF replicate the Corfo leverage co-investment program (which lends to VC funds on favorable terms alongside their equity commitments) nearly as-is, citing twenty years of iterative refinement that has eliminated most structural flaws. The key design principle: leverage makes sense at Series A and B stages but should not apply at seed or pre-seed, which should remain pure equity. Interest rates must be kept below-market to stimulate rather than tax the ecosystem. The critical correction from the Corfo experience is that roughly half of the cases needed equity rather than leverage — a gap this regional structure would address by combining both instruments.

### Venture Debt Is Underappreciated and Already Happening Quietly at Banco Estado
Banco Estado operates a startup guarantee program called "Scale for Investment," under which the guarantee agency backs loans that Banco Estado originates for startups — a de facto venture debt instrument. A senior figure at an asset management firm (identified as "Luca") is exploring converting this guarantee into a fund structure, removing the bank from the lending chain entirely. Meneses stated he is "sure" that Banco Estado has already deployed more venture debt capital than Partners for Growth (the IDB-Silicon Valley Bank joint venture), but does so without international visibility.

### Private Fund Manager Preferred Over Internal CAF Management — With Hard Governance Guardrails
Meneses explicitly recommended that CAF hire an external private fund manager rather than run the fund of funds internally, citing the risk of political pressure distorting geographic allocation decisions. He named Fondo de Fondos and Spectra (Brazil) as examples of qualified regional managers. His non-negotiable condition: governance documents must hard-code allocation constraints — for example, a cap preventing more than 50% of capital going to a single country — to insulate investment decisions from sovereign politics.

### Dual-Listing on London AIM (or Canada/Australia) as an Exit Liquidity Bridge
Meneses confirmed that Chilean pension fund managers have explicitly requested Delaware-domiciled structures with a defined liquidity path — specifically dual listings that allow them to sell positions in two to four years or at ten-year horizons. He validated the London AIM as a credible venue, noting that most major Chilean mining companies already list abroad (e.g., Alto Patagonia Minerals in London) because sector-specific investment expertise resides in London and Canada, not in Santiago. He suggested a pre-IPO SPV model — writing a first check in exchange for a board seat, then listing within 18 months — as a mechanism to solve the first-mover problem.

### Access to Private Capital in Latin America Is Structurally Exclusionary
Meneses was emphatic that the current private venture market concentrates capital among a tiny, socially homogeneous network: "95% male, from 10 high schools and three municipalities." Junior public exchanges directly counteract this by opening deal flow to founders from any city, background, or gender — a structural inclusion effect that no private fund program can replicate at scale. He cited the Scalex experience as evidence, with two of five early listings being female-founded companies.

## Regulatory & Structural Observations

The absence of a "prudent man" regulatory framework — the U.S. provision that permitted pension funds to allocate a defined percentage to alternative assets in the 1970s — is the single most binding structural constraint in the region. Without an equivalent rule, pension funds face both legal uncertainty and reputational exposure if they invest in unregulated private vehicles. A junior stock exchange solves this by converting private-market investments into publicly-traded securities subject to oversight by securities regulators. Meneses noted that the regulatory architecture must also resolve company domicile issues: the historical use of Cayman Islands holding structures created blacklist complications (due to anti-money-laundering classifications) that blocked development bank participation; this has eased as Cayman has been removed from key blacklists, but domicile clarity must be a condition of fund eligibility. Changes to pension fund superintendent regulations in Chile are actively being worked on by the Nuam team in parallel.

## Recommendations & Insights

- CAF should approach Juan Pablo Córdoba, CEO of Nuam, immediately — Nuam has already committed approximately $1 million toward a Scalex expansion and is an institutional co-builder, not merely a conversation partner.
- The fund of funds should launch at $100–200 million, sourced from CAF, IDB, and IFC, with an explicit second-generation plan to bring in Colombian, Chilean, and Peruvian pension systems once a track record exists.
- A Corfo leverage facility should be layered on top of the equity fund-of-funds, using below-market debt to multiply deployed capital without proportional balance-sheet strain on CAF.
- Governance documents must hard-code geographic allocation caps from day one to prevent political interference.
- CAF should explore a pre-IPO SPV product — writing a first institutional check in exchange for a board seat and a commitment to dual-list on London AIM or a Canadian exchange within 18 months — as a mechanism to create exit liquidity for pension fund LPs.
