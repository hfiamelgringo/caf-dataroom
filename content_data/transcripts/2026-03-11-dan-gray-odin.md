## Executive Summary
Dan Gray, a veteran of 20+ years across startup operations, angel investing, and VC research, argues that the conventional "missing middle" diagnosis for Latin American venture ecosystems is correct but that the standard policy response — direct government funding of growth-stage deals — is wrong. His core thesis is that capital-desert regions must sequence interventions bottom-up: first cultivate local angel ecosystems, then help successful angels graduate into fund managers, and only then will a genuine middle-stage opportunity set emerge organically. He points to the British Business Bank's asymmetric return structure as the most replicable model for government co-investors.

Gray adds important structural context: Latin American pension systems collectively manage $1.2–1.4 trillion in assets but allocate only roughly 0.05% to venture capital, representing a massive untapped pool. He warns against chasing hot categories — a mistake governments have repeated for decades — and instead advocates for a strategic-capabilities lens modeled on In-Q-Tel. For CAF, these insights argue for patient, geographically concentrated, pipeline-first capital deployment rather than headline growth-stage funds.

## Stakeholder Background
Dan Gray has spent more than two decades across the startup lifecycle — as an early-stage operator, angel investor, and VC practitioner — before specializing in private-market back-office infrastructure and VC research analytics. He works regularly with emerging fund managers, institutional LPs, and the British Business Bank on ecosystem strategy, and has applied this expertise in emerging markets across Africa and Latin America through accelerator programs. His current work at Odin gives him a data-driven vantage point on 30–40 years of academic venture literature.

## Key Findings

### The "Missing Middle" Is Real, but Direct Government Intervention at the Growth Stage Fails
Gray affirms a genuine funding gap in Latin American venture markets but argues that governments consistently misdiagnose the solution. Attempting to fund growth-stage companies directly has not worked. The correct sequence is to fill the bottom first: invest in early-stage pipeline development, which then naturally generates the deal flow that attracts middle-stage capital. Decades of government VC history show this bottom-up approach outperforms top-down growth-stage targeting.

### Latin American Pension Systems Are a Vastly Underutilized VC Capital Pool
Pension systems across Latin America manage an estimated $1.2–1.4 trillion in assets under management, yet allocate only approximately 0.05% to venture capital — amounting to hundreds of millions of dollars in potential capital sitting on the sidelines. Gray identifies regulatory constraints and risk-aversion as the primary barriers, suggesting that even modest regulatory or incentive changes could unlock a disproportionately large flow of institutional capital into early-stage local ecosystems.

### The British Business Bank's Asymmetric Return Structure Is the Most Replicable Government Model
Gray recommends that government or anchor investors adopt asymmetric return structures — offering preferential economics to co-investing LPs — to crowd in private capital without distorting deal selection. This is the mechanism that has made the British Business Bank effective. The model avoids the crowding-out problem common to direct government VC funds and aligns incentives so that private managers bear real return accountability while government absorbs disproportionate downside.

### Government VC Can Become Self-Sustaining After Roughly 30 Years with the Right Design
Citing the Kyle Briggs paper on government VC models, Gray notes that a well-designed program combining portfolio diversification with disciplined capital recycling can reach self-sustainability after approximately 30 years. This long time horizon is politically inconvenient but empirically grounded. The implication for CAF is that patient, multi-decade commitment to a consistent strategy — rather than program cycling — is the necessary condition for ecosystem maturity.

### Geographic Concentration Beats Multipolar Dispersion for Network Effects
Gray warns that spreading resources across multiple cities or sub-regions simultaneously dilutes network effects and slows ecosystem formation. He recommends concentrating capital and programming geographically in the early phases to build critical mass before expanding. This runs counter to political instincts in multi-country programs but is supported by research on how successful ecosystems in emerging markets have historically formed around single dense nodes.

### Emerging Managers Have Been Disproportionately Damaged by the Post-2021 Correction
The 2020–2021 boom created excessive exuberance in hot categories, and the subsequent correction has been severe and asymmetric — hitting emerging managers hardest while making the overall market more top-heavy. In Europe and Canada, the problem is compounded by a structurally lower LP risk tolerance and shrinking appetite for early-stage investing. Gray observes that this concentration at the top is precisely where VC performs worst at identifying outlier opportunities, which are the asset class's primary source of returns.

### Tax Incentives Work for Angels but Create Perverse Incentives for Funds
Drawing on the UK's Enterprise Investment Scheme and Seed Enterprise Investment Scheme experience, Gray distinguishes sharply between angel-level and fund-level tax incentives. Incentives work well for angels — lowering the effective cost of early-stage risk-taking and expanding the pool of individual investors. Applied to funds, however, the same incentives tend to distort behavior, rewarding compliance with incentive criteria over genuine return-seeking. He recommends structuring any incentive regime to target individuals rather than institutional vehicles.

## Regulatory & Structural Observations
Gray's analysis of pension fund allocation points to regulatory constraints as the binding barrier rather than investor appetite — the 0.05% VC allocation figure across $1.2–1.4 trillion in Latin American pension AUM implies that even small regulatory adjustments could unlock a significant capital wave. He also cautions that governments historically pursue "hot categories" in their VC mandates — a pattern he describes as a consistent mistake across decades — and that incentive design must actively counteract this tendency rather than accommodate it.

## Recommendations & Insights
- **Sequence interventions bottom-up**: Build angel ecosystems first, support graduation into fund management, and allow middle-stage opportunities to emerge naturally rather than funding growth stage directly.
- **Adopt asymmetric return structures**: Model co-investment programs on the British Business Bank to attract private LPs without crowding out market discipline.
- **Concentrate geographically**: Resist political pressure to spread resources across multiple nodes simultaneously; dense geographic concentration is the precondition for network effects.
- **Use a strategic-capabilities lens**: Rather than chasing hot sectors, identify capabilities with durable long-term value — the In-Q-Tel / CIA model is the reference case.
- **Apply spray-and-pray at the government level**: For government VC seeking solid risk-adjusted performance plus positive externalities, broad early-stage diversification outperforms concentrated sector bets.
- **Target tax incentives at angels, not funds**: Avoid the EIS/SEIS distortion problem by structuring incentives at the individual investor level.
- **Plan for a 30-year horizon**: The Kyle Briggs research suggests self-sustainability requires multi-decade consistency — program design should anticipate and survive political cycles.
