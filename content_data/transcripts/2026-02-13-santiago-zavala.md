## Notes
- Santiago Zavala, Angel Investor at Osyva (500 LatAm B16)
- Family of entrepreneurs in Mexico, learned coding early
- Started Super Happy Dev House community - hackathons across 16 Mexican cities
- Joined 500 Startups in 2012 for first thematic fund (Latin America)
- 500 Startups now: $2.2B AUM, 3,300 companies invested (including Canva, Twilio, SendGrid)
- 500 LatAm Portfolio Performance
- 4 funds completed: 2013, 2016, 2019, 2024
- 350 investments, ~$40M deployed across all vehicles
- Geographic focus:
- 40% Mexico (largest economy, companies from other countries expand there)
- Argentina, Colombia, Chile as next tier
- Peru was growing but stopped due to government complications
- Hit rate: ~4-5% success rate across funds
- Largest portfolio in Mexico, possibly all Spanish-speaking LatAm
- Need 100x returns for fund success (after dilution, multiple rounds)
- Requires companies reaching $150M revenue (at 4x software multiples)
- Market size reality:
- Mexico market can support these numbers
- Smaller countries need 4x bigger market share or regional expansion
- Central America companies must think “multilatina” from day one
- Central America Investment Challenges
- More successful raising capital in Central America than deploying
- ~10% of last fund from Guatemalan LPs
- Time machine investing doesn’t work in small markets
- Local Uber/Rappi clones get killed when originals expand
- Market too small for venture returns
- Only one company based in Guatemala in portfolio
- Two in Dominican Republic (one doing very well)
- Attempted major Honduras engagement that failed (willing to discuss off-record)
- Successful Central America Example: Art Alperstate
- Dominican Republic-based real estate software company
- $$76K MRR, 12 people, profitable
- 3,375 agents/companies paying for service
- Most customers actually in Mexico and Colombia
- Growing faster than dominant Mexican competitor
- Founders stayed in DR for cost advantages, talent access
- Biggest Pain Points in LatAm
- Fundraising is #1, 2, 3, and 4 biggest problem
- Lack of belief from LatAm business community in local startups
- Family offices prefer diversifying to Bay Area over local investment
- “Don’t want to invest because already have businesses in LatAm”
- Liquidity challenges:
- No strong public markets (only Nubank, MercadoLibre as examples)
- Will be too late when they want to buy at higher valuations
- Invest primarily in Delaware C-corps for downstream capital access
- Mexican entities face slower, more expensive processes
- Global investors rarely invest in local entities (Guatemalan, Honduran corps)
- US entities provide:
- Anti-money laundering perception
- Easier legal processes ($600 Stripe Atlas vs weeks/months locally)
- Access to top global investors (SoftBank invested in Confío)
- IFC investment in Fund 2 crucial for credibility when Santiago took leadership
- IDB helped with reputation, though limited LP introductions
- Some major Mexican families invest but consider it philanthropy/impact, not returns
- Examples of corporate engagement:
- MobilityADO created CVC, Santiago on investment committee
- Proeza (Metallica owners) deployed $50M in tech via CVC
- Coppel family office invests in 500 global funds but mandate prevents LatAm investment
- Currently running “Release Before Ready” - 12-hour hackathons
- 13 events last year, planning 20 this year across multiple cities
- Will expand to Central America
- AI impact on education: people less willing to invest in learning, believing AI makes skills obsolete
- Need “thousand Juan Minis” - referring to Guatemalan leader who:
- Worked at Apple on first laptop cooling system
- Took prop-tech company public during dot-com
- Returned to Guatemala, created Tec Guatemala with free entrepreneur space
- Publicly advocates for local ecosystem development
- Corfo funded by lithium extraction from Salar de Atacama
- Best funding entity in LatAm for innovation
- Problems with execution:
- Too focused on “pro-Chile” optics vs results
- Money attracts people trying to extract rather than create value
- Loan structure instead of LP investment creates complications
- Political nature requires hiring Corfo specialists
- Overall positive for Chile but could be structured better
