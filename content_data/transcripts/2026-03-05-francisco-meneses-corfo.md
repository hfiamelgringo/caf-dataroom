## Executive Summary

Francisco Meneses, former Chief Investment Officer at CORFO, offers a forensic diagnosis of Chile's venture capital architecture and a concrete blueprint for how CAF could replicate its most powerful mechanisms across Central America and the Caribbean. His core argument is that Chile's financial sector — though superficially sophisticated, with domestic credit and stock market capitalization each near 100% of GDP — contains a critical structural gap between seed funding and Series C, specifically the $5–50 million ticket range. Everything that fills that gap has been government-funded through CORFO, and the private sector only participates at Series C and above.

His prescription for CAF is a three-fund-of-funds structure (Central America, Caribbean, and South America), seeded with roughly $50 million in equity and supplemented by co-lending. He argues that at proper scale, a single public dollar can be multiplied nine times through institutional co-investment from Canadian, Japanese, Colombian, Chilean, and Mexican pension funds. He points to the IFC's 1994 creation of the Pioneros small-cap fund in Chile — which now represents 10% of the Santiago Stock Exchange — as proof of concept that targeted multilateral catalysis can create entire asset classes where none existed.

The session is notable for its candor about institutional limits: Scalex, the junior stock exchange Meneses helped create, has listed only five companies in three and a half years. Despite setbacks, his analytical framework and specific structural recommendations are directly applicable to the four CAF focus countries.

## Stakeholder Background

Francisco Meneses holds an MPA from Harvard and a PhD in public policy from Duke. He served as Chief Investment Officer at CORFO and Director of Corporate Finance at Banco Estado, Chile's state-owned bank. He has also worked at the Central Bank of Chile and the World Bank as a consultant. He is currently writing a book on Chile's development finance model and is actively advising policymakers in Colombia on replicating Scalex. His perspective is uniquely valuable because he operated at the intersection of policy design and capital markets execution during the period when Chile's venture ecosystem was being constructed from scratch with public money.

## Key Findings

### Chile's Series A/B gap is structural, not accidental — and entirely government-filled
Chile has no private venture funds investing in the $5–50 million ticket range. CORFO is the sole provider of capital for Series A and B rounds; the private sector enters only at Series C and above. This gap exists because Chile's financial architecture was designed by the state over four decades to channel mandatory pension fund savings into large privatized utilities and listed companies, leaving mid-market growth companies without institutional investors.

### CORFO's $1 billion in venture deployment is half of the IDB's entire Latin American portfolio
CORFO has deployed approximately $1 billion into venture capital in Chile alone. The Inter-American Development Bank, by comparison, has invested $2 billion across all of Latin America. This scale asymmetry — a single national agency equaling half the regional multilateral's footprint — illustrates both CORFO's unusual capacity and the chronic underfunding of venture ecosystems elsewhere in the region.

### Startup Chile pays for itself in value-added taxes alone from foreign companies
Startup Chile disburses between $2 million and $4 million per year — a fraction of CORFO's $2 billion annual deployment — yet Meneses describes it as the "crown jewel" of the ecosystem. Limiting the analysis to foreign companies that entered Chile through the program, the VAT receipts alone cover the program's cost. Income taxes and payroll taxes are additive. The program has produced multiple unicorns and fundamentally shifted entrepreneurial culture, delivering fiscal ROI before counting any multiplier effects.

### The 1994 IFC Pioneros fund created an asset class that now represents 10% of Chile's stock exchange
When the IFC and Chilean government created the Pioneros small-cap fund in 1994 and changed pension fund investment regulations, they seeded a segment that had no private investors. That segment — companies with $50–100 million in annual revenue, too small for direct pension fund investment but too large for CORFO seed programs — now accounts for roughly 10% of the Santiago Stock Exchange's total capitalization. Meneses frames this as the definitive proof that targeted multilateral capital can create permanent, self-sustaining asset classes.

### Scalex, a junior stock exchange, was built to bridge the Series A/B gap through public markets
Because Chilean institutional investors (pension funds and insurers) can only access companies through public markets, Meneses and CORFO created Scalex, a junior stock exchange modeled on the Nordic First North market, to allow growth-stage companies to raise $5–50 million from institutional capital without waiting for a full exchange listing. After three and a half years of operation, five companies have listed. The initiative is a proof of concept but has not yet scaled, partly because a planned companion fund-of-funds did not move forward at the cabinet level.

### A $50 million CAF fund-of-funds could mobilize a nine-times multiplier through pension fund co-investment
Meneses proposes that CAF deploy approximately $50 million in equity across three regional funds of funds — covering Central America, the Caribbean, and South America — and supplement that with co-lending. By bringing in Canadian, Japanese, and Latin American pension funds (Colombia, Chile, Costa Rica, Mexico) as limited partners, he estimates each CAF dollar could be multiplied up to nine times at full deployment efficiency. He contrasts this with current CAF impact, which he characterizes as barely a three-times multiplier, calling the gap a failure of scale and structure rather than strategy.

### The target investee is the "gazelle" — regional exporters, not unicorn-track tech companies
Meneses explicitly differentiates the fund-of-funds target from typical venture portfolios. He is targeting companies in wood, small industry, and exportable services — firms that compete regionally, not globally, and that can triple in size over ten years, generating a four-times return at low entry prices. These companies are currently neglected by both institutional investors and venture funds chasing unicorns, yet they are the segment with the highest employment-per-dollar-invested ratio: a $100 million injection into companies of this size, he estimates, generates roughly 1,000 new direct jobs.

## Regulatory & Structural Observations

Chile's apparent free-market financial sector is, in Meneses' analysis, a state-constructed protected economy. Pension fund contributions were mandatory, and for decades those funds were legally prohibited from investing outside Chile, effectively captive-financing the privatization of state utilities and creating the deep capital market Chile is known for. The 450 largest Chilean companies account for roughly 80% of GDP and benefited from this captive investor base. Only after this foundation was built did CORFO's venture programs attempt to extend capital market logic downward to smaller firms. He warns that this history is often misread as laissez-faire success when it was, in fact, directed industrial policy executed through financial regulation.

## Recommendations & Insights

Meneses recommends CAF pursue the following sequence: (1) Establish three regional funds of funds — Central America, Caribbean, and South America — seeded with roughly $15 million each in equity, with committed co-lending capacity. (2) Approach IDB's new leadership (he names the incoming IDB Invest CEO by name) immediately to co-structure the initiative, turning a competitive relationship into additive capital. (3) Target pension funds in Canada, Japan, Colombia, Chile, Costa Rica, and Mexico as limited partners, using CAF's balance sheet credibility to anchor institutional participation. (4) Focus investable capital on gazelle-stage companies — regional exporters and service firms with $10–100 million revenue — rather than early-stage tech. (5) Consider publicly traded fund structures modeled on Scalex as the exit vehicle, since institutional investors in the region predominantly operate through public markets. He also recommends CAF explore a "Scale-Up" program modeled on Startup Chile but targeting companies with 100+ employees rather than three-person startups, arguing the fiscal return per dollar is likely higher at that stage.
