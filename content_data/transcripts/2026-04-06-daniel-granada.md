## Context
Daniel Granada of Pomona Impact — a Central America-focused debt-first impact fund ($33M Fund II, ~$2.1M avg ticket) — spoke with the PFA/CAF team on April 6, 2026 about the nascent but growing VC ecosystem in Central America, including regulatory fragmentation, the near-failure of local LP fundraising, and concrete recommendations for how DFIs like CAF can catalyze the market through matching capital structures and parallel ecosystem-building. This interview is a high-signal practitioner perspective on why regional investing is structurally hard and what policy-level interventions could unlock it.

## Notes
- Central America VC Ecosystem — Key Dynamics
- Deal flow in Central America is growing but still nascent — a small handful of actors doing interesting things, slowly attracting larger outside investors
- Karikako Summit (organized by the Kidos family / El Trueno family office) flagged as the best investment event in the region
- FLEA has shifted toward NGOs/impact-first, making it less relevant for investors
- Karikako attracts global LPs interested in LatAm and brings them into Central America
- Regulatory fragmentation is the core structural barrier to regional investing
- Each country has different capitalization requirements, licensing regimes, and financial laws
- Example: a fintech organized under El Salvador’s fintech laws faces a near-zero regulatory framework in Guatemala — effectively building two companies
- Cross-border deals often require country-by-country loan tranching or local institutional partners
- Nicaragua (political risk) and Honduras flagged as particularly difficult environments
- Focus: scale-ups (not startups), via debt; avg ticket ~$2.1–2.2M; range $500K–$6M
- Fund II: $33M total raised; 80% debt / 20% equity
- Equity investments concentrated outside Central America — exits in the region are too difficult
- Fund III will be 100% debt
- Sectors: agribusiness, basic services, digital economy — intentionally broad to generate pipeline
- Impact philosophy: business viability is the first filter; if no measurable/scalable impact can be identified, they pass — but impact is not the lead criterion
- Pomona Impact Foundation runs grant-funded acceleration programs
- Focus areas: agro-processing / food systems, and green tech (Costa Rica)
- Has helped portfolio companies raise millions through investment readiness support
- Biggest pipeline challenge: established family businesses ($2–4M revenue range) don’t attend startup events and don’t know funds exist — harder to find than early-stage companies
- Local LP fundraising largely failed for Fund II
- ~$400K raised from Central American families out of $33M total — after 2 years of effort across Panama, Guatemala, El Salvador
- Wealthy regional families are LPs in BlackRock etc. but resist investing locally
- Their own businesses yield ~30% returns (quasi-monopolies: cement, beer, telecoms)
- A 12% net fund return doesn’t compute against that baseline
- Smaller families fear losing control — don’t understand the LP structure
- DFIs (development finance institutions) are far more efficient counterparties — clear and fast on fit/no-fit
- Funded VC funds in Central America: fewer than 5 serious ones (Alterna, IDC flagged; Karikako is a family office, not a fund; Balam/Honduras status unclear)
- A one-size-fits-all regional fund won’t work — country-specific mechanisms needed, though a regional vehicle can still exist alongside
- Matching capital mechanisms (e.g., Peru’s 2:1 model) flagged as high-value for first-time fund managers
- Reduces LP risk on first close; acts as a “hunting license” for new managers
- If CAF/CABEI put in $10M alongside $5M from a manager + $5M from others → immediately de-risks the raise
- Ecosystem building (accelerators, incubators, investment readiness) is worthwhile but must run in parallel with fund development — pipeline and capital need to grow together
