## Executive Summary
Lizanka Guevara of AMCHAM Honduras provided a detailed overview of the legal and regulatory landscape governing foreign investment and venture capital in Honduras. The picture she presents is more permissive than the country's reputation might suggest: there are no restrictions on 100% foreign ownership, the 1950 Commercial Code already accommodates preferred and convertible shares, and cross-border lending faces minimal registration requirements unless conducted habitually. Business formation costs run $3,000–$5,000 and take 1.5–2.5 months, with municipal permitting being the primary bottleneck.

For CAF and potential fund managers, the key takeaway is that Honduras's legal infrastructure is adequate for standard venture and private equity deal structures. The framework supports shareholder agreements, atypical contracts, movable-asset guarantees, and trusts as collateral instruments. The 2022 Economic Recovery and Reactivation Law adds time-limited fiscal incentives for small employers, though its current validity requires confirmation. The National Investment Commission offers a direct government channel to unblock permitting delays.

The country's investment protections are anchored in CAFTA-DR, a 1994 bilateral treaty with the United States, the 2011 Investment Promotion and Protection Law, and 12 active free trade agreements — a surprisingly robust multilateral safety net for a frontier market.

## Key Findings

### Business formation is achievable but municipal permitting is the critical bottleneck
Establishing a legal entity in Honduras costs between $3,000 and $5,000 and takes 1.5 to 2.5 months. The process involves drafting an incorporation deed, obtaining a fiscal number, securing an operating permit, and opening bank accounts. The operating permit — delayed by municipal requirements — is consistently the longest step and the primary drag on the overall timeline.

### Honduras allows 100% foreign ownership with no capital repatriation restrictions
There are no statutory restrictions on foreign investors owning 100% of a Honduran entity. Investors can choose between a Honduran subsidiary, which carries its own balance sheet and limits parent-company liability, or a foreign branch, which is treated as an extension of the parent entity's patrimony. This structural choice has direct implications for how liability and capital are managed across borders.

### A layered set of international treaties provides meaningful investor protections
Honduras is covered by CAFTA-DR (effective 2006–2007), a bilateral investment treaty with the United States signed in 1994, and 12 active free trade agreements. The 2011 Investment Promotion and Protection Law provides a domestic statutory layer. Together, these create arbitration rights and non-discrimination guarantees that give foreign investors recourse beyond Honduran courts.

### The 1950 Commercial Code already supports venture-capital-compatible equity structures
Honduras's Commercial Code, dating to 1950, permits the issuance of preferred and convertible shares. The legal principle of freedom of contract validates atypical agreements, meaning shareholder agreements with liquidation preferences, anti-dilution clauses, and drag-along rights are enforceable. This removes the need for legislative reform before standard VC term-sheet structures can be deployed.

### Cross-border lending is lightly regulated — no Central Bank registration required for non-habitual loans
Foreign lenders are not required to register loans with the Central Bank unless lending is conducted systematically or habitually. Withholding taxes apply: 10% on interest payments to foreign entities and 25% on commissions. Promissory notes are available as an executive enforcement instrument in local courts, enabling creditors to pursue collection actions without full litigation.

### The 2022 Economic Recovery Law offers fiscal incentives for small employers — but its status is uncertain
The Economic Recovery and Reactivation Law, passed in 2022, provides fiscal benefits for two to three years to companies employing up to 10–20 workers and promises expedited processing with government agencies. AMCHAM flagged that the current validity of this law must be confirmed with COEP, suggesting possible amendments or implementation gaps since passage.

### The National Investment Commission functions as a government-backed permit unblocking mechanism
The National Investment Commission promotes Honduras internationally and provides on-the-ground support for operational and environmental permitting. Notably, it has authority under a presidential order to address permit backlogs — making it a practical escalation channel for investors stuck in administrative delays rather than a purely promotional body.

## Regulatory & Structural Observations
Honduras's regulatory framework for investment is more complete on paper than its frontier-market label implies. The combination of the 2011 Investment Protection Law, CAFTA-DR arbitration rights, and a Commercial Code that already accommodates complex equity structures means the legal foundation for venture and private equity activity exists without requiring new legislation. The practical gaps are in implementation: municipal permitting delays, uncertainty about whether the 2022 Recovery Law remains operative, and the need to use trust structures or movable-asset guarantees to give foreign lenders meaningful collateral security. The Movable Guarantees Law provides a statutory basis for liens on personal property, and fiduciary trust arrangements — typically administered through local banks — offer an additional collateral mechanism. The 25% withholding on commissions is notably higher than the 10% on interest and could affect structuring decisions for management fees or advisory arrangements paid to foreign entities.

## Recommendations & Insights
AMCHAM committed to sending a one-page legal framework summary and links to the relevant laws, and recommended confirming the status of the 2022 Recovery Law directly with COEP (specifically Gabriel Molina, Manager of Sustainable Businesses at COEP). For any investor or fund manager entering Honduras, engaging the National Investment Commission early — particularly for environmental and operational permits — is advisable given its presidential mandate to clear backlogs.
