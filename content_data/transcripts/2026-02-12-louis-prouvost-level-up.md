## Executive Summary

Louis Prouvost and his co-founder Jose presented Level Up's "Act Big" strategy to train 20,000 technology professionals across Central America by 2030, positioning the region as a nearshoring destination for North American and European companies. The program has already trained over 4,000 students through a blended funding model combining employer sponsorships (GBM, Amazon), European Union grants, and a newly structured student loan product co-designed with a local Guatemalan bank. The model's core mechanic is fee-for-service: companies pay Level Up to build their specific talent pipeline, and graduates are placed directly into sponsoring employers.

The central problem Level Up faces is a demand-side gap. Training investment is accelerating — with roughly $1.5 million already secured and a $48.5 million target to reach by 2030 — but the pace of hiring commitments from employers is not keeping up. To bridge this, Level Up has launched its own IT outsourcing company to provide graduates with their critical first year of work experience, since even sponsor companies frequently reject candidates for lacking prior professional experience. The co-founders are actively seeking a demand broker — an entity or network that can connect trained graduates to companies with specific IT, AI, data analytics, or cybersecurity needs.

For CAF, Level Up represents a mature, market-tested workforce-development model with a replicable financing structure and genuine employer traction. The missing link — a structured demand-side intermediary — is a potential entry point for CAF or its portfolio partners to add value and accelerate the region's nearshoring positioning.

## Stakeholder Background

Louis Prouvost is a French entrepreneur based in Guatemala who co-founded Level Up, a technology training and workforce-placement organization. He previously developed the AI curriculum for GBM — the largest technology company headquartered in Central America — which established Level Up's regional credibility. Jose is a co-founder who brings expertise in banking-sector software development and is responsible for the commercial and employer-partnership strategy. Both have been building Level Up's multi-stakeholder funding model for several years, with backing now spanning corporate sponsors, European institutions, and a local Guatemalan bank.

## Key Findings

### Level Up has trained 4,000+ students and is targeting 20,000 by 2030 under its "Act Big" nearshoring strategy
Level Up has already trained over 4,000 students, primarily in AI, and is scaling toward 20,000 graduates by 2030. The program secured the regional training contract for GBM — Central America's largest tech company — and has since added Microsoft and Amazon as corporate partners. Google is in negotiation for 2026. Governments of Guatemala and El Salvador have formally joined the initiative, lending it cross-border institutional legitimacy.

### A blended funding stack of $1.5M secured against a $48.5M target creates a significant financing gap
Level Up has assembled roughly $1.5 million in confirmed funding: approximately $250,000 from GBM, $700,000 from the European Union, and an additional ~$500,000 expected from Amazon's local reseller network. The program's 2030 goal requires $48.5 million total. The gap is substantial, and the co-founders are actively seeking additional corporate sponsors, donor institutions, and financing mechanisms to close it.

### A novel employer-guaranteed student loan product provides a replicable financing model
Level Up co-designed a student loan instrument with a local Guatemalan bank that issues low-interest loans to students whose enrollment is backed by a corporate employment guarantee. Students receive a three-month payment deferral after graduation before repayment begins. This model is described as new to Guatemala and directly ties training investment to employer demand — a structure that could be replicated or expanded by a development-finance institution like CAF.

### The demand-side gap is the program's existential bottleneck
Despite strong supply-side momentum, Level Up cannot place graduates fast enough to justify continued donor and corporate investment. Even committed employer-partners like GBM decline to hire graduates who lack prior professional experience. This creates a circular problem: training investment grows, but placement rates stall, threatening the credibility of the entire pipeline. The co-founders describe needing a "broker" entity — a company or network — that can consistently surface employer demand and match it to trained graduates.

### Level Up launched its own IT outsourcing company to manufacture the missing first-year work experience
To solve the experience catch-22, Level Up created an in-house IT outsourcing company that acts as a first employer for recent graduates. The company offers managed IT services, AI support, data analytics, and cybersecurity remotely from Central America. Level Up is actively seeking its first external clients for this entity, targeting companies with 50–100 person IT departments that are under cost pressure and would benefit from nearshore staffing at lower rates.

### Banking-sector software development is the most mature and defensible specialization in the region
Level Up's co-founder Jose has deep roots in banking-sector programming, and the broader Central American tech ecosystem has a decade-long track record in this vertical. Every major bank in the region has been migrating from physical branches to mobile and web applications since roughly 2012, generating a substantial pool of developers with banking-system expertise. The co-founders identified this as a sector where Level Up could credibly position itself as a go-to nearshore supplier rather than competing as a generalist.

### Training is customized by company toolstack, not just by skill domain
Level Up's curriculum is built around a ten-month foundational program covering AI, low-level software development, software integration, and marketing automation. On top of this base, the program layers company-specific toolstack training — Microsoft or Google environments, for example — according to the hiring employer's requirements. English-language proficiency and professional mindset training are built into every cohort, with the explicit goal of making graduates indistinguishable from comparable talent available domestically to the hiring company.

## Regulatory & Structural Observations

No formal regulatory discussion took place. However, the student loan co-design with a local bank signals that Level Up is operating in a space that sits at the intersection of financial regulation (loan products, employment guarantees) and labor market policy. The co-founders noted that the low-interest-rate loan structure was only made possible by the employment guarantee from the corporate sponsor — a mechanism that could require regulatory clarification if scaled broadly across banking institutions in the region.

## Recommendations & Insights

- **Build or partner with a demand-side broker.** The most urgent need is not more training capacity but a structured intermediary that aggregates employer demand and routes specific profiles to Level Up. A network of IT body shops or staffing firms acting as resellers of Level Up graduates is one viable model.
- **Sector-specialize rather than generalize.** The co-founders acknowledged that positioning Level Up as a generalist IT training provider makes it hard to differentiate. Focusing on banking-sector software, AI infrastructure, or cybersecurity — where Central American talent already has proven depth — would sharpen the value proposition for prospective employer clients.
- **Replicate the student loan model at scale.** The employer-guaranteed, low-interest loan product is genuinely innovative for the region. A development bank could serve as the guarantee backstop, enabling multiple local banks to issue similar instruments without requiring individual corporate guarantees for every cohort.
- **Pursue CAF or multilateral co-investment to bridge the $47M gap.** Level Up's $48.5 million target by 2030 is beyond what corporate sponsors and EU donors can cover alone. A blended-finance structure with a multilateral anchor investor — providing first-loss capital or a guarantee facility — could unlock the remaining funding needed to hit the 20,000-graduate target.
