## Executive Summary

Aline Murlick of StartSe, a Brazilian education company operating in Silicon Valley for roughly six years, runs week-long immersion programs designed to shift the mindset of Brazilian executives and entrepreneurs from large, traditional companies. The program runs Monday through Friday at Circuit Launch in Oakland, covering AI, exponential technologies, robotics, hardware, and direct interaction with Silicon Valley founders and makers. The central value proposition is not technical training per se but a visceral encounter with a faster, less bureaucratic culture of decision-making and experimentation.

The program is relevant to CAF's thinking about a Silicon Valley presence because it demonstrates a proven, replicable model for delivering high-impact, short-duration executive education to Latin American audiences. StartSe shows that cohorts of 45 executives paying roughly $30,000 per person for a week-long program will return voluntarily and refer colleagues — suggesting strong demand and willingness to pay. The Circuit Launch co-working and fabrication facility proves especially valuable as an ambient learning environment where participants encounter live robotics and AI projects outside the classroom.

## Stakeholder Background

Aline Murlick is the Silicon Valley-based program host for StartSe, a Brazilian education and innovation company founded approximately six years ago. She manages logistics, curriculum curation, and participant experience for executive cohorts traveling from Brazil to the Bay Area. Her role sits at the intersection of corporate learning, ecosystem access, and community facilitation, giving her a ground-level view of what resonates with Latin American business leaders when they encounter Silicon Valley for the first time.

## Key Findings

### Week-Long Immersion Follows a Structured Five-Day Curriculum Anchored in AI and Robotics
The StartSe program runs Monday through Friday. Monday covers Silicon Valley culture and history; Tuesday focuses on AI, AI agents, and practical tool adoption; Wednesday features visits to startups and conversations with successful Brazilian-American entrepreneurs; Thursday centers on robotics and mobility; Friday synthesizes the week into an action plan participants take back to Brazil. The entire program is framed around AI as the dominant thread.

### Cohorts of Up to 45 Executives Pay Approximately $30,000 Per Person for the Week
Groups run at a maximum of 45 participants. Pricing varies by cohort type — mixed open-enrollment groups versus company-sponsored groups have different rate structures — but the benchmark figure cited for mixed groups is approximately $30,000 per participant for the full week. Demand is strong enough that executives return for repeat visits, typically two years after their first cohort, citing rapid ecosystem change as the reason.

### The Deepest Value Delivered Is Mindset Change, Not Technical Content
Executives arrive expecting access to big tech campuses; they leave citing mindset transformation as the most impactful outcome. Coming from large Brazilian companies with heavy approval processes and hierarchical decision chains, they are most affected by seeing small teams run experiments, make decisions autonomously, and iterate without bureaucratic friction. Murlick describes this as the dominant takeaway reported consistently across cohorts.

### Circuit Launch Provides an Ambient, Living Laboratory That Amplifies Classroom Learning
StartSe relocated from a dedicated Palo Alto office to Circuit Launch, a fabrication-focused co-working facility in Oakland, because the environment itself functions as a learning asset. Approximately 80 companies building AI-driven robotics operate in the same building — ranging from agricultural robots to conversational AI companions to prosthetic limbs — giving participants immediate physical exposure to frontier technology simply by walking the floor. Alex Dantas, the facility's operator, is cited as a globally recognized authority in AI robotics and a key speaker resource.

### Personalized Matching to Participant Industry Sectors Drives Perceived Value
Before and during each program, Murlick individually assesses participants' professional backgrounds — finance, agribusiness, software, and others — and tailors speaker sessions and introductions accordingly. When six or more participants share a sector, the program adds a dedicated session with a domain expert in that field. This personalization is positioned as the mechanism that converts generic inspiration into actionable, sector-specific connections.

### Repeat Attendance and Peer Referrals Signal Durable Demand
Executives return for second programs roughly two years after their first visit, motivated by the pace of change in the ecosystem — the shift from metaverse hype to AI dominance is cited as a concrete example. Companies also send cohorts of employees after a senior leader attends individually. This organic renewal and referral pattern suggests the program creates genuine institutional appetite rather than one-time curiosity tourism.

## Recommendations & Insights

Murlick's implicit model suggests several design principles relevant to a CAF Silicon Valley hub: (1) physical co-location with active builders and makers — not just office space — dramatically amplifies the learning experience; (2) a five-day immersive format with a structured arc from culture to action planning works well for senior executives who cannot commit to longer programs; (3) sector-personalized matchmaking within a cohort program is feasible and valued at group sizes of 45; (4) pricing at $30,000 per participant for a week indicates robust willingness-to-pay among Latin American corporate leaders, suggesting a hub could be partially self-funding through program fees; (5) the action-planning session on the final day is a critical mechanism for translating inspiration into organizational change.
