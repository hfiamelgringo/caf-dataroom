## Executive Summary
Allen Taylor presented Endeavor's global model and a concrete proposal for a regional Central America office. Endeavor, operating in 50 countries with 600 staff and an $800M fund, focuses on a small number of very high-growth companies rather than broad-based support, targeting a 2–5% GDP impact in small countries. Taylor argued that individual Central American countries are too small to justify standalone Endeavor chapters, making a regional hub the only viable structure.

The proposed model requires $3M in philanthropic capital for the first five years and $10M for a regional venture fund, with the expectation that carried interest eventually self-funds operations. Taylor cited Endeavor's track record — Fund Zero returned 8.6x ($10M → $86M DPI) and 71 of 400 Endeavor-backed companies became unicorns at Series A/B — as evidence of the model's financial and developmental credibility. For CAF, this conversation surfaces a concrete, co-fundable vehicle to anchor high-impact entrepreneurship support across Costa Rica, Honduras, Guatemala, and beyond, with an identified network of local business leaders already in place.

## Stakeholder Background
Allen Taylor is a senior strategy leader at Endeavor, the global high-impact entrepreneurship organization founded in 1997. He oversees regional expansion planning, including the Central America initiative under discussion. With Endeavor operating across 50 countries and managing a $300M (Fund Five) vehicle, Taylor brings both a global comparative lens and specific knowledge of how Endeavor structures new market entry, blended finance models, and venture co-investment rules. His perspective is directly relevant to CAF's interest in scalable, self-sustaining innovation support structures.

## Key Findings

### Individual Central American Countries Are Too Small for Standalone Chapters — A Regional Model Is the Only Viable Path
Endeavor has assessed that no single Central American country generates sufficient deal flow or philanthropic capacity to sustain a standalone chapter. The proposed solution is a single regional office covering multiple countries, modeled on approaches Endeavor is also exploring for Central Asia, the Baltics, and the Andean cluster (Ecuador, Peru, Colombia, Bolivia). A regional board of 1–2 business leaders per country would govern the structure, ensuring local legitimacy without duplicating fixed costs.

### The Regional Launch Requires $3M Philanthropic + $10M Venture Capital Over Five Years
The financial blueprint is explicit: $3M in philanthropic funding covers operational costs for the first five years, while $10M seeds a regional venture fund. The long-term business model depends on carried interest from the fund replacing philanthropic support over time, making the structure financially self-sustaining. This blended finance approach — grants funding operations while returns fund their own successor — is the same model Endeavor has used globally since adding its investment arm in 2012.

### Endeavor's Fund Track Record Is Unusually Strong — 8.6x on Fund Zero, 71 Unicorns from 400 Investments
Fund Zero (launched 2012, the first 15 years were mentorship-only) converted $10M into $86M in distributed proceeds, an 8.6x DPI. Across 400 equity investments in Endeavor companies, 71 became billion-dollar businesses at the point Endeavor backed them at Series A or B. Endeavor is currently raising Fund Five, a $300M vehicle. These figures give the proposed Central America fund a credible institutional benchmark when approaching LPs or development-finance co-investors.

### Endeavor Never Leads Rounds and Only Co-Invests Alongside Professional Funds — Not Family Offices
Endeavor's investment rules are deliberately non-competitive with the broader venture ecosystem: it never leads a round, always co-invests with professional institutional funds (explicitly excluding family offices), and invests automatically in every Endeavor company that meets its criteria. This structure is designed to act as a quality signal to larger investors rather than to deploy capital at scale. The $800M fund is explicitly framed as a "small check to signal quality" rather than a primary capital source.

### Key Local Champions Already Identified in Three of the Four Target Countries
Endeavor has pre-identified anchor contacts for the regional network: the organizers of the Carikako Summit in Costa Rica, the Creek family in El Salvador, and the Mata family in Guatemala. Central American founders are already participating in Endeavor's Mexico chapter, demonstrating that a regional demand signal exists even before a formal chapter launches. Honduras and the Dominican Republic are not yet listed with identified contacts, representing a gap CAF could help bridge.

### Endeavor's Impact Model Targets GDP-Level Change Through a Multiplier Effect from a Small Number of Companies
Endeavor's theory of change holds that 3–4 high-growth companies, developed over 20–25 years, can account for 2–5% of GDP in a small country. The Uruguay example is instructive: dLocal, a single Endeavor-affiliated company, became a multi-billion-dollar business from a country with fewer than 4 million people. The three impact pillars — job creation (future-of-work quality jobs), local wealth creation (capital that stays in-market), and role model creation (inspiring the next entrepreneurial generation) — are tracked systematically across the portfolio.

### Global Entrepreneurship Has Shifted — 40% of New Companies Are Global from Day One, and Peer Networks Outperform Silicon Valley Access
Two decades ago, 90% of Endeavor-backed companies targeted regional or national markets; today that figure is 60%, with 40% global from inception. Taylor emphasized that the most valuable resource for entrepreneurs is peer-to-peer learning within the Endeavor network — citing Brazilian founders inspiring Nigerian founders — rather than access to Silicon Valley. Surveys of Endeavor entrepreneurs consistently rank "thinking bigger" as the single most important shift the network enables.

## Regulatory & Structural Observations
No specific regulatory discussion was recorded in this session. Taylor noted that fewer than 5% of Endeavor's 600 global staff hold US passports (more are Brazilian than any other nationality), which reflects a deliberate organizational philosophy of local embedding over imported expertise — a structural choice with implications for how any Central America office would be staffed and governed.

## Recommendations & Insights
Taylor's implicit recommendation to CAF is to serve as the anchor philanthropic and/or investment partner for the $3M + $10M Central America launch package. He framed Endeavor as a proven vehicle that de-risks the capital deployment problem: the co-investment rules, the automatic investment structure, and the fund track record collectively reduce selection risk. He also suggested that similar regional models are worth considering for other CAF geographies where individual countries are sub-scale (Central Asia, Baltics, Andean cluster), implying a replicable template rather than a one-off experiment. The identification of local board candidates in Costa Rica, El Salvador, and Guatemala suggests that a first convening could happen quickly if funding were committed.
