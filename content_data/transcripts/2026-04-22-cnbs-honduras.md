## Executive Summary

Honduras's regulatory framework for pension funds currently offers no explicit pathway for venture capital investment. Betsabe Franco, Superintendent of Pensions and Securities at CNBS, confirmed that the investment regulations governing both public pension institutes and private pension fund administrators (AFPs) require all holdings to meet standards of safety, liquidity, and prudence — effectively excluding early-stage or high-risk assets. However, she identified an immediate legal workaround: existing regulations already permit pension funds to invest in registered investment funds, meaning a properly structured venture capital fund of funds could theoretically access pension capital without requiring a regulatory overhaul.

Franco also noted that CNBS had previously studied Argentina's model — where pension institutions are required to allocate roughly 2% to SME-linked instruments — and while the idea was not rejected outright, it was never formally developed. Any reform to enable alternative asset allocation would only require amending CNBS's own investment regulations, not the organic laws of individual pension institutes, making the regulatory path shorter than commonly assumed. A Fintech desk operating jointly between CNBS and the Central Bank of Honduras is active but currently focused on digital wallets rather than capital-formation instruments.

## Stakeholder Background

Betsabe Franco is the Superintendent of Pensions and Securities at Honduras's National Commission for Banking and Insurance (CNBS), the apex financial regulatory body. She oversees supervision of both public and private pension previsional systems as well as the securities market, including the stock exchange, brokerage houses, the central securities depository, risk-rating agencies, and price providers. She was accompanied by Benjamín Santos, who has over twenty years of experience in securities supervision at the same institution. Franco has prior experience at the Ministry of Finance and previously collaborated with the CAF project team on Honduras's 2017 sovereign bond issuance. Her institution is also involved in drafting a new Securities Market Law currently in advanced socialization with the private sector.

## Key Findings

### No Explicit Venture Capital Regulation Exists Under CNBS Jurisdiction
Honduras has no regulation within the Superintendency of Pensions and Securities that promotes, enables, or incentivizes venture capital investment. Franco stated plainly that within her supervisory scope — covering both the previsional system and the securities market — "there is nothing that fosters, enables, or promotes" this type of investment activity. A separate Fintech desk exists jointly between CNBS and the Central Bank, but its current focus is limited to digital wallet solutions rather than alternative financing structures.

### Pension Investment Rules Mandate Safety, Liquidity, and Prudence — Excluding Venture Capital
Both public pension institutes and private AFPs are governed by separate CNBS investment regulations that establish limits by instrument type, issuer, and sector. Article 1 of the applicable regulation requires all investments to adhere to principles of prudence, safety, yield, liquidity, and risk diversification. Currently, no instrument category within these regulations accommodates high-risk or early-stage company investment. Direct venture capital positions are not explicitly prohibited but are "subject to challenge" regarding due diligence quality, creating a de facto barrier.

### Investment Funds Are the Fastest Legal Route — Already Permitted
Franco identified investment funds as the most immediate legal vehicle through which pension capital could reach venture-backed companies. The existing investment regulations for both AFPs and public institutes already permit allocations to registered investment funds. A venture capital fund structured as a regulated investment fund — with a professional external manager — could access pension assets without any new legislation, only a potential regulatory clarification or limit adjustment by CNBS. Franco said: "That would be the fastest route — investment funds. The legal framework already permits investing in investment funds."

### CNBS Studied Argentina's Mandatory SME Allocation Model but Never Formalized It
CNBS previously conducted study visits to Argentina, where pension institutions are required to invest approximately 2% of assets in SME-linked instruments. Franco confirmed this model was analyzed — including direct visits to and from Argentine counterparts — and was not rejected, but was shelved without further development. She cited Honduras's draft Securities Market Law (currently in advanced stakeholder consultation) as including SME-oriented instruments such as negotiable invoices and negotiable certificates of deposit, signaling continued interest in this direction.

### Regulatory Reform Requires Only a CNBS Reglamento Amendment, Not Congressional Action
A critical structural clarification: enabling pension funds to invest in alternative assets would not require amending the organic laws of individual pension institutes. Franco confirmed that those laws govern governance structures, not investment management. The only instrument that would need modification is the CNBS-issued investment regulation — a regulatory act within the Commission's own authority. This significantly lowers the legislative barrier compared to what the project team initially assumed.

### Risk Aversion and Limited Institutional Expertise Are the Real Barriers
Beyond the regulatory text, Franco emphasized that Honduran pension fund managers remain deeply conservative because any poor investment decision invites regulatory scrutiny and sanctions. She noted: "Why do you think we stay in certificates of deposit? Why has it been so hard to move away from government bonds? Because it is the easiest thing to understand." She attributed this to limited analytical capacity within pension institutions and strong aversion to reputational and regulatory risk — a cultural and human-capital constraint that precedes any legal reform.

### Graduated Allocation Model Is Conceptually Viable — Starting Very Small
Franco expressed openness to a phased approach: beginning with a very small percentage allocation, with CNBS empowered to increase limits incrementally as market familiarity grows. She noted that the Commission's regulatory instrument — the investment regulation — can be amended without legislative action, allowing iterative calibration. She also flagged corporate governance weakness in Honduran SMEs as a structural risk factor that any investment model must address, citing evidence that many small businesses fail within three years.

### Mutual Guarantee Society Infrastructure Identified as a Critical Missing Link
Franco repeatedly cited the absence of a robust Mutual Guarantee Society in Honduras as a key obstacle to pension fund participation in SME or venture investment. In Argentina's model, multiple such societies serve as guarantors for SME-linked investments, providing the security layer that conservative pension regulators require. She suggested that CAF or government funding directed toward establishing or strengthening such a society could unlock pension capital more effectively than regulatory reform alone.

## Regulatory & Structural Observations

The CNBS Superintendency of Pensions and Securities issues and enforces two distinct investment regulations: one for public previsional institutes and one for private AFPs. Both set limits by instrument type, issuer, and sector, and both require compliance regardless of whether the fund is public or private — AFPs must adhere to the CNBS regulation even though their boards make investment decisions. Permitted instruments already include common and preferred shares of corporations, structured notes, real estate investments, and equity stakes in certain public infrastructure projects (such as the Palmerola airport and the Civic Center), as well as cross-border investments up to defined limits. A new Securities Market Law draft is currently in advanced socialization and includes provisions for negotiable invoices and negotiable certificates of deposit aimed at SME market access — but does not yet address venture capital. The Fintech regulatory desk is a joint CNBS–Central Bank of Honduras working group, currently focused on wallet regulation, with limited capacity to address capital formation instruments.

## Recommendations & Insights

Franco's implied recommendation is that the most viable near-term path for CAF to enable pension fund participation in venture capital is through a regulated investment fund structure — avoiding the need for new legislation entirely. She suggested that CAF or a government-backed entity could also explore establishing or capitalizing a Mutual Guarantee Society, which would provide the backstop needed to satisfy the prudence requirements embedded in CNBS regulations. For any broader reform, she recommended a phased approach: beginning with a minimal allocation percentage set by CNBS regulatory amendment, allowing the Commission to increase limits as institutional expertise and market track records develop. She also implicitly recommended that CAF facilitate a connection with the CNBS Fintech desk (a joint CNBS–Central Bank team) for a more granular conversation about non-traditional funding instruments.
