## Executive Summary

Iyinoluwa Aboyeji, co-founder of Andela and Flutterwave and now manager of the roughly $100 million Future Africa fund, offers a sharp practitioner critique of development finance institutions as limited partners. His core argument is that DFIs are process-driven rather than outcomes-driven, deploy capital at minimum ticket sizes ($10 million, requiring 20% fund ownership) that force fund managers into scale they neither need nor can responsibly deploy, and impose job-creation rubrics that have no empirical track record. He built his entire fund without a single DFI LP — his one institutional investor is the Africa Finance Corporation, which he values precisely because it behaves like a commercial investor.

His affirmative framework for job creation distills to a single formula: skill multiplied by productive assets multiplied by infrastructure. He argues no hiring happens without all three conditions being met, and that DFIs and governments consistently try to shortcut the formula. His new operating company, Buco, translates the formula directly into three concurrent ventures: an AI workforce program (25,000 applicants screened to 750 participants), an electric-vehicle fleet leasing program targeting 10,000 cars, and a bid to acquire a West African subsea cable platform to expand connectivity infrastructure.

For CAF, the key takeaways are: smaller, flexible LP checks with no minimum fund-size threshold; outcomes-linked debt instruments tied to verifiable job placements rather than equity stakes; and the structural insight that job creation requires investing in platforms and systems, not individual companies.

## Stakeholder Background

Iyinoluwa ("E") Aboyeji studied at the University of Waterloo in Canada before returning to Lagos to co-found Andela — now an $800 million talent platform that trained 15% of Africa's engineers — and Flutterwave, a continent-wide payments platform that raised $20 million in early funding under his tenure as founding CEO. After a period in the United States studying venture capital, he moved back to Nigeria before COVID and launched Future Africa, a fund focused on turning Africa's biggest structural challenges into global businesses. His first investment was Moove, a vehicle-leasing company now valued at $1.85 billion and partnering with Waymo on autonomous vehicles in the United States. He is currently 34 years old and has recently launched Buco, an operating company that directly builds job-creating ventures across AI training, vehicle fleets, and connectivity infrastructure.

## Key Findings

### DFIs Are Process-Driven, Not Outcomes-Driven — and It Shows
Aboyeji's central critique is that DFIs design funds around compliance rubrics rather than testable job-creation logic. They mandate 40-page requirements documents with no empirical track record, demand minimum checks of $10 million at 20% fund ownership (implying a $50–100 million minimum fund size), and prioritize documentation over experimentation. The result is that fund managers with a genuine $10 million problem are forced to raise $100 million they cannot responsibly deploy.

### The Jobs Formula — Skill × Productive Assets × Infrastructure
Aboyeji's investment thesis reduces job creation to three non-negotiable inputs: training (skill), access to physical tools like laptops or vehicles (productive assets), and connectivity, power, payments, and roads (infrastructure). He will not invest in any company that does not address at least one of these three conditions. Fintechs and AI, he argues, are definitionally job-destroyers; platforms that lease cars or train engineers are job-creators.

### Future Africa Built a ~$100M Portfolio Without DFI Capital
Aboyeji managed to construct a roughly $100 million venture portfolio in Africa with only one institutional LP — the Africa Finance Corporation, a $17 billion entity that is 60% owned by the Central Bank of Nigeria but operates with full commercial discipline. He credits AFC's entrepreneurial culture (it originated the Lobito Corridor rail project entirely in-house) as the reason the relationship works. Every other DFI he encountered was structurally incompatible with early-stage fund building.

### DFI-Backed VC Ecosystem in Africa Is Now "Constipated" — Liquidity Is the Fix
Aboyeji argues that because DFIs funded most African VCs, and those VCs over-valued companies, the whole ecosystem is now locked up with no exits. His proposed solution is systems thinking: DFIs should audit their aggregate exposure across all fund LPs, mark positions to market at reasonable prices, conduct a coordinated fire sale, and unlock liquidity. This would allow companies to recapitalize, adopt AI-era operating models, and start growing again — rather than sitting on balance sheets at inflated valuations.

### Outcomes-Linked Debt Is the Right Instrument for Workforce Investment
Aboyeji sketches a concrete financial structure: for every AI engineer trained who passes certification and gets hired, a funder pays a fixed fee (he uses $5 as an example unit). The trainer borrows against that future receivable at a discount ($3), the lender earns a spread ($2), and everyone wins without requiring equity or a traditional fund structure. Applied to his vehicle fleet program — 500 jobs at $10,000 per car — the total capital need is $5 million, structured as an asset-backed loan against a leasing revenue stream.

### Buco — Three Concurrent Ventures Operationalizing the Jobs Formula
In 2026 Aboyeji launched Buco as an operating company inside Future Africa to directly build job-creating businesses. The three active tracks: (1) an AI workforce program that screened 25,000 candidates and enrolled 750, targeting Nigeria's estimated 30,000-person AI-trainable population; (2) an electric-vehicle fleet leasing program with agreements for 10,000 cars at roughly $1,000 per month per vehicle; and (3) a bid to acquire a West African subsea cable platform with metro networks in Lagos and Benin, to expand national connectivity infrastructure.

### Barbados as a "Black Singapore" — A Caribbean Application
Aboyeji is actively advising Barbados (population 240,000) on a development strategy he describes as building a "black Singapore": attract African diaspora talent, build out housing and training infrastructure, and leverage the island's hospitality culture as a foundation for skill development. He has a close relationship with the Prime Minister and sees Barbados not as a destination in itself but as a regional talent and infrastructure hub. He expressed direct interest in any CAF-adjacent work in the Caribbean.

## Regulatory & Structural Observations

Aboyeji does not engage with specific regulatory frameworks but makes a structural observation relevant to CAF's design choices: DFIs that require fund managers to accept a $10 million minimum check at 20% ownership are imposing a de facto $50–100 million minimum fund size. This structurally excludes managers who are solving $10 million problems — the exact scale appropriate for small-country ecosystems like those in Central America and the Caribbean. He recommends that a well-designed DFI LP would be stage- and scale-sensitive, offer smaller flexible checks, and measure success by verifiable job placements rather than portfolio company headcounts or documented employment forms.

## Recommendations & Insights

- **Reframe the LP offer around outcomes, not process.** CAF should consider outcomes-linked instruments — disbursements triggered by certified job placements, not fund formation milestones or portfolio documentation.
- **Lower the minimum check threshold dramatically.** A $2–3 million LP check into a $10–15 million fund is more useful to a small-country ecosystem than a $10 million check that forces artificial scale.
- **Audit aggregate DFI exposure and pursue coordinated liquidity events.** Rather than letting constipated portfolios run to zero, CAF could convene DFI co-investors to mark positions to market and engineer structured secondaries.
- **Invest in platforms and infrastructure, not just companies.** Workforce training pipelines, vehicle fleet programs, and connectivity infrastructure are the systems through which jobs emerge — individual company equity investments are insufficient.
- **Engage Africa Finance Corporation as a model.** Aboyeji offered to introduce the team to AFC leadership as an example of a DFI that operates with commercial discipline and entrepreneurial culture — directly relevant to CAF's design questions.
- **Connect with Aboyeji on Caribbean/Afro-Latin applications.** He is already engaged in Barbados, has expressed interest in Afro-descendant communities in Colombia and the broader Caribbean, and is willing to share his full playbook with partners focused on those populations.
