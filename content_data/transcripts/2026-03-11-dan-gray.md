This interview focuses on structural strategies for building VC ecosystems from the ground up in capital-constrained regions, with direct relevance to CAF's mandate in small Latin American countries.

- Current VC Market Analysis
- Emerging managers hit hard since 2020-2021 boom
- Too much exuberance in hot categories during peak
- Severe overcorrection now, market became top-heavy
- Worse at finding outlier opportunities VC should target
- Europe/Canada face similar issues with additional challenges
- Less risk tolerance among LP base
- Reduced appetite for early-stage investing
- Already top-heavy ecosystems worsened by recent shifts
- Latin America pension systems: $1.2-1.4B AUM, only ~0.05% in VC (hundreds of millions)
- Strategic Recommendations for Emerging Markets
- “Missing middle” funding gap is real but wrong solution
- Don’t fill the middle directly - fill the bottom first
- Early-stage pipeline growth naturally creates middle-stage opportunities
- Government attempts to fund growth stage directly don’t work well
- Optimal approach for capital-desert regions:
- Start with local angel investor ecosystems
- Help successful angels build funds eventually
- Use asymmetric return structures (like British Business Bank model)
- Kyle Briggs paper: government VC model with diversification + capital recycling becomes self-sustaining after ~30 years
- Network effects crucial - avoid multipolar dispersion, concentrate resources geographically
- Tax incentives work well for angels, problematic for funds (EIS/SEIS example)
- Research shows: more market inefficiencies = greater productivity gains from early-stage allocation vs late-stage
- Spray-and-pray approach works for government VC seeking solid performance + positive externalities
- Avoid pursuing “hot categories” - consistent government mistake for decades
- Better model: strategic capabilities valuable long-term (like In-Q-Tel/CIA approach)
