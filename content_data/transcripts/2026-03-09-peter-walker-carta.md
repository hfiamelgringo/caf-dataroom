## Executive Summary

Peter Walker, who leads Carta's research function and sits atop the most comprehensive private-company and fund-administration dataset in the US (60,000+ companies, 3,000+ venture funds), offers a clear-eyed and somewhat sobering picture of the current US venture landscape. Venture capital has effectively bifurcated: a narrow "golden circle" of roughly 25 hyped AI companies absorbs a disproportionate share of capital, while the rest of the market struggles to fundraise. Forty-four cents of every dollar on the Carta platform in 2024 went to an AI company — more than double the 2022 share — and the median time from seed to Series A has stretched to over two years. For small-country ecosystems, Walker's most actionable message is that capital right-sizing matters more than geography, and that a development bank willing to be an anchor LP — writing the first check, providing candid feedback, and opening doors to other LP classes — can be a catalytic force that no other actor in these markets can replicate.

Walker also cautions that the venture proposition has gotten harder as capital has flooded the asset class: top-quartile funds typically do not beat the S&P 500, and only the top 5% reliably produce 4–5× net returns. For CAF, this argues for cost-averaging entry across vintages, favoring a larger number of smaller fund bets over mega-fund concentration, and using fund-of-funds relationships selectively for learning and co-investment access rather than pure return optimization.

## Stakeholder Background

Peter Walker runs the Insights team at Carta, the dominant equity-management and fund-administration platform for US private markets. Carta administers cap tables for over 60,000 private companies and serves as back-office provider (general ledger, valuations, audit, tax) for more than 3,000 US venture funds — a majority of all active US VC managers. His role is effectively that of a public researcher and internal data strategist, producing market-direction analysis that is widely followed by founders, GPs, and LPs. He has direct visibility into fundraising timelines, capital flows, and fund performance across the broadest available private-market dataset.

## Key Findings

### Venture capital has split into two irreconcilable markets — and the divide is widening
The term "venture capital" now covers at least two distinct asset classes. A "golden circle" of roughly 25 AI-adjacent companies commands the overwhelming majority of capital and attention, while founders outside that circle face a structurally difficult fundraising environment. OpenAI's most recent round alone would have equaled or exceeded 50% of all venture raised in any prior year, yet Walker argues this capital was never on offer to the broader market — it simply would not have been deployed otherwise.

### AI absorbs 44% of all Carta-tracked venture dollars, and that share is still climbing
In 2024, 44 cents of every venture dollar on the Carta platform went to an AI company, more than double the 2022 figure, and Walker estimates AI now accounts for over 65% of software investment. A significant rotation is also underway from software into hardware, biotech, energy, and robotics — sectors Walker expects to represent roughly 50% of venture by 2027, up from today's 35%. Any company pitching US investors without an AI component faces an uphill battle regardless of geography.

### Fundraising timelines have stretched materially, compressing the founder runway
The median time between a seed round and a Series A in the US is now just over two years, up from approximately 18 months three to four years ago. At the 75th percentile, founders are waiting over three years. The traditional VC advice to plan for fundraising every 18–24 months is now "optimistic." This compression is less acute for golden-circle companies, which in extreme cases (e.g., Ramp in 2025) raised multiple rounds in a single year.

### Only the top 5% of venture funds reliably beat public markets — the asset class average is weak
Top-quartile venture capital typically does not outperform the S&P 500. Roughly 60% of funds fail to return initial capital; about 30% return 1–3× over 10–12 years. Only the top 5% produce 4–5× net multiples. The old benchmark of a 3× net TVPI over 10 years no longer compensates investors adequately; in a 15-year liquidity environment, Walker argues the hurdle should be 4×. The fund-raising environment for LPs has also tightened because many existing LPs are simply waiting for liquidity from earlier vintage funds before re-upping.

### Bay Area concentration is structural, not cyclical — but the pattern holds globally
The Bay Area captures approximately 40 cents of every US venture dollar and is larger than the next nine global venture cities combined. Walker describes this as a fractal pattern: concentration at the city level mirrors concentration at the fund and company level. For founders in Latin America, he recommends visiting San Francisco several times a year to understand investor thinking — but explicitly advises against opening a US office until a company is actively selling to US customers.

### A development-bank LP's most powerful tool is being a willing anchor check
Walker identifies anchor investing — being the first substantial check into a fund — as the single most catalytic action available to an LP in a nascent ecosystem. Most institutional LPs refuse to invest first and wait for social proof. A development bank willing to move early removes the most common fund-formation bottleneck. He also emphasizes the value of cross-pollinating LP types: many emerging GPs have strong family-office relationships but cannot get a callback from institutions, or vice versa, and a well-networked anchor LP can bridge that gap.

### Strategy discipline is the scarce resource in emerging ecosystems — and LPs must enforce it
Walker observes that many GPs drift toward whatever is generating markups rather than maintaining their stated thesis. For a development bank trying to build self-sustaining local ecosystems, the most valuable thing it can do beyond writing checks is hold GPs accountable to their declared strategy. When 25–40% of a GP's portfolio does not match the thesis, the LP should say so directly. That discipline is what prepares emerging GPs to eventually raise from US fund-of-funds and institutional LPs.

### International venture can outperform the US on a multiples basis if capital stays right-sized
Walker's most pointed insight for CAF's strategy: European and other non-US venture funds may outperform US funds on a multiples basis over the next decade — not in absolute dollars, but in return multiples — precisely because capital has not flooded those markets. The key risk is the exit environment: understanding whether a company will need to access US or larger markets to generate a real fund-returning outcome is essential due diligence for any LP deploying into small countries.

## Regulatory & Structural Observations

Walker does not address specific regulatory frameworks but makes a structural observation relevant to fund design: the historic 10-year venture fund lifecycle is now effectively 12–15 years (Stripe is 17 years old), and LPs modeling IRRs on a 10-year clock are systematically underestimating how long capital will be locked up. For a development bank designing fund commitments, this has governance and budget-cycle implications that differ from traditional project-finance timelines.

## Recommendations & Insights

- **Be the anchor LP.** The most impactful thing CAF can do is write the first substantial check into emerging funds in the region. This removes the chicken-and-egg problem that stalls most fund formation in thin LP markets.
- **Cost-average entry across vintages.** Do not vary commitment pace based on liquidity conditions or short-term market signals. Consistent annual deployment smooths vintage-year risk.
- **Favor a larger number of smaller bets over mega-fund concentration.** Mega funds will struggle to produce the multiples needed to justify venture's illiquidity premium. Smaller, focused managers are where the highest multiples will come from.
- **Use fund-of-funds selectively for learning.** The fee layer is a drag, but fund-of-funds relationships provide co-investment access and a window into how institutional-grade fund management operates — valuable for a team building expertise in a new asset class.
- **Open doors to other LP types.** CAF's institutional brand can bring emerging GPs into conversations with family offices, pension funds, and development-finance institutions they could never access independently. This network function may matter as much as the capital itself.
- **Hold GPs to their thesis.** When portfolio construction drifts from the stated strategy, say so clearly. Strategy discipline is what makes regional funds legible to outside institutional capital.
- **Bring research questions to Carta.** Walker explicitly invited CAF/PFA to surface specific data questions. If Carta has the data, he will share it; if not, practitioner questions help direct his research agenda.
