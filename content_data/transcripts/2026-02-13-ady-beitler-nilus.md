## Executive Summary

Ady Beitler brings a decade of hard-won operational experience building Nilus — a food-access social enterprise that evolved from an Argentine NGO into a $20M venture-backed for-profit — into a candid critique of how impact investing actually functions for entrepreneurs on the receiving end. His central argument is that "impact" as a label is nearly meaningless in practice: impact funds and commercial VCs ask the same questions (exit timeline, revenue multiples, EBITDA), and the divergence between them tracked closely with interest rate cycles rather than mission conviction. When rates were near zero, impact funds were patient and humane; as rates rose post-2022, they converged with commercial VCs under pressure from their own LPs.

For CAF's proposed impact equity fund, Beitler's most actionable guidance centers on three design choices: (1) be honest about accepting lower multiples (2–3x is real impact, not a failure); (2) if the fund uses CAF's own balance sheet with no external LP pressure, lean into that advantage with longer timelines; and (3) embed mission protection directly into governance documents — quorum thresholds, founder veto rights on mission changes, and share class structures — because the difference between a real impact fund and a rebranded VC lives entirely in those details.

This perspective is directly relevant to CAF's dual mandate of launching an impact vehicle and rethinking its commercial VC fund, particularly for smaller markets where exit pathways are thin and founder protection from mission drift is existential.

## Stakeholder Background

Ady Beitler is the CEO and co-founder of Nilus, a company he built from a nonprofit food-distribution model in Argentina into a venture-backed for-profit that raised $20M from over 90 equity investors, including Plug and Play Ventures, TechnoServe, and the Elea Foundation for Ethics and Globalization (Switzerland). Nilus subsequently licensed its logistics and technology platform to the UN World Food Programme, powering school meals programs in Guatemala, El Salvador, and Ecuador reaching millions of beneficiaries. Beitler previously served as a senior specialist in trade and investment at the IDB (2010–2017/18). He was named Social Entrepreneur of the Year at the World Economic Forum in 2023 and is a member of the Schwab Foundation. His perspective is grounded in both the policy world and the lived experience of being on the entrepreneur's side of the impact investing table through multiple fundraising rounds, board conflicts, and a structural pivot away from direct operations toward technology licensing.

## Key Findings

### Impact funds and commercial VCs are functionally indistinguishable — the difference is in what they call themselves
After a decade of raising from over 90 investors across impact funds, foundations, and VCs, Beitler concluded that the due diligence, exit expectations, and governance pressure are nearly identical across fund types. Both ask for multiples on invested capital, exit timelines, and revenue or EBITDA comparables. The only meaningful distinction, in his view, is branding — and even that is eroding as impact funds chase LP capital in a higher-rate environment.

### Rising interest rates post-2022 drove impact funds to mimic commercial VC behavior
Beitler identifies a direct causal link between the price of money and the patience of impact capital. When interest rates were near zero (roughly 2015–2021), impact funds could raise easily and afford to be patient with timelines and modest multiples. As rates rose sharply from 2022 onward, funds faced LP pressure to promise competitive returns, collapsing much of the behavioral gap with commercial VCs. He frames this as rational financial market behavior, not a failure of mission.

### A CAF-owned fund with no external LP obligations is a structural competitive advantage — if it accepts lower multiples honestly
If CAF's impact fund is capitalized entirely from the bank's own balance sheet and does not need to return capital to outside LPs on a fixed schedule, Beitler argues this removes the primary mechanism that forces mission drift. The key condition is explicit, upfront acceptance of 2–3x returns over a 7+ year horizon, communicated clearly to portfolio companies. He contrasts this favorably with IDB Lab, which he says only follows rounds led by named VCs and applies no genuine impact discipline to investees.

### Mission protection lives in governance documents, not in fund marketing
Beitler's most concrete recommendation is that CAF's legal and investment teams focus intensely on articles of incorporation, share class structures, and quorum thresholds. He cites the Elea Foundation's model term sheets as best-in-class: Elea requires an 80% supermajority to change a company's mission, compared to the standard 51% required under most venture association bylaws. Without this protection, founders lose mission control at Series B when dilution drops them below majority, and investors can legally redirect the company toward any profitable activity.

### Regulatory barriers in Latin America force social enterprises into for-profit structures, distorting capital needs
Nilus converted from a nonprofit to a for-profit not for strategic reasons but because Argentine law prohibits nonprofits from operating commercial food distribution services — a restriction that does not exist in the United States, where nonprofit supermarkets and theaters are common. This forced Nilus into venture capital markets with standard return expectations despite a social mission, creating structural tension that persisted throughout its growth. Beitler sees this regulatory gap as a systemic problem across the region that CAF should factor into fund design.

### Nilus's technology now powers UN WFP school meals in Guatemala, El Salvador, and Ecuador — but the commercial model broke before reaching scale
At its peak, Nilus operated direct food delivery into low-income urban areas across Argentina and Mexico, essentially functioning as a DoorDash for food deserts in slums. It licensed its logistics platform to the UN World Food Programme, currently serving millions of beneficiaries across Guatemala, El Salvador, and Ecuador. However, the direct-delivery model proved unsustainable without $100M+ in capital, leading to a strategic retreat to pure technology licensing — a pivot that Beitler acknowledges left food-desert communities paying higher prices without Nilus's subsidized delivery.

### Credible impact investors in the region are scarce; Beitler identifies three worth studying
Beitler names Promotora Social México (PSM), founded by Ignacio Avaros (also founder of Banco Compartamos), as the strongest Latin American example of a genuinely mission-aligned fund investing its own capital with no LP pressure. He also cites the Elea Foundation for Ethics and Globalization (Switzerland), led by Peter Woofley, former UBS CEO, as the most technically sophisticated impact investor Nilus encountered. TechnoServe, despite being a 501(c)(3) equity investor that does not return capital to shareholders, still subjected Nilus to identical valuation and audit conversations as commercial VCs.

## Regulatory & Structural Observations

The nonprofit-to-for-profit conversion that Nilus underwent in Argentina reflects a broader structural problem: Latin American legal frameworks do not recognize hybrid legal forms that allow nonprofits to operate revenue-generating commercial services. This forces social enterprises with genuine impact missions into standard equity markets where return expectations are misaligned with their operating models. Beitler notes that this same barrier does not exist in the United States, where nonprofits routinely operate commercial enterprises. CAF's fund design should account for the fact that many of its potential portfolio companies in Guatemala, Honduras, Costa Rica, and the Dominican Republic may be operating as for-profits not by choice but because no legal alternative exists, and their capital needs and governance requirements should be assessed accordingly.

## Recommendations & Insights

- **Anchor the fund's value proposition on honest multiple expectations.** Publicly committing to 2–3x returns over 7+ years will differentiate CAF's fund from commercial VCs and attract entrepreneurs who have been burned by impact investors who promised patience and delivered VC-style pressure. The entrepreneurs will know who is real.
- **Leverage the no-external-LP structure aggressively.** If CAF funds the vehicle from its own balance sheet, it should market this explicitly to entrepreneurs as a structural guarantee of patience — something no LP-dependent fund can credibly offer.
- **Hire lawyers who understand impact governance, not just venture law.** The work of protecting mission happens in quorum thresholds, preferred vs. ordinary share distinctions, and founder veto clauses — not in fund marketing documents. Study the Elea Foundation's model term sheets as a starting point.
- **Include entrepreneurs who have actually taken impact capital in the design process.** Beitler offers to convene a group of 100+ founders who have raised from impact funds and can provide detailed, candid feedback on what worked and what failed — a resource CAF should use before finalizing fund structure.
- **Do not replicate the IDB Lab follow-on-only model.** IDB Lab's practice of only investing alongside named VCs means it provides no genuine additionality and applies no real impact discipline. CAF should lead rounds in its target markets, not follow.
- **Connect with Promotora Social México and the Elea Foundation** as structural and governance models before finalizing fund documents. Beitler offers introductions to both Ignacio Avaros at PSM and the CIO of the Elea Foundation.

## Notable Quotes

> "Impact has a price. If you want to do impact investing, your expectations on the multiples need to be adjusted. The idea that your exit will be to General Catalyst while you're doing something for the world — that's just not realistic." — on the fundamental trade-off in impact fund design

> "Impact funds and VCs are very similar. The only thing that's very different is what they like to be called. The questions are pretty much the same: how long until exit, what's the multiple on the valuation?" — on the convergence of impact and commercial investing

> "When interest rates started going up around 2022, all the niceties of the impact funds began to look a lot more like VCs. They had to promise more, because their LPs had better options elsewhere." — on the interest-rate driver of impact fund behavior

> "The Elea Foundation requires an 80% supermajority to change the company's mission. Standard venture association law requires 51%. So at Series B, when founders no longer hold 51%, the shareholders get together and — for all the mission cares — you can become a Hooters franchise." — on why governance details are decisive

> "Make sure entrepreneurs are at the table. You need to have bruises from 10 or 15 investors to be able to speak about this. It is traumatic — the commitments that were made and never fulfilled. It's a horror story." — on the importance of founder voices in fund design
