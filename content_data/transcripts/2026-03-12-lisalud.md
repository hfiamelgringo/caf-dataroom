## Executive Summary

Marjorie, co-founder of LiSalud — a Honduran cloud-based laboratory information management system — offers a ground-level account of building a bootstrapped health-tech startup in one of Central America's most underserved innovation ecosystems. Founded around 2019, LiSalud has grown to over 80 laboratory clients in Honduras on a subscription model, with a small team of four full-time employees. The company has received only approximately $7,000 in total non-dilutive support from programs like the Honduras Digital Challenge and Tech for Dev, and has never closed a formal financing round. Despite having clients in El Salvador and running advertising campaigns in Guatemala, the company has been unable to achieve regional scale due to a combination of knowledge gaps, absence of in-country partners, reputational bias against Honduran firms, and lack of suitable financing instruments.

For CAF, this interview is particularly valuable because Marjorie articulates what startups at the seed-to-early-growth stage actually need: not just capital, but network access, hands-on operational support (developers, designers, market-specific agents), and a patient investor willing to understand a niche vertical. She identifies the collapse of structured support programs — the Digital Challenge has shut down, Tech for Dev closed around 2023 — as leaving the ecosystem dormant. Her call for a bold, visible convening mechanism and a special fiscal regime for digital-service companies offers concrete policy hooks for CAF's intervention design.

## Stakeholder Background

Marjorie is a microbiologist by training who co-founded LiSalud after identifying persistent information-management failures in clinical laboratories across Honduras. She partnered with a systems engineer who shared a laboratory background, and the two launched the product formally around 2019 after winning the Honduras Digital Challenge that year. She left two jobs in 2020 to pursue LiSalud full-time, reaching commercial scale from 2021 onward. She is an active voice in the Honduran startup community, serves as a guest lecturer at UNITEC, and has direct experience with multiple accelerator and grant programs in the region.

## Key Findings

### LiSalud is a revenue-generating, bootstrapped health-tech company that has outgrown its domestic funding environment
LiSalud serves over 80 clinical laboratory clients in Honduras on a tiered monthly subscription, ranging from basic patient-record management to full equipment automation via barcode integration. The company has four full-time employees with full legal benefits, plus two to three freelancers. In seven years of operation, total external funding received has been approximately $7,000 — composed entirely of prize money and small grants from the 2019 Honduras Digital Challenge and two cohorts of the Tech for Dev program — never a formal investment round.

### Honduras is too small a market to justify a domestic-only technology strategy, but regional expansion has stalled at the conversion stage
Marjorie is explicit that no technology business in Honduras can reach meaningful scale without a regional footprint, contrasting Honduras with Chile, Brazil, and Mexico where domestic TAM is sufficient. LiSalud already has paying clients in El Salvador and has run paid advertising campaigns in Guatemala, generating strong inbound interest. However, deals consistently fail to close: prospective clients express interest after product demos but do not complete payment, which Marjorie attributes to credibility bias against Honduran-origin companies and the absence of a trusted local presence or reference customer.

### The Honduran startup support infrastructure has effectively collapsed, leaving the ecosystem without an anchor institution
The Honduras Digital Challenge, which LiSalud credits as its launch platform in 2019, has shut down. Tech for Dev — a joint initiative of UNITEC, the IDB, and Ficohsa — closed around 2023 under the previous administration. An INCAE knowledge program also concluded. Marjorie states flatly that as of early 2026, she is unaware of any active structured support program for tech startups in Honduras. She describes the remaining entrepreneurial talent as dispersed and isolated, with some active in crypto communities in San Pedro Sula but with no centralizing institution.

### Bank credit is structurally inaccessible for digital-service companies, with rates around 17% and no appropriate product category
When LiSalud explored bank financing, the best available rate was approximately 17% annually — assessed two to three years ago and believed to have risen further since. More fundamentally, Honduran banks lack a credit product category for digital-service businesses: loan officers classify them alongside photography studios or social-media agencies, requiring documentation frameworks designed for goods-based businesses. LiSalud pays VAT and income tax and maintains formal accounts, but even accessing the correct tax-filing process for a pure digital-service firm is described as unnecessarily complex and burdensome.

### A prior attempt to close an accelerator deal in El Salvador collapsed over equity terms and in-kind misalignment
Approximately eighteen months before the interview, LiSalud advanced to the third meeting stage with a Salvadoran accelerator (identified only by a triangular logo, offering approximately $100,000) before discovering the entire commitment was in-kind — legal advisory and technical consulting — rather than cash. The accelerator also requested approximately 8% equity in exchange. Marjorie rejected the deal because she needed cash to hire developers to build the features the accelerator required within a three-month acceleration window. She describes this as a structural mismatch between what accelerators offer and what early-stage product companies actually need.

### Startups want investors who bring networks and operational resources, not just capital or abstract mentorship
Marjorie articulates a clear preference profile for an ideal investor: capital combined with a curated network that opens specific doors — a hospital for a pilot, a country-specific marketing specialist, junior developers paid by the fund, a legal advisor on cross-border incorporation. She explicitly distinguishes useful in-kind support (a funded graphic designer for six months, or two paid junior programmers) from generic mentorship sessions, which she regards as educational but insufficient to accelerate execution. She emphasizes that investors should also invest time in understanding the specific vertical rather than imposing generic playbooks.

### A special regulatory and tax category for digital startups is the single most urgent structural reform Marjorie identifies
LiSalud is currently classified as a micro or small enterprise with no differentiated treatment. Marjorie argues that pure digital-service businesses face a category error in the Honduran tax system: VAT filing is designed for product-based businesses, making compliance unnecessarily complex and costly for firms with no physical inventory. She proposes a five-to-ten year tax exemption or zero-filing threshold for digital startups until they surpass a defined revenue ceiling, arguing that current compliance costs actively discourage formalization and deter talented Hondurans — who can earn up to $20,000 per month working remotely for U.S. companies — from founding local ventures.

## Regulatory & Structural Observations

Honduras currently has no dedicated legal or fiscal category for digital-service businesses or technology startups. LiSalud operates under general micro and small enterprise rules, which creates cascading friction: VAT declarations assume physical inventory transactions, income-tax reporting is designed for product businesses, and bank credit underwriting has no framework for evaluating recurring-revenue SaaS models. Marjorie recommends creating a distinct classification for digital and innovation-oriented enterprises, paired with a temporary tax exemption regime tied to revenue thresholds — analogous to startup-friendly frameworks she has observed in El Salvador (though she notes El Salvador also fails to invest sufficiently despite favorable rhetoric). She also notes that government programs in Honduras are generally perceived as politically captured and unreliable, which is why LiSalud has deliberately avoided participating in any government-run initiative.

## Recommendations & Insights

- **Ecosystem reactivation requires a visible, high-energy convening event** — not a hotel seminar but a competitive, media-generating format like the original Honduras Digital Challenge — to identify and aggregate dispersed talent currently working in isolation on crypto, AI, and other emerging areas.
- **CAF or a similar multilateral should anchor a structured program** combining grant capital, curated mentorship, and operational resources (paid developers, designers, market-entry agents) rather than replicating the in-kind-only accelerator model that LiSalud rejected.
- **In-country presence or a trusted local partner is essential for B2B health-tech sales** in new markets; even a single pilot client at a recognized hospital functions as a credibility signal that unlocks the sales pipeline.
- **Funds should distinguish between serious entrepreneurs and curiosity-driven participants** — Marjorie will herself deliver this message at a UNITEC entrepreneurship class the week after the interview and sees it as foundational to designing any selection process.
- **Regulatory reform is a prerequisite, not a complement**: without a digital-business tax category and streamlined compliance, the compliance cost alone discourages formalization and diverts talent toward remote employment with foreign companies.
- **Key ecosystem connectors to engage**: Jesús Bermúdez (crypto and general ecosystem mapper, San Pedro Sula), Gabriela Torres (former Tech for Dev advisor, currently consulting, UNITEC-linked), and César Rul (former IDB representative within Tech for Dev).
