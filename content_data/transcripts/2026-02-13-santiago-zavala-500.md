## Executive Summary

Santiago Zavala, who leads Latin America for 500 Startups, offers one of the most data-grounded assessments in this series of the structural barriers to venture investing in small Central American economies. His core argument is that the math of venture — roughly 4–6% portfolio success rates requiring 100x returns at entry — makes pure-domestic-market investing in countries the size of Guatemala, Honduras, or Costa Rica nearly impossible without a credible multi-country or global expansion thesis. Companies that try to replicate a regional "time machine" playbook in markets too small for Uber or Rappi to bother competing in will generate good businesses but not venture returns.

His second major theme is the failure of local capital to circulate within the region. Despite raising roughly 10% of his most recent fund from Guatemalan LPs, 500 has deployed very little capital back into Guatemala — a contradiction he openly admits. Family offices across the region consistently prefer to diversify into U.S. or global funds rather than back domestic innovation, driven by currency risk, a lack of inspiring local leadership, and in some cases explicit institutional mandates against regional venture exposure. The self-reinforcing nature of this dynamic — capital flight weakens ecosystems, weaker ecosystems justify further capital flight — is the structural problem he most wants CAF and its partners to address.

For CAF specifically, the transcript surfaces concrete lessons: the catalytic signaling value of development bank LP participation (especially repeat vintages), the importance of Delaware entity structures for downstream capital access, and a handful of portfolio companies (including a Dominican Republic proptech and a Mexican logistics firm approaching $100M ARR) as live case studies.

## Stakeholder Background

Santiago Zavala grew up in a Mexican entrepreneurial family, taught himself to code, and built a developer community called Super Happy Dev House spanning 16 Mexican cities before moving to Silicon Valley as an early startup employee. He raised a $250,000 angel syndicate at age 24 — spending 35% on legal fees and generating one surviving fintech — which caught 500 Startups' attention and led to him joining the firm in 2012 to launch its first thematic regional fund. Now a member of 500's leadership team, he oversees $2.2 billion in assets under management across roughly 3,300 portfolio companies globally, with 350 investments and approximately $40 million deployed across Spanish-speaking Latin America through four funds (2013, 2016, 2019, 2024). He is also a Kauffman Fellow and sits on the investment committees of two corporate venture funds — one tied to Mexico's largest mobility conglomerate and one to a major Monterrey manufacturing group.

## Key Findings

### Venture math structurally excludes pure-domestic Central American companies from VC consideration
With a 4–6% portfolio success rate across four funds (e.g., 1 winner out of 74 companies in Fund 2, potentially 7 out of 109 in Fund 3), 500 needs approximately 100x returns on winners — implying a minimum $600M exit value after 50%+ dilution. At current software multiples of roughly 4x revenue, that requires $150M in sales. This is achievable in Mexico but mathematically improbable for a company permanently domiciled in Guatemala, Honduras, or Costa Rica without regional expansion.

### 500 raised ~10% of its most recent fund from Guatemalan LPs but deployed minimal capital back into Guatemala
Zavala explicitly flags this asymmetry as something he is not proud of. The firm has only one company that was ever based in Guatemala, a handful from Costa Rica, and two in the Dominican Republic. Honduras resulted in a significant failed engagement he described as too sensitive for full on-record discussion. The gap between capital raised in Central America and capital deployed there is the clearest quantitative expression of the region's deployment challenge.

### A Dominican Republic proptech is outgrowing Mexico's dominant incumbent with 12 people and $76K MRR
The company — Art Alperstate — builds white-label property listing and lead-conversion tools for real estate brokers. Starting in the Dominican Republic, it has 3,375 paying agents or companies, the majority of whom are now in Mexico and Colombia, growing faster than the established Mexican market leader. The team of 12 remains based in the Dominican Republic, citing cheaper operating costs and sufficient talent access, and has not raised local Dominican capital — it incorporated as a U.S. entity and received follow-on from a venture fund.

### Delaware incorporation is non-negotiable for accessing downstream global capital
500 invests almost exclusively into Delaware C-corps. Zavala is direct: virtually no global fund will invest in a Honduran, Guatemalan, or even Mexican corporate entity. Stripe Atlas has reduced U.S. entity formation to a $600 one-click process; the reputational signal of a U.S. entity also reduces perceived AML risk for international LPs. The firm stopped investing in Mexican entities entirely after AMLO-era tax changes in 2014–2015. Cayman structures are viewed as expensive and complex without sufficient benefit for early-stage companies.

### IFC and IDB participation had strong catalytic signaling value, especially for repeat vintages
IFC's investment in Fund 2 was pivotal at a moment when Zavala was stepping into a more visible leadership role without an MBA credential. IDB's participation in Funds 3 and 4 carried even stronger signaling precisely because two consecutive vintages from the same institution is rare — most development banks deliberately spread across many funds rather than backing the same manager repeatedly. Zavala expects IDB will not be in Fund 5 for this reason, and he is already managing how to explain their absence to prospective LPs.

### The Latin American family office capital flywheel is broken by a self-reinforcing diversification mandate
Mexico's wealthiest families — including a Coppel family office that already holds positions in 500's global and regional funds — frequently cite institutional mandates preventing them from investing in Latin American venture. The Coppel example is illustrative: the same family has invested in 500's global and non-LatAm funds but its LatAm-focused mandate is blocked. Zavala describes this as systemic: currency hedging logic, past liquidity disappointments, and the absence of visible ecosystem leadership all compound each other, and no single actor has emerged to break the cycle.

### Uruguay dramatically outperforms comparable small economies by combining government innovation grants with a global-first product mindset
500 has invested in many Uruguayan companies that use grants from the government innovation agency to build product, find initial customers in Uruguay and Argentina, then expand to Mexico and beyond — without relocating. Zavala contrasts this explicitly with Central American companies that build for the local market first, attempt Mexico, fail, and retreat. He attributes Uruguay's relative success to educational quality, a globally oriented culture, and the grant infrastructure — not market size.

### Corfo/Startup Chile is structurally sound but, in Zavala's view, suffers from operational drift and weak performance incentives
Chile's competitive advantage is that lithium extraction revenue flows directly to Corfo rather than through general government budgets, creating the best-funded innovation institution in Latin America. However, Corfo deploys capital as a senior loan with a first-lien repayment priority ahead of LPs — functioning effectively as a 1x liquidation preference — rather than as a true LP. This means a modestly successful fund returns nothing to equity LPs. Additionally, access to Corfo programs in practice often requires engaging specialist consultants who navigate the application process — limiting the value add the institution provides.

## Regulatory & Structural Observations

Zavala's most pointed structural observation is that Latin American tax regimes offer no meaningful incentive for local angel or fund investment, in contrast to the United Kingdom's scheme under which individual angel losses are deductible directly against tax liability (not income), and fund-of-fund structures carry similar loss-deductibility benefits — a framework he credits with generating $6 billion in new UK private investment over ten years. He is skeptical that current Mexican government leadership would be open to analogous reform, and more broadly believes that incentive design alone is insufficient without a cultural shift in the business elite's orientation toward domestic risk capital.

On entity structure, the practical message for CAF-backed vehicles or investees is unambiguous: any fund or accelerator in the four target countries should default to U.S. entity formation as the standard rather than an exception, and should proactively address the question of downstream investor expectations from the first check.

## Recommendations & Insights

Zavala's implicit and explicit recommendations for CAF and ecosystem builders:

- **Back regional-expansion-thesis companies, not domestic-only ones.** Any fund or accelerator in Guatemala, Honduras, Costa Rica, or the Dominican Republic should screen for founders who can credibly articulate a path to Mexico, Colombia, or global revenue — not just local market capture.
- **Replicate the Uruguay model.** Government grant capital (equivalent to what Corfo or ANII provides) combined with a cultural expectation of global product thinking is more impactful than large equity funds with domestic deployment mandates.
- **Development banks should do repeat vintages with the same managers.** The signaling value of a second consecutive investment from IDB or IFC is disproportionately large relative to the capital deployed. One-off LP positions spread across many first-time funds waste this signaling power.
- **Find and amplify the Juan Minis.** A handful of returnee entrepreneurs-turned-investors (Juan Mini in Guatemala, the Invariantes fund) are the only actors currently capable of closing the capital flywheel locally. CAF should identify, resource, and publicly platform these figures rather than building new institutions from scratch.
- **Avoid Corfo-style loan-disguised-as-LP structures.** If CAF designs a fund-of-funds or co-investment vehicle, it should participate as a true equity LP, not a senior lender, to avoid the perverse incentive problem that makes Corfo capital unattractive to serious fund managers.
- **Address the liquidity gap directly.** Public market listings (London Stock Exchange pre-IPO pathway, discussed on the call) and secondary tender offers are more impactful near-term liquidity solutions than waiting for a domestic IPO market that does not yet exist.

## Notable Quotes

After a decade and a half of doing this, what I'm most frustrated by is the lack of belief among the Latin American business community that the Latin American business community can actually create businesses. It's that simple." — on the core structural barrier to ecosystem development

> "I've had meetings with family offices of some of the most important people in this country where they told me they don't want to invest here because they already have their businesses in Latin America and want to diversify. I'm like — yeah, that's a great way for us to never grow." — on the self-defeating logic of regional capital flight

> "Can you, if everything aligns and success just happens to happen, get to $150 million in sales in the next seven to eight years? That's the summary of it. And now, if you go to a country that is one-quarter the size of Mexico's economy — which could be Argentina, Colombia, Chile — you'd need to be four times as big. And if you go to Central America, then you'd need to eat the absolute market. Is it really big enough?" — on the venture math applied to small markets

> "We have been more successful at raising capital in Central America than at deploying capital in Central America. That is not something I feel proud about." — on the Guatemala LP paradox

> "One of our biggest investors has absolutely told me he doesn't see this as an investment — he sees it as philanthropy and impact on Mexico and job creation.
