## Executive Summary

The Rockefeller Foundation's Latin America and Caribbean office — opened less than a year before this conversation — is organized around five sub-regions (Mexico, Brazil, Colombia, the Caribbean, and Central America) and three thematic pillars: energy abundance, heat-and-health, and food systems anchored by a global $100 million school-meals commitment. The Foundation is deliberately avoiding open grant calls and new projects built from scratch, preferring instead to join initiatives already in motion and to broker multi-stakeholder collaborations involving private sector actors, civil society, and government. Central America is a priority sub-region precisely because Rockefeller has had limited historical presence there, and the office views climate migration — especially along Guatemala's dry corridor — as a distinctive, regionally specific theme that no other Rockefeller regional office is currently pursuing.

For CAF's ecosystem strategy, the most actionable near-term opportunity is a focused convening on climate migration and sustainable agriculture in Guatemala or Honduras, designed to produce a published ecosystem map and seed a multi-organization collaboration. The Foundation has an existing Honduras footprint through a large World Food Programme school-meals grant that is nearing completion, making Honduras a natural bridge. The expressed interest in partnering with private sector companies already engaged in regenerative agriculture and rural food systems aligns directly with CAF-connected companies operating in the region.

## Stakeholder Background

Liana leads the Rockefeller Foundation's newly established Latin America and Caribbean regional office, based in Bogotá, Colombia. She joined the Foundation in 2025 to build the office from the ground up, with a mandate to develop strategic programming across five sub-regional clusters. Andrea Acevedo joined simultaneously to manage operations and program execution. Their perspective is relevant because the Foundation is actively seeking experienced regional partners to co-design and host convenings and collaborative projects — and has signaled openness to structured engagement with entities like PFA that have multi-country private-sector networks in Central America.

## Key Findings

### The Rockefeller Latin America office is new, small, and deliberately focused — not open for general grant applications
The office has been operating for less than a year, is based in Bogotá, and has divided the region into five sub-regions: Mexico, Brazil, Colombia, the Caribbean, and Central America. Rather than issuing open calls — which the team acknowledged would be unmanageable — the Foundation is proactively seeking a small number of experienced local partners who can co-design programming within its three defined pillars. Private-sector partnerships are an explicit strategic priority for the regional office, seen as more stable than government relationships given political transitions across the region.

### Three thematic pillars define all programming — energy access, heat-and-health, and food systems
The Foundation's programming framework is fixed: energy abundance (prioritizing off-grid communities gaining first-time clean energy access), heat-and-health (focused on extreme climate events affecting community health), and food systems (anchored by school meals). Within food systems, the Foundation made a global $100 million commitment in 2024 to reach 100 million children through school-meals initiatives, with linked programming in regenerative agriculture, local sourcing, and school gardens. The Foundation explicitly does not fund the per-child meal subsidy itself, which it considers a government obligation; it funds everything surrounding that ecosystem.

### Honduras is already a Rockefeller country — a large school-meals grant with the World Food Programme is winding down
Honduras was selected as the sole Central American country for one of Rockefeller's largest current global grants, executed through the World Food Programme's school-feeding program. That grant is now in its final phase. This creates both an organizational knowledge base about Honduras and a strategic interest in transitioning to a new engagement in the country — or broadening to a different Central American country — to maintain regional presence without duplicating what Brazil and Colombia already cover in school meals.

### Climate migration along Central America's dry corridor is the Foundation's single most distinctive regional thesis
Liana identified climate-driven migration — particularly from the dry corridor spanning Guatemala, Honduras, and El Salvador — as the theme most differentiating the Latin America office from Rockefeller's Africa and Asia operations. The Foundation sees this as an area where Central America can generate genuinely novel global programming. Guatemala's dry-corridor dynamics, where prolonged drought destroys subsistence agriculture and drives northward migration, are already recognized locally but have not yet been mapped into a structured investable framework. A current El Salvador project on heat stress among sugarcane workers is a small proof-of-concept in this direction.

### The Foundation's preferred engagement model is a multi-stakeholder convening that produces a follow-on collaboration — not a standalone grant
Drawing on recent experience in Brazil, Colombia, and New York, the Foundation wants convenings that result in a published ecosystem map and a commitment by three or four organizations to collaborate on a concrete follow-up project. The Foundation is willing to be one of those committing organizations. The ideal convening for Central America would bring together private-sector companies, small farmers, cooperatives, technical institutions, and government representatives around climate migration and sustainable agriculture — with Guatemala or Honduras as the most likely host country, targeting the first half of 2026.

### Private sector engagement is a core strategic differentiator for this regional office — and "regenerative agriculture" language may need softening
Liana was emphatic that the Latin America office is specifically designed to work with private sector actors as partners, not just beneficiaries or funders. She noted that the word "regenerative" can intimidate Central American agribusiness audiences because of its associated costs and technical framing, and recommended framing initiatives as "sustainable agriculture" or "resilient agriculture" to lower the barrier to private sector participation. Companies already connected to PFA — including those with track records in food systems and rural communities — were cited as exactly the type of anchor stakeholders the Foundation would want in a convening.

### The Foundation's innovative finance team is in internal restructuring and is not currently an active program arm
Although innovative finance was raised as a potential area of collaboration in earlier conversations, Liana clarified that the team responsible for this work is undergoing a reorganization and has paused external programming. Finance is expected to be repositioned as an enabler of the three core pillars rather than a standalone program, meaning it will not function as an entry point for near-term partnership design.

## Regulatory & Structural Observations

The Foundation does not operate as a government-linked institution but noted that its Africa and U.S. programs have historically been more government-partnered. For Latin America, the team has consciously pivoted toward private sector co-investment models, partly because of what they described as two years of significant government transitions across the region making public sector partnerships less stable. The closure of USAID programming and broader shifts in international cooperation financing were cited as context for why many previously funded initiatives are now stranded mid-implementation — making them attractive candidates for Rockefeller support as "push" investments rather than new starts.

## Recommendations & Insights

- **Design a focused convening, not a large forum:** The Foundation wants a technically deep, relatively small gathering (not a mass public event) in Guatemala or Honduras in the first half of 2026, centered on climate migration and sustainable agriculture. The explicit goal is to produce an ecosystem map of who is working on these issues and to facilitate a concrete multi-organization follow-up commitment.
- **Lead with "sustainable agriculture" not "regenerative agriculture"** when engaging Central American private sector audiences, particularly agribusinesses, to avoid signaling prohibitive cost burdens.
- **Leverage the Honduras WFP relationship:** The ending of the Foundation's current Honduras school-meals grant is a strategic opening. A new Honduras engagement — or one that bridges Honduras with Guatemala — would extend institutional continuity without competing with existing Colombia and Brazil programming.
- **Position PFA as the local coordination hub:** The Foundation is explicit that it wants a local partner to identify the right actors, design content, and manage logistics — and has offered to co-develop the convening concept collaboratively rather than wait for a finished proposal.
- **FAO Honduras and COHEP are validated entry points:** The Honduras agricultural sector's key conveying infrastructure — FAO co-organized forums with the Ministry of Agriculture reaching approximately 300 companies, and the Honduran Private Enterprise Council's enterprise development division — are already mapped and relationship-ready.
