## Executive Summary

Alex Macfarlan and Jacob Stern, co-founders of Albedo Solar, offer a ground-level account of building a lease-to-own solar SME company across Guatemala, El Salvador, and Honduras — and the systemic financing gaps that nearly prevented them from launching at all. With 200+ installations completed and 44% of the portfolio classified as high-social-impact clients, Albedo has validated its business model over five years. Yet their capital journey — from friends-and-family tickets of $20–50k, through angel investors and family offices, to a current $2 million seed equity round — has been driven more by personal networks and serendipitous conference meetings than by any functional ecosystem.

Their core message for CAF is threefold: MDB on-lending facilities are often stranded inside local banks whose internal credit processes are too slow and collateral-rigid to actually deploy the capital; impact equity investors paradoxically demand higher risk and higher returns than Albedo's low-risk, moderate-return model offers; and the most valuable interventions have been warm introductions and subsidized technical assistance — not financial products per se. A two-year IDB process for a modest facility exemplifies the process dysfunction they want MDBs to fix first.

For CAF, this interview is a detailed use case for what a fund-of-funds or DFI-backed facility must overcome to reach climate-impact SMEs in Central America: collateral inflexibility, slow institutional processes, absence of standardized legal structures for regional expansion, and a tech-biased VC market that leaves asset-backed, cash-flow-positive businesses without equity partners.

## Stakeholder Background

Alex Macfarlan is an Australian national who moved to Guatemala in 2011, spending roughly nine years in the nonprofit and USAID-funded development sector before co-founding Albedo Solar. Jacob Stern worked at Alterna, a Guatemalan social entrepreneurship center, where he helped launch an early impact debt fund for SMEs — an experience that directly informed Albedo's derisked solar lease model. The two met in Quetzaltenango and launched Albedo in January 2021. Their perspective combines on-the-ground operational experience in three Central American markets with firsthand engagement across the full spectrum of regional capital — from friends-and-family to impact funds to development bank facilities.

## Key Findings

### Central American SME Solar Has World-Class Fundamentals That the Financing Market Cannot Service
Guatemala, El Salvador, and Honduras offer three-to-four-year solar payback periods without subsidies — compared to twelve-to-eighteen years in North America and Europe even with heavy subsidies. Electricity prices in Guatemala run approximately three to four times higher than in Mexico. Despite this, market penetration across the three countries sits at just two to two and a half percent, leaving an enormous addressable market. The constraint is entirely on the financing side, not on customer demand or technology.

### The Friends-and-Family Dependency Is a Structural Failure, Not a Founder Quirk
Albedo could not have launched without access to personal networks willing to write $20–50k checks and tolerate early-stage risk. Jacob Stern explicitly flagged this as a systemic problem: "We wouldn't have been able to get off the ground if we didn't have access to friends and family networks and philanthropic capital." Founders without those social and financial privileges — the majority of Central American entrepreneurs — face a zero-to-one gap that no formal institution currently bridges.

### Local Banks Cannot Deploy Green On-Lending Facilities Because Their Internal Processes Block Disbursement
DFIs routinely place $100 million+ green credit lines with local banks, but local bank credit committees apply traditional collateral requirements — personal guarantees, land titles — that disqualify asset-rich, cash-flow-positive companies like Albedo. Albedo's solar lease portfolio, a strong liquid asset, carries almost no weight in local bank underwriting. DFI-backed partial guarantees that allow banks to evaluate portfolio health rather than fixed collateral could unlock capital that is already allocated but sitting idle.

### Impact Equity Investors Are Paradoxically Misaligned With Low-Risk Impact Companies
Albedo's equity search has repeatedly collided with a market structured around two poles: tech-focused VCs seeking 30–100x returns, and deep-impact funds seeking high-risk, high-return bets in narrow thematic areas. Albedo offers a third profile — low risk, roughly 10x client return, 0.3% default rate, 44% high-social-impact portfolio — but finds very few equity investors positioned to value that profile. The company is currently in a $2 million seed round with roughly $200k raised, targeting close in Q2 2026.

### MDB Process Speed Is as Important as Product Design
The most pointed recommendation from both founders was that MDB innovation should target process, not just financial instruments. Albedo has been in an IDB facility process for approximately two years. Jacob Stern's formulation: "What if a fund's innovation wasn't about the product but about the process — move faster, be a little more risk-accepting, and just see how it goes? If we lose a couple hundred thousand dollars trying that, we lose a couple hundred thousand dollars." Smaller, faster commitments — say $200k in a fraction of the time rather than $1 million after a year — would be more valuable to early-growth companies.

### The FLEA Conference Functions as an Informal Capital Market Patch
FLEA (Forum Latin America) has been among the most impactful single touchpoints for Albedo's fundraising — more effective than most formal accelerator programs. Macfarlan described it as "the Band-Aid on the illiquid capital market," where a chance conversation at a bar can change the trajectory of a company. He noted that if FLEA ran for two weeks instead of a few days, Albedo would attend the entire time. This dependence on informal conference networking signals how thin and relationship-gated the formal capital market remains.

### Regional Expansion Requires Subsidized Legal and Structural Playbooks That Do Not Exist
Albedo spent close to a year trying to determine the optimal structure for a regional holding company, consulting multiple lawyers and receiving contradictory advice across jurisdictions. The company also found that renewable energy tax incentives in El Salvador and elsewhere are designed for large utility-scale projects, not SME-facing installers. A standardized, publicly available decision tree — "if your company has these characteristics, use this legal structure, for these reasons" — would save companies months of founder time and expensive legal fees.

### Pomona Impact Accelerator Was the Most Effective Formal Support, But Technical Assistance Budgets Were Undersized
Among four to five accelerators Albedo has participated in — including two USAID Palladium rounds and a Pomona Impact renewables cohort in 2023 — Pomona was the only one that delivered significant return. Pomona established an impact center with a curated consultant marketplace, but the subsidy level was insufficient and the cost of genuinely specialized consultants (international tax structuring, legal holding company design) remained prohibitive. CrossBoundary was engaged for pre-capital-raise preparation but had its funding pulled alongside the broader USAID program cuts, cutting that process short.

## Regulatory & Structural Observations

Regulatory environments vary significantly across the three countries Albedo operates in, and none currently provides incentives accessible to SME-scale solar installers. El Salvador has the most explicit renewables incentive framework, but it is designed exclusively for large utility projects, leaving companies like Albedo ineligible. Honduras required navigating a complex local legal system with no obvious template. Guatemala's banking regulation focuses on personal and real-property collateral, making it structurally unable to recognize a performing solar lease portfolio as creditworthy collateral — despite that portfolio representing a predictable, long-duration revenue stream.

The absence of a standardized SAFE note instrument in Guatemala required Albedo to adapt the US SAFE contract for local legal conditions — a process they described as manageable but unnecessarily burdensome. Their current price round in 2026 represents their first formal equity pricing, a milestone that took five years of convertible instruments and legal iteration to reach.

## Recommendations & Insights

- **Guarantee facilities for on-lending**: CAF or a peer DFI should design partial first-loss guarantees specifically targeted at enabling local banks to underwrite renewable energy lease portfolios as collateral — not just personal or land guarantees. This could unlock already-allocated green credit lines that currently sit undisbursed.
- **Process speed as a product feature**: A fast-track facility offering $200–500k decisions in weeks rather than years would serve growth-stage impact companies better than larger, slower instruments. Piloting this with a defined loss tolerance would be a meaningful innovation.
- **Subsidized, selective technical assistance**: A curated pool of high-caliber consultants (legal, tax, capital markets) accessible to vetted companies at subsidized rates — with companies still paying a meaningful co-contribution — would address the gap left by CrossBoundary's defunding. Pomona's impact center was the closest attempt; the model needs deeper subsidy and more specialist options.
- **Regional legal playbooks**: CAF or a regional accelerator could commission standardized legal and tax structure guides for the most common regional expansion scenarios (three-country operations, holding company in Delaware or Panama, etc.), dramatically reducing founder time and legal cost.
- **Equity category creation**: There is a missing equity category — sub-$5 million tickets, three-to-five-year hold, asset-backed, 2–3x target return — that neither tech VCs nor traditional impact funds currently occupy. CAF's fund-of-funds could seed a vehicle explicitly positioned in this gap.
