## Executive Summary

José Kont, Director of Cuantico, brings together market research, media, and venture ecosystem advisory work across Latin America. Drawing on a confidential study commissioned by Peru's venture capital association, the Swiss cooperation agency, and Peru's development bank — in which his team interviewed over 350 funds active in Latin America — he identifies three structural barriers to VC growth in the region: an information gap that prevents investors from learning about early-stage deal flow outside their home markets; political instability that outweighs tax incentives in investor calculus; and the near-total absence of large family office and pension fund capital from the venture asset class, driven simultaneously by regulatory prohibition and basic investor ignorance of how the asset class works.

For CAF specifically, Kont sees multilateral institutions as uniquely positioned to play the catalytic education and convening role that underfunded national VC associations cannot. He explicitly endorses a CAF presence in Silicon Valley as a regional connectivity hub, drawing a direct parallel to Costa Rica's Ministry of Economy opening a permanent office there. His most pointed insight for Central America — particularly Guatemala — is that the bottleneck is not capital but the near-absence of founder talent with a global mindset, and that the fastest path to fixing this is importing experienced founders from other ecosystems, not teaching entrepreneurship in classrooms.

## Stakeholder Background

José Kont is the Director of Cuantico, a Guatemala-based firm operating at the intersection of market research, digital media, and venture ecosystem advisory. He entered the technology sector in 2005, co-founded a startup in 2012 that became the first Central American company accepted into the Wayra accelerator in Mexico (second cohort), and founded an online reputation management agency in 2019 that is now the regional leader serving major Latin American conglomerates. He also serves as a deal-flow scout for Impact ABC, a Latin American impact fund, giving him active investment exposure. His firm manages eight owned media properties and co-owns three regional outlets, making him both a primary researcher and a significant amplifier of ecosystem intelligence across the region.

## Key Findings

### Investor Information Gaps — Not Just Language, But Media Structure — Are the Primary Barrier to Cross-Border Deal Flow

Even neighboring countries in Latin America have near-zero awareness of each other's startup activity, because the region's journalism schools trained reporters to serve hyper-local audiences operating as near-monopolies. This structural media fragmentation means a fund in Argentina does not know what is happening in Brazil, and investors in Guatemala are unaware of deal flow in El Salvador. For investors without a local office, identifying early-stage companies worth backing is effectively impossible, suppressing cross-border capital flows regardless of fund appetite.

### Investors Prioritize Macro Stability Over Tax Incentives — and El Salvador Is the Evidence

In Cuantico's Peru study, fund managers consistently stated that government match funds and tax exemptions are secondary to rule of law, security, transport infrastructure, internet connectivity, and access to healthcare and education. El Salvador's security improvements under Bukele were cited as having directly attracted new funds interested in deploying capital in the country — a more powerful signal than any fiscal incentive. This finding challenges the default assumption in public policy that the primary lever is creating new financial instruments.

### Family Offices Across Latin America Are Almost Entirely Absent From Venture — and Don't Know It

Large family offices in Guatemala, El Salvador, Peru, and across the region — many built on agro-industrial wealth from coffee and sugar — do not participate in venture capital, and typically lack even one percent allocation to the asset class within broader private equity portfolios. Kont's research attributes this not to lack of interest but to a deep knowledge gap: many family office managers conflate venture with generic private equity and have never been approached with a coherent explanation of venture's risk-return profile or its role in reducing long-term portfolio risk through illiquidity.

### Pension Funds Face a Double Lock — Regulatory Prohibition and No Suitable Vehicles

Pension funds in the region cannot legally invest in venture in most jurisdictions, but Kont argues that even if regulations were liberalized, they still could not participate: the overwhelming majority of Latin American venture funds — over 90 percent of more than 350 funds in Cuantico's sample — are smaller than $15 million in size, well below the minimum ticket size of ~$50 million that pension funds require. GPs have not designed vehicles scaled or structured for institutional limited partners, creating a circular deadlock that Kont describes as a "padlock" requiring simultaneous work at three levels: regulator, GP vehicle design, and institutional LP readiness.

### Guatemala's VC Problem Is a Founder Shortage, Not a Capital Shortage

Kont states directly that capital is available in Guatemala — large, prosperous conglomerate families are willing to invest if presented with quality founders — but that he cannot source enough qualified deals working as a scout for Impact ABC. He attributes this to the absence of a local "mafia" effect: no PayPal Mafia, no Rappi Mafia, no BREX Mafia equivalent has yet emerged to transmit the venture playbook by example. The $140 million in Central American venture investment recorded in the most recent year was driven primarily by foreign founders relocating to special economic zones like Próspera in Honduras (including a single $40 million biotech seed round), not by organically grown local founders.

### The Fastest Ecosystem Accelerant Is Importing Experienced Founders — Not Classroom Education

Kont's prescription for Central America's founder gap is not curriculum-based entrepreneurship training but attracting 100 or more foreign founders per year to relocate and build companies in the region, following the logic of Startup Chile but stripped of its teaching component. He argues that founders learn to found by watching other founders, not in universities — and that Central American business culture is demonstrably more receptive to foreign entrepreneurs (Brazilian, Chilean, American) than to local ones pitching the same ideas, which he calls a persistent "malinchismo" dynamic that can be turned into a policy advantage.

### Public Sector Must Lead Ecosystem Education — Private Sector Has No Incentive To

Cuantico's research found that VC associations in Latin America typically have only 20–30 members and are entirely consumed by basic operational costs from membership dues, leaving them no capacity to educate regulators, pension fund committees, or ministries. Kont argues that only the public sector has the logistical capacity and credibility to convene multi-country education initiatives, pointing to Costa Rica's new Ministry of Economy office in Silicon Valley as a model. He explicitly names CAF, IDB, and CABEI as institutions with the research capacity, convening power, and alliance-building capability to catalyze this at a regional scale — and notes that CAF establishing a Silicon Valley presence would be an especially high-leverage move.

## Regulatory & Structural Observations

Pension fund investment in venture capital is blocked in most of the countries discussed through explicit regulatory investment manuals issued by national banking and insurance commissions (Kont references Honduras's National Commission for Banking and Insurance as a specific example). These manuals specify permitted asset classes and allocation percentages, and venture is absent or de facto excluded. Kont's core observation is that the regulators themselves do not understand the asset class — including why illiquidity is a feature rather than a defect — and therefore will not reform the rules until they are educated. He frames this as a sequential dependency: educate the regulator first, then reform the rules, then prompt GPs to design appropriately scaled vehicles, then bring in institutional LPs. He views this pathway as "long-term and difficult" compared to the faster lever of attracting foreign founders.

## Recommendations & Insights

- CAF should establish a permanent presence in Silicon Valley, potentially as a shared regional hub that allows each member country to rotate personnel through, distributing the cost while centralizing the relationship-building infrastructure — modeled on Costa Rica's new Ministry of Economy office but at a multi-country scale.
- Rather than one-week "tourism" delegations to Silicon Valley, CAF or governments should invest in sustained, long-term placements — sending engineers and founders to work inside companies like OpenAI and other frontier firms for months or years, then return to their home ecosystems.
- A regional Startup Chile equivalent — focused on attracting foreign founders to relocate and build in Central America, with fiscal and logistical incentives, but without a teaching mandate — would be the single highest-leverage intervention for the founder shortage.
- VC associations cannot carry the education burden alone; CAF and other multilaterals should step into the gap explicitly, using their convening and research capacity to educate regulators, family office committees, and pension fund investment boards.
- Family office engagement should be done through issues they already care about — generational wealth transfer, legacy, and impact — rather than leading with asset class education, meeting them in existing forums rather than creating new ones.
- Kont offered to connect the team with Shaun Lemon Hinner, an American-based multifamily office manager who manages the wealth of seven U.S. families from within Latin America and could offer a cross-cultural LP perspective.
