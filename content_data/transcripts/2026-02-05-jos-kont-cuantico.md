## Executive Summary

José Kont, Executive Director of Cuantico, a Latin American investment research firm, offers a frank diagnosis of why Guatemala has failed to develop a meaningful venture and startup ecosystem. His core argument is that the problem is not capital scarcity or high incorporation costs — both of which he considers manageable — but rather a structural absence of high-impact entrepreneurial talent and role models. The best-prepared Guatemalans emigrate to Mexico, the United States, or Miami, while those who stay default to traditional businesses. Without a critical mass of founders who have built scalable companies, there are no signals to attract domestic capital or inspire the next generation.

Kont's prescription draws on proven international playbooks: attract global founders via incentive programs modeled on Startup Chile or El Salvador's CornerNest partnership, unlock patient capital through matching fund structures similar to Brazil's 2000 pension fund vehicle with the Multilateral Investment Fund, and focus regulatory reform narrowly on fintech — the sector that captures 60% of Latin American venture investment — rather than attempting a broad, politically difficult all-of-government innovation strategy. He is particularly emphatic that a multilateral development bank like CAF should channel funds through professional fund managers, not directly to entrepreneurs, to avoid perverse political incentives.

For CAF, the key takeaway is that Guatemala's barriers are primarily cultural and structural rather than legal or fiscal. Targeted, time-bound interventions — talent attraction programs, matching funds with professional managers, and a focused fintech sandbox — offer more realistic leverage than comprehensive legislative reform in a country with chronic political instability.

## Stakeholder Background

José Kont is Executive Director of Cuantico, a Guatemala-based firm that produces research on how to invest in and from Latin America. He entered the startup world in 2012 as co-founder of SouthTour, which was selected by the Huayra accelerator in Mexico — an experience he describes as formative and only possible because it required him to leave Guatemala. That personal trajectory shapes his analysis: he views ecosystem development as inseparable from exposure to more mature environments, and is currently conducting Cuantico's annual study of the four markets covered by this project (Guatemala, Honduras, Costa Rica, Dominican Republic).

## Key Findings

### Guatemala Lacks a Fintech Regulatory Sandbox, Creating a Structural Barrier for the Most-Invested Sector

Guatemala has no flexible regulatory sandbox for fintech, which globally represents approximately 60% of Latin American venture investment and nearly $3 billion in 2024. Establishing a bank in Guatemala is described as a legally complex, expensive, and protracted process with high effective entry barriers, dominated by a small set of incumbent institutions. Without a tailored fintech framework analogous to Mexico's or Colombia's fintech laws, the country systematically excludes itself from the primary driver of regional venture activity.

### Brain Drain, Not Capital Shortage, Is the Binding Constraint on Startup Formation

Guatemala's most prepared talent emigrates to Mexico, the United States, or Miami rather than building companies locally. Domestic investors do have capital — Castillo Hermanos acquired a U.S. company for $1.5 billion, and Corporación Multi-Inversiones invested $75 million in an Israeli 3D-printed meat startup — but they invest it externally or in foreign startups entering Guatemala, because local founders do not yet generate the signals needed to attract that capital. The problem is a chicken-and-egg loop: no founders, no investor learning curve; no investor track record, no founder incentives to stay.

### Foreign Startups Are Beginning to Enter Guatemala, Funded by Local Family Offices

A nascent but meaningful trend is emerging: startups from Mexico, Colombia, Venezuela, and El Salvador are landing in Guatemala and receiving investment from local family offices. Kont cites Airbus (from Impacta's portfolio), La Guagua (founded by Venezuelan founders, backed by a local family office), and Boxful (a Salvadoran startup) as examples. Younger members of wealthy Guatemalan families are demonstrably open to these deals, even if they still perceive a structural deficit of locally grown, globally scalable founders.

### Startup Chile and El Salvador's CornerNest Model Are the Most Replicable Templates for Guatemala

Kont argues that talent attraction programs — offering cash grants to international founders in exchange for establishing local headquarters — are faster and more viable than university-based ecosystem development or broad legislative reform. Startup Chile attracted global founders with $100,000 grants. El Salvador's CornerNest, a partnership with a Silicon Valley fund backed by presidential matching funds (reportedly $25 million each), has attracted numerous global companies to establish there. Honduras's Prospera zone in Roatán produced what Kont describes as "the largest single round in all of Central America" — a $40 million raise by a health-tech startup with founders from Italy, Estonia, and Israel — through tax exemptions and global talent access incentives.

### Matching Funds Through Professional Managers Are the Right Vehicle for a Multilateral

Rather than direct grants to entrepreneurs or complex multi-agency legislative coordination, Kont recommends that CAF deploy capital as matching funds channeled through professional fund managers. The government or multilateral provides patient capital with long-horizon return expectations; private fund managers deploy it under venture discipline. He explicitly warns against direct government-to-entrepreneur grants due to "perverse political incentives." Brazil's 2000 structure — where the five largest pension funds co-invested with the Multilateral Investment Fund in a $2 billion vehicle — is his preferred precedent, as it generated positive returns and catalyzed Brazil's current hub status.

### Guatemala's Territorial Tax System Is an Underutilized Asset for Attracting Remote-First Startups

Guatemala operates a territorial tax system: income generated from services delivered to foreign clients is subject to neither U.S. nor Guatemalan taxation. Combined with a low overall fiscal pressure environment (contrasted explicitly with Mexico and Brazil), this creates a structurally favorable condition for remote-first or export-oriented startups that is not currently marketed or leveraged. Kont notes that incorporating a corporation (the local equivalent of a C-Corp with multiple partners) costs approximately $1,000 and takes two to three weeks — not a meaningful barrier.

### Fragmented Public Institutions and Political Instability Make All-of-Government Strategies Unrealistic for Guatemala

Guatemala has a Secretariat of Innovation and Technology, a Ministry of Economy, a competitiveness program, and an exporters' association, all operating independently with no coordinating body. Kont explicitly contrasts this with Colombia's integrated approach and Chile's CORFO-anchored structure. He notes that Guatemala's president "lives on the edge because Congress won't let him work," making El Salvador-style executive-driven fintech legislation essentially impossible today. The politically realistic alternative is institution-anchored vehicles (matching funds, accelerator partnerships) that bypass the need for legislative consensus.

### Costa Rica Demonstrates What Guatemala Could Have Achieved — and Missed Twice

Kont uses Costa Rica as a cautionary benchmark: a country roughly one-third Guatemala's population that exports more than $16 billion in high-value services annually, versus Guatemala exporting three times less. Guatemala lost Intel to Costa Rica roughly 20 years ago when it lacked sufficient engineers, and Volkswagen chose Mexico over Guatemala more than a century ago for similar talent-pipeline reasons. Both losses, he argues, trace to the same root: an economic incentive structure that rewards exporting sugar, coffee, and bananas over developing human capital, reinforced by agricultural conglomerates whose political influence he describes as dominant. Guatemala's third-largest global sugar exporter status (behind only Brazil in Latin America) means the smallest sugar mill generates approximately $1 million per hour in revenue — giving these sectors overwhelming leverage in any fiscal policy debate.

## Regulatory & Structural Observations

Guatemala's regulatory environment for venture and fintech is characterized by absence rather than explicit prohibition. There is no fintech sandbox, no coordinating innovation body, and no legislation equivalent to Mexico's or Colombia's fintech laws. However, the cost and timeline for corporate formation are not significant barriers: a single-owner small business can be established in under a week at minimal cost with a 5% flat tax on revenue up to approximately $70,000–$100,000 per year; a multi-shareholder corporation costs ~$1,000 and takes two to three weeks.

The territorial taxation principle is the most structurally distinctive feature: offshore services income is taxable neither in Guatemala nor in the United States, a feature Kont believes is underexploited by policymakers and multilaterals promoting Guatemala as a destination for export-oriented startups.

The deepest structural obstacle is political: chronic instability between the executive and legislature makes comprehensive fintech or innovation legislation effectively unreachable in the current environment. This is compounded by the political economy of commodity exporters, whose fiscal privileges would be relatively threatened by technology-sector incentives and who exercise disproportionate influence over economic policy.

## Recommendations & Insights

- **Focus any regulatory push narrowly on fintech**, not broad innovation policy. A fintech-friendly sandbox or dedicated law — directed at the banking superintendent rather than requiring full legislative consensus — would capture the highest-return sector and is more politically tractable.
- **Deploy CAF capital as matching funds through professional managers**, not directly to startups or via government grants. This avoids political capture, ensures venture discipline, and mirrors the Brazil/Multilateral Investment Fund model that produced positive returns over a patient horizon.
- **Fund a talent attraction program** modeled on Startup Chile or CornerNest: offer incoming international founders a financial incentive tied to establishing headquarters locally. This is faster than university ecosystem development and directly addresses the founder supply gap.
- **Market Guatemala's territorial tax system** proactively as a competitive differentiator for remote-first, export-oriented startups — particularly given the country's 18 million population (comparable to Chile), dual-coast access, and proximity to the U.S. market.
- **Consider a single large anchor institution** (a major bank or development finance entity) taking on the Kazakhstan model role — committing $500 million–$1 billion, creating a physical hub, and associating with a global academic partner — as an alternative to multi-agency coordination that historically stalls.

## Notable Quotes

> "Guatemala is not a country that stands out for venture investment. The Global Entrepreneurship Monitor reports high entrepreneurship rates, but it doesn't make clear that 90% of that entrepreneurship is out of necessity — people who have nothing to eat and need to generate income somehow. High-impact entrepreneurship, in the strict sense, simply doesn't exist here." — on the gap between reported and actual innovation activity

> "Capital is very efficient — it goes where there is talent. And today, that startup-founder talent isn't here. So how do you develop it through universities? Very slowly. Through programs like Startup Chile? Much faster. You bring people who already know how to build startups, who've done it three times, and they come here and do it from here. That creates muscle memory in the people around them." — on talent attraction as the primary lever

> "A multilateral could come in and say: government, just assign me funds — not given directly to entrepreneurs, because that generates perverse political incentives — but assigned to professional capital managers, where the government's contribution has a longer-term return expectation. What I'm describing are matching funds." — on the preferred CAF deployment model

> "Guatemala has what's called territorial taxation. If I, from Guatemala, provide a service to a company in the United States, I don't have to pay taxes in the U.S. or in Guatemala, because I'm not providing it here for a local business. That's a real advantage that isn't being used." — on an undermarketed structural asset

> "In El Salvador, Nayib Bukele controls Congress and the judiciary.
