### Context

PROCOMER is Costa Rica's semi-autonomous export and investment promotion agency, 30+ years old, ~350 staff, 45 international offices. It is funded by zona franca canon plus a small export tax — not the general state budget — and reports through the Ministry of Foreign Trade.

### Key Findings

- Six-time ranked **#1 export-promotion agency in the world**; ISO-certified, KPI-driven
- Manages **800+ buyer-supplier matches**, ~3,000 local suppliers, ~1,500 exporting companies across 4,000+ products into 120 markets
- Operates **"Acá Global"** — a credit-bridge program with 50 private funds in network. Routes startups through traditional banking → Sistema Banca de Desarrollo (convertible notes up to **$500K**) → external private funds
- Costa Rica hosts **~700 multinationals** (J&J, Boston Scientific, Amazon, Intel) clustered in Alajuela, Grecia, and Cartago — "a 20-kilometer radius" enabling fast supplier coinnovation
- Opened a **Silicon Valley OPI office in February 2026** (director: Carolina Sánchez) to attract startups to Costa Rica and recruit foreign capital

### Regulatory Observations

- Costa Rican banking is structured for **consumer banking, not enterprise**. Founders typically can't access formal credit until ~8 years post-formation
- Banking regulation does **not differentiate by ticket size** — same compliance burden applies to a $100K loan as to a $5M loan, blocking smaller companies and R&D-heavy projects
- **PROCOMER cannot directly invest by law** — it can only issue non-reimbursable grants up to $40K and pay for technical assistance, training, and certifications
- No "startup law" or differentiated regulatory regime exists in Costa Rica today

### Recommendations & Insights

- **Cross-administration policy continuity** is the single largest reason PROCOMER outperforms peer agencies. Foreign-trade strategy survives 4-year political cycles; new governments ask "what are you doing and how do I support it?" rather than reset direction
- **Public-private dual perspective**: PROCOMER acts as both regulator (zona franca governance, ventanilla única integrating 80+ institutions) and market-facing partner — letting it translate private-sector pain points into policy
- **Ireland is the relevant peer**, not Singapore: similar small-country trajectory, but Ireland invested heavily in R&D in the early 2000s — Costa Rica is now attempting that pivot
- **Korea model for human capital**: pair science/engineering credentials with MBA training to bridge technical-to-commercial gap. Costa Rica has strong engineers but weak commercialization skills
