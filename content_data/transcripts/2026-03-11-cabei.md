## Executive Summary
Jorge Daneri, a senior multilateral finance executive with deep experience at CABEI and IFAD, offered a structured assessment of Central America's venture capital ecosystem and specific recommendations for how MDBs like CAF should engage. He tiered the region's readiness: Costa Rica and Panama are most developed; Guatemala and El Salvador resemble Chile or Mexico a decade ago; Honduras and Nicaragua require foundational work. He was unambiguous that DFI engagement should go beyond pure financial returns — linking VC to SDG goals like employment creation and financial inclusion — and that current return benchmarks like FIDE's 3.5% are too low to attract institutional capital.

Daneri's central structural recommendation is a regional fund-of-funds encompassing multiple countries simultaneously, which would unlock larger ticket investors ($50M+) rather than the fragmented $10M commitments that single-country vehicles attract. He identified a critical bottleneck: the scarcity of locally trained, specialized general partners, noting that BID Invest is already developing GP training programs in the Caribbean as a model to replicate. For portfolio companies, he stressed that regional ambition must begin at inception — citing Hogares.com's expansion from El Salvador into Honduras as a proof of concept.

## Stakeholder Background
Jorge Daneri retired from CABEI in 2022 after a career focused on financial markets and capital investments, including management of bilateral relationships with GCF, KFW, and the Korea Trust Fund — the latter a $50M program he describes as successful. He then served 2.5 years as VP Finance at IFAD, the UN agriculture and rural poverty fund based in Rome, where he developed new financial instruments including a partial credit guarantee platform and a climate bond taxonomy for agriculture in partnership with the Climate Bond Initiative. At the time of the interview he was transitioning into a new permanent role in investment fund structuring, making his perspective both institutionally grounded and practically current.

## Key Findings

### Central America Has Three Distinct VC Readiness Tiers
Daneri segments the region into three tiers: Costa Rica and Panama are the most developed markets; Guatemala and El Salvador are roughly comparable to where Chile or Mexico stood a decade ago; Honduras and Nicaragua require substantial foundational development before institutional VC is viable. This tiering has direct implications for sequencing — a one-size-fits-all regional strategy will misfire.

### CAF Is Already Deploying at Scale in the Region, Creating an Entry Point
CAF is allocating $400–500 million annually to Honduras alone and is actively expanding into Guatemala, having already established a presence in El Salvador. The institution has set a 70% growth target over five years in the region. This aggressive deployment posture means CAF has both the capital footprint and the relationship infrastructure to anchor a regional VC initiative without starting from zero.

### A Regional Fund-of-Funds Is the Preferred Structure for Attracting Institutional Capital
Single-country vehicles cap ticket sizes at roughly $10 million, whereas a multi-country fund-of-funds can attract investors willing to commit $50 million or more. Daneri recommends a structure that pools Honduras, Guatemala, Costa Rica, El Salvador, and the Dominican Republic, with potential co-anchor partners including CABEI, the Caribbean Development Bank, Fonplata, and CAF itself. Scale is the precondition for institutional credibility.

### MDBs Must Frame VC as a Development Tool, Not Just a Financial Product
Daneri argues that multilateral development banks justify VC engagement most compellingly when it is explicitly linked to SDG outcomes — employment creation, financial inclusion, rural access. He flagged FIDE's reported 3.5% return as illustrative of the problem: returns that low neither attract private capital nor demonstrate the asset class's potential, eroding the political and institutional case for continued MDB involvement.

### Local GP Capacity Is the Critical Bottleneck — and BID Invest Has a Model Worth Replicating
The region lacks specialized general partners trained in venture capital; most existing fund managers come from traditional private equity or credit backgrounds. BID Invest is already running GP training programs in the Caribbean, which Daneri cited as the most concrete model available for replication in Central America. Without deliberate capacity-building, even well-structured funds will underperform due to execution gaps at the GP level.

### Startups Must Be Structured for Investors and Built for Regional Scale from Day One
A persistent structural problem is that Central American startups are not properly organized for external investment — weak governance, unclear cap tables, no investor-ready documentation. Daneri also stressed that regional ambition cannot be an afterthought: companies should target Central America plus Panama plus the Dominican Republic from inception. He cited Hogares.com's expansion from El Salvador into Honduras as evidence that cross-border scaling is achievable.

## Regulatory & Structural Observations
Daneri did not focus extensively on specific national regulatory frameworks, but he highlighted structural issues at the fund level: the mismatch between MDB concessional return expectations and market-rate VC requirements. The 3.5% return associated with FIDE was cited as a benchmark that needs to be revisited — it is insufficient to attract private limited partners and does not reflect the risk-adjusted opportunity in the asset class. He implicitly recommended that MDB participation be structured as first-loss or subordinated capital to make the overall fund economics attractive to commercial co-investors, consistent with standard DFI blended finance practice.

## Recommendations & Insights
- **Structure a regional fund-of-funds**, not a country-specific vehicle, to access $50M+ institutional tickets and share due-diligence and governance costs across markets.
- **Anchor the narrative in development outcomes** (job creation, financial inclusion, SDG alignment) to justify MDB participation and mobilize concessional capital alongside commercial returns.
- **Invest in GP training before or alongside fund deployment** — replicate or partner with BID Invest's Caribbean GP development program for Central America.
- **Require portfolio companies to demonstrate regional scalability** as a condition of investment, targeting the combined Central America + Panama + Dominican Republic market from the outset.
- **Revisit return benchmarks** to ensure they are high enough to attract co-investors; concessional DFI capital should be used to de-risk, not to subsidize low returns that drive away private capital.
- **Engage CABEI, CDB, and Fonplata** as potential co-anchor LPs in a regional structure, leveraging existing bilateral relationships Daneri himself built during his CABEI tenure.
