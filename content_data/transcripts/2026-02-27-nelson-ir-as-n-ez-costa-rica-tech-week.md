## Executive Summary

Nelson Irías Núñez is one of Central America's most experienced ecosystem builders, and his core message is blunt: the region is fifteen to twenty years behind peers like Ecuador or Peru, and the immaturity runs in both directions — founders do not understand investment, and many self-described VCs are not practicing venture capital at all. He documents specific cases of local funds extracting 20% equity for $20,000 tickets, and another fund demanding real estate collateral on a $120,000 check — practices that permanently damage cap tables, repel institutional investors, and burn promising companies before they reach scale.

His programmatic vision to fix this is layered and sequenced: begin with events and physical space to build founder density (grounded in IDB research on "dynamic entrepreneurship"), then layer in grants for MVP-stage companies (not institutional capital), then introduce structured angel networks, and only then connect investment-ready startups to institutional funds. He took six Costa Rican startups to Mexico Tech Week last year; five discovered they were not ready, which he treats as a success — a diagnostic that resets expectations.

For CAF specifically, Nelson recommends a founder-visa model inspired by Chile's Startup Chile (run by Corfo), positioning Costa Rica as a regional hub, and calls out the newly decreed Costa Rican public innovation agency — the Innovation Promoter of Costa Rica — as an entry point for partnership. He also designed El Salvador's national incubator program, Incubatech, and can open doors to Ministries of Economy across the subregion.

## Stakeholder Background

Nelson Irías Núñez has sixteen years of ecosystem-building experience across Latin America, having lived and worked in Brazil, Colombia, Mexico, Honduras, and Costa Rica. He co-founded a company in Honduras thirteen years ago and worked with that country's government-IDB startup program in its earliest iteration. His most recent regional role was with TechStars and TxSource, running an IDB-funded investment-readiness program for Latin American founders out of the New York office. One year ago he returned to Costa Rica after a decade abroad and founded Costa Rica Tech Week, positioning it as the nucleus of Central America's first innovation hub. He currently serves as the Central American representative director for the Latin American Startup Alliance and works directly with the governments of Costa Rica, Honduras, and El Salvador.

## Key Findings

### Central America's Investor Class Is Practicing a Counterfeit Version of Venture Capital
Local funds are structuring deals that mimic VC language while violating its core logic. One named Costa Rican fund, Parque Tec — acquired by Mexican operator Marcus Dantus — routinely takes 20% equity for tickets of $20,000–$40,000, implying a $100,000–$200,000 valuation. A second fund requires a residential property as collateral on checks of $120,000. Both practices destroy cap tables, signal founder naivety to outside investors, and make follow-on institutional investment nearly impossible. A medical-device AI startup called iNova lost its entire investor pipeline at Mexican pitch events solely because of a contaminated cap table from an early Parque Tec deal.

### A Forty-Five-Person Mapping Exercise Revealed a Region-Wide Investment Literacy Gap
In January of last year, Nelson convened forty-five Central American ecosystem actors — funds, incubators, and foundations — in Quito, Ecuador for a comprehensive diagnostic that fed into a 220-page report for the IDB. The central finding: neither founders nor investors share a coherent framework. Institutional funds are writing checks into companies generating $1,000/month in revenue, while Costa Rica's state development banking system has deployed over $50 million in the past decade into non-scalable microenterprises — jam sellers, flower vendors — rather than technology startups. The report is not public but Nelson offered to provide a summary.

### Costa Rica Sent Six Startups to Mexico; Five Discovered They Were Not Ready
Last year, Nelson brought six Costa Rican startups to Mexico Tech Week for pitches with institutional investors and angels. Only one continued meaningful conversations — and none closed investment. Critically, five of the six founders arrived believing they were investment-ready because they had already received funding from Central American funds; exposure to Mexican investors revealed those rounds had been premature. Nelson frames this as a useful diagnostic: the region needs exposure events to calibrate founder expectations against international standards before pursuing institutional capital.

### The IDB's "Dynamic Entrepreneurship" Framework Identifies Two Non-Negotiable Infrastructure Elements
Nelson references two IDB working papers on dynamic entrepreneurship that identify physical co-location space and recurring event cadence as the foundational prerequisites for ecosystem development — neither of which currently exists at adequate scale in Central America. His model calls for bi-weekly events, drawing founders from outside major cities, combined with a dedicated physical hub. He observed both elements operating in Colombia (Ruta N in Medellín) and argues that if Colombia and Costa Rica share cultural and structural similarities, the model is directly transferable.

### The Correct Financing Sequence Is Grants → Angels → Institutionals — Not VC First
Nelson argues forcefully that institutional venture capital should not enter the ecosystem until a startup is generating at least $12,000/month (~$144,000/year) in revenue. Below that threshold, the appropriate instrument is non-dilutive public grants of $10,000–$20,000 for prototyping and MVP validation. Once companies hit consistent revenue milestones, they should be introduced to a structured angel investor network — a layer that does not yet exist in the region — before any institutional fund engagement. The absence of this sequencing is the root cause of cap table contamination and founder burnout.

### Costa Rica Has Decreed a Public Innovation Agency That Does Not Yet Operate
Three years ago, Costa Rica established by presidential decree the Innovation Promoter of Costa Rica, the country's equivalent of Chile's Corfo or Uruguay's National Innovation Agency. The institution legally exists but is not yet operational. Nelson describes it as nascent and somewhat uncertain of its own mandate, but identifies it as the correct government entry point for CAF and offered to make introductions. This represents a structurally important moment: an institution that can be shaped before it hardens into bureaucratic form.

### Nelson Designed El Salvador's National Incubator and Can Open Ministerial Doors Across the Subregion
Nelson recently delivered the full program design for Incubatech, El Salvador's national incubation program, to the country's Ministry of Economy. He has active ministerial relationships in El Salvador, Honduras, and Costa Rica, and is connected to the Latin American Startup Alliance leadership (Junior, the alliance president, was specifically recommended as a follow-up contact). He positioned himself as a one-degree-of-separation node for any actor CAF needs to reach in the Central American public or ecosystem sector.

## Regulatory & Structural Observations

Costa Rica's development banking system — a state entity that provides grants and subsidized loans to entrepreneurs — has misallocated capital at scale: over $50 million distributed in ten years went predominantly to non-scalable, non-technology businesses. This is a structural failure of definitional criteria, not program design alone; the system lacks a filter distinguishing scalable startups from lifestyle microenterprises. The newly decreed Innovation Promoter of Costa Rica could theoretically provide that filter, but has not yet operationalized any programs. Nelson also flagged that a founder-visa mechanism — analogous to Chile's Startup Chile program administered by Corfo — does not exist in Central America and would require government-level policy intervention, ideally with multilateral backing, to create.

## Recommendations & Insights

Nelson's prescription for CAF in Central America is a sequenced, infrastructure-first program rather than direct fund investment. His specific wish list:

1. **Establish a founder visa and regional hub in Costa Rica**, modeled on Startup Chile, drawing founders from Guatemala, El Salvador, and Honduras to a single high-density environment with events, investor access, and mentorship.
2. **Create a grant facility** of $10,000–$20,000 per company for pre-revenue prototyping and MVP development — not equity investment. This insulates early-stage companies from predatory local capital.
3. **Build a structured angel investor network** across the region. Many high-net-worth individuals have $200,000 in idle savings and could contribute $20,000 to a startup portfolio but lack education, deal flow, or a network structure to do so.
4. **Partner with universities** to generate the earliest pipeline of potential founders through awareness and sensitization programs before incubation even begins.
5. **Engage the Innovation Promoter of Costa Rica** immediately, while it is still formative, to shape its mandate toward scalable tech startups rather than microenterprise.
6. **Use cross-border events** — connecting Costa Rica to networks like Emprelatam, Corvo in Chile, and Ruta N in Colombia — as the mechanism to raise quality standards and expose founders to international benchmarks.
