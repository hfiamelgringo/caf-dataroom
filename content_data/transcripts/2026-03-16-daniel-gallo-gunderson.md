## Executive Summary

Daniel Gallo, who leads Gunderson Dettmer's Latin American practice from Silicon Valley, offers a practitioner's view of the legal and regulatory friction that limits venture capital activity in Central America and the Dominican Republic. His core argument is that legal certainty and trained regulators matter more than elaborate new fund structures: countries that cannot enforce the laws they already have offer little comfort to foreign investors, regardless of what is written on the books. Brazil leads the region on this front; among Spanish-speaking markets, Chile and Mexico are the clearest benchmarks.

On fund structures, Gallo is skeptical that creating local limited partnership vehicles in Honduras, Guatemala, Costa Rica, or the Dominican Republic would meaningfully attract VC capital — international investors simply do not want to hold local entities, preferring Cayman or, increasingly, US Delaware structures. He argues that bilateral tax treaties would do more practical good than local fund formation laws, pointing to the Mexico-Canada treaty as a model that makes Canadian limited partnerships attractive vehicles for regional investing.

For CAF and multilateral partners, Gallo's most actionable insight is that investors are willing to provide ultimate beneficial ownership disclosure to development finance institutions — because they trust multilateral data governance — but refuse to hand the same information to national governments, citing political instability, data-leak risk, and fear of tax harassment. This asymmetry is a structural opportunity for CAF to position itself as a trusted intermediary in the ecosystem.

## Stakeholder Background

Daniel Gallo is a Mexican-born attorney who began his career in M&A and private equity in Mexico City after graduating from law school in 2011. He became involved in early VC transactions in Mexico starting around 2013–2014, helping Stanford and Harvard alumni introduce venture capital to the market. After postgraduate study in the United States, he joined Latham & Watkins' project finance and M&A practice covering Latin America, then moved to Gunderson Dettmer five years ago to focus on high-growth tech transactions. Gunderson Dettmer has ranked as the most active law firm in US venture and startup transactions for more than ten consecutive years and currently serves eight or nine unicorns in Latin America. Gallo's perspective is grounded in day-to-day deal execution across the region rather than policy theory.

## Key Findings

### Legal Certainty and Regulator Training Are the Foundational Prerequisites — Not New Laws

The single most important reform governments can make is establishing clear, predictable rules of the game, analogous to Mexico's and Brazil's fintech laws. But certainty on paper is insufficient without trained regulators: even in more advanced markets like Mexico and Chile, regulators default to conservative interpretations that go beyond what the law actually requires, creating unpredictable friction. Gallo treats these two reforms as inseparable — one without the other provides little real benefit to investors.

### Investors Trust Multilaterals With Beneficial Ownership Data, But Not National Governments

A critical distinction shapes LP behavior: funds generally accept ultimate beneficial ownership disclosure requirements from multilateral institutions like CAF or the IDB because they trust these bodies' data security and political neutrality. They refuse equivalent disclosure to national governments, fearing leaks, political weaponization by changing administrations, and what Gallo calls "tax terrorism" — authorities using disclosed wealth information to pursue investors. This trust asymmetry is consistent across politically stable and unstable markets alike.

### Local Fund Structures Add Little Value — Tax Treaties Are the More Efficient Lever

Gallo dismisses the idea that creating GP/LP fund vehicles in Central American or Caribbean jurisdictions would attract meaningful VC capital, rating the impact close to zero. Investors do not want to hold local entities and will not change that preference based on new domestic legal forms. By contrast, the Mexico-Canada bilateral tax treaty demonstrates that favorable tax treatment upon exit is a genuine motivator: it makes Canadian limited partnerships the preferred vehicle for cross-border investing into Mexico. He recommends that CAF push target countries toward similar bilateral tax treaties rather than domestic fund formation reform.

### Cayman Structures Remain Dominant, but US Delaware Incorporation Is Rising

The Cayman holding company structure — the "Cayman sandwich" — remains the standard vehicle for VC investment across Latin America, used even in Brazil where local entities might theoretically suffice. UK holding companies serve as an alternative when Cayman is unavailable. Notably, Gallo observes a recent shift: over the past year or so, founders have begun incorporating directly as US corporations at a rate that rivals or exceeds Cayman structures, reflecting founders' increasingly global ambitions and investors' comfort with US legal frameworks.

### Employee Stock Options Should Be Left Largely Unregulated

Most Latin American jurisdictions — including Honduras, Costa Rica, and peers in the region — do not require options to be granted at fair market value, allowing companies to issue options at one cent. This creates a meaningful wealth-building mechanism for local employees by minimizing the exercise price. Gallo warns against tightening option regulations: introducing fair-market-value requirements or other rules would shift the benefit from employees to tax authorities and undermine one of the few compensation tools available to attract talent in these markets.

### Chile's Corfo Program Is the Most Replicable Model for Central America

Chile's Corfo program — in which the government provides capital to fund managers who then deploy it into local startups — is Gallo's recommended model for Central America. He describes it as a genuine success in its initial phase, significantly expanding the Chilean startup ecosystem. Its main limitation emerged when portfolio companies needed to grow regionally: the local-entity requirement forced cumbersome approval processes that slowed internationalization. A Central American adaptation of Corfo that builds in flexibility for holding company restructuring from the outset could avoid this bottleneck.

### Brazil Leads, Chile and Mexico Follow — but Lessons Transfer Imperfectly

Brazil is the clear regional leader on legal certainty, regulatory transparency, and VC ecosystem maturity. However, its Portuguese-language legal tradition, market size, and institutional density make direct replication difficult in Spanish-speaking markets. Chile and Mexico are the most useful benchmarks for the four target countries, with each offering distinct lessons: Chile on government-backed fund programs, Mexico on tax treaty strategy and fintech regulation. Gallo notes his team includes attorneys from Colombia, Argentina, Uruguay, Chile, and Mexico — broad coverage but no Central American specialist, a gap he acknowledges.

### Standardization of Operational Setup Across Jurisdictions Is Valuable but Stalled Without Multilateral Support

A company called Latitude attempted to standardize company formation and legal infrastructure across Brazil, Chile, Uruguay, Argentina, Mexico, and Colombia, and made meaningful progress before abandoning the effort due to insufficient transaction volume. Gallo sees this as a clear case where multilateral backing — financial and political — could have made the difference. The primary complexity in each jurisdiction is tax-driven rather than corporate-law-driven, meaning any standardization effort must engage tax authorities, not just company registries.

## Regulatory & Structural Observations

The beneficial ownership disclosure issue cuts across all four target countries. Gallo's framing — that investors tolerate multilateral disclosure requirements but resist government requirements — suggests a structural role for CAF as a disclosure intermediary or data trustee, rather than expecting national regulators to build equivalent trust independently.

On entity types, Gallo notes that most Latin American corporate laws could be amended to accommodate preferred and common shares, but argues this is largely moot because VC investors will not invest directly into local entities regardless. The legal infrastructure question for operating subsidiaries is distinct from the holding structure question, and reforms to the former do not resolve the latter.

The Corfo precedent introduces a regulatory complication worth noting: when the Chilean government funded managers investing in local startups, exit and internationalization paths were blocked by approval requirements that the program designers had not anticipated. Any CAF-sponsored program modeled on Corfo should be designed from the outset to permit holding company migration without triggering approval bottlenecks.

## Recommendations & Insights

Gallo's practical recommendations for CAF and governments in the target countries, synthesized from the conversation:

1. **Prioritize regulator training alongside legislative reform.** New laws without trained, market-literate regulators produce no improvement in investor confidence.
2. **Pursue bilateral tax treaties aggressively.** The Mexico-Canada model demonstrates that treaty-based tax benefits on exit are more effective at attracting international capital than creating domestic fund vehicles.
3. **Do not regulate employee stock options further.** The current low-exercise-price environment is a competitive advantage for local talent retention; additional regulation would erode it.
4. **Design any Corfo-like program with built-in internationalization flexibility.** Fund programs that trap companies in local entity structures will hit a growth ceiling.
5. **Leverage CAF's position as a trusted data steward.** Since LPs will disclose beneficial ownership to multilaterals but not to governments, CAF can serve as a credible intermediary that lowers the compliance burden on funds while satisfying due diligence requirements.
6. **Engage Gunderson Dettmer on regulatory position papers.** Gallo expressed clear interest in collaborating on model regulatory frameworks for Central America, noting his team's coverage of Colombia, Argentina, Uruguay, Chile, and Mexico provides substantial regional expertise even without a Central America specialist on staff.
