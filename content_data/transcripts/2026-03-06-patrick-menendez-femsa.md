## Executive Summary

Patrick Menendez spent nearly a decade building FEMSA Ventures, one of Latin America's most prominent corporate venture capital vehicles, first at its predecessor Varys/Cometa and then leading direct and fund-of-fund investing at FEMSA. His core message is that FEMSA Ventures got the structural model largely right — operating as a finance-first fund, building an internal "platform" team to connect portfolio companies to FEMSA's business units, and deliberately limiting fund size — but was ultimately undone by a CFO change and shifting corporate priorities, not by poor investment performance. The fund is shutting down by end of March 2026.

Beyond FEMSA's story, Menendez offers a sharp diagnosis of the broader LatAm VC market: Spanish-speaking Latin America cannot responsibly support $100M+ fund sizes because deal flow and exit capacity simply do not exist at scale. Mexico in particular has no meaningful new deal flow, no functioning secondary market, and a cohort of managers raising oversized funds who will never return them. Brazil is the only market where professional firm-building is happening; everywhere else, managers are running lifestyle boutiques optimized for management fees rather than returns. For CAF, the implication is clear: multilateral capital should be pushing managers toward smaller, disciplined fund sizes and rotating toward new managers rather than recycling LP commitments to the usual suspects.

## Stakeholder Background

Patrick Menendez joined what was then Varys Capital in early 2018, which later evolved into Cometa. He departed after roughly three years and joined FEMSA Ventures, where he led both the fund-of-funds strategy and direct startup investing. He has deep relationships across the LatAm VC ecosystem — including time at Bain & Company's Mexico City office alongside the founding team of Bimbo Ventures — and has been a consistent public commentator on LatAm VC market structure. His perspective is unusually candid precisely because he is exiting the industry and has no fund currently in market.

## Key Findings

### FEMSA Ventures is shutting down — a finance-first CVC model killed by a CFO change, not poor returns
FEMSA Ventures, a 2019-vintage fund with roughly $75M under management, is closing by end of March 2026 following a corporate restructuring at FEMSA. Menendez was explicit that performance was not the cause: the fund's returns were above the LatAm average, with at least one investment in a pricing-and-promotions startup measurably driving OXXO beer sales by 1%, a return he argues paid for the entire program. The trigger was a new CFO with different priorities — illustrating how CVC programs are existentially dependent on individual executive champions.

### The "finance-first" CVC model outperforms strategic-first models — but requires board-level patience
FEMSA Ventures deliberately structured itself as a finance-first fund rather than an M&A pipeline tool, contrasting sharply with Bimbo Ventures, which Menendez describes as treating CVC as a pre-acquisition screening mechanism indifferent to financial returns. FEMSA required portfolio companies to meet genuine investment standards. However, maintaining this model inside a corporation demands unusually long time horizons: internal operators resent a "hoodie guy" controlling $100M while business units face budget pressure, and the natural four-year corporate planning cycle is fundamentally misaligned with a 10–14 year venture cycle.

### The internal "platform" team was FEMSA Ventures' most underrated structural advantage
FEMSA Ventures employed a dedicated two-person platform team whose sole job was mapping the needs of FEMSA's hundreds of business-unit touch points across geographies and business lines. When an investment team identified a supply-chain startup, the platform team could instantly route it to the right internal counterpart — whether convenience stores in Switzerland, drugstores in Chile, or gas stations in Mexico. A practical result: NDAs that normally took six months could be executed in three weeks, dramatically accelerating commercial pilots.

### The right CVC structure is a spinoff or major-LP model — not a captive internal fund
Based on his experience, Menendez argues that successful CVCs almost always eventually spin out of the corporate structure. He cites Samsung Ventures-style spinoffs as the durable model. His prescription for large conglomerates across Latin America: become a significant LP in an independent fund, negotiate real collaboration rights, and stop trying to run venture investing as an internal department. Keeping it internal creates unresolvable governance friction and makes fund sizing politically difficult — FEMSA's board was unwilling to approve new fund vehicles every two to three years, forcing a single oversized vehicle.

### Spanish-speaking Latin America cannot responsibly support $100M+ VC funds — the math simply does not work
Menendez argues there is no ethical path to deploying a $100M fund in Spanish-speaking LatAm and returning it. The region produces approximately one breakout company per decade — a Mercado Libre, a Nubank, occasionally a Despegar — and there is no realistic path to aggregating $500M–$3B in portfolio value from any single vintage. FEMSA Ventures refused to take a single Mexican fund to investment committee in the past three years because none could justify their target fund size. His standing offer to Mexico's major managers: come back at $80M with a hard cap of $80M and he would commit immediately.

### Mexico's VC ecosystem has collapsed into a fee-extraction culture with no deal flow
Mexican VC managers are raising oversized funds ($120M–$150M), running lean four-to-five-person teams with high turnover, and not investing in talent development or infrastructure — optimizing for management fees over returns. Meanwhile, Mexican-founded startups have largely disappeared from the deal flow; Menendez noted that a Colombian manager he met was baffled by the absence of Mexican founders in the market. The contrast with Brazil is stark: Brazilian funds recruit former central bank presidents, build technical due diligence capabilities, and grow fund size incrementally across vintages (e.g., $20M → $30M → $45M).

### IDB/IFC LP processes are a drag on emerging managers and systematically recycle capital to the wrong funds
While the IDB was an investor in Cometa, Menendez describes the LP experience as grueling — multi-month or multi-year commitment timelines, repetitive documentation requests, and an ultimately fair question of whether a $2M ticket is worth the process burden. More damaging, he observes that multilaterals tend to re-commit to familiar managers regardless of performance, citing a case where a former IDB executive champions a manager whose fund numbers Menendez describes as clearly underperforming. This incumbency bias crowds out new managers and sends misleading quality signals to the market.

## Regulatory & Structural Observations

No formal regulatory discussion took place. The structural observation most relevant to CAF is the fund-size constraint: the absence of a functioning secondary market in Spanish-speaking LatAm means that funds that over-raise become permanently illiquid. Menendez described funds sitting on assets like Clip and Kueski for twelve to fourteen years, facing single-digit IRRs simply because there is no secondary buyer and no realistic exit path until a rare large liquidity event occurs. A secondary market infrastructure — or a London-listed public market pathway for $700M–$1.5B companies — was floated as a potential structural fix.

## Recommendations & Insights

- **Conglomerates should be LPs, not operators.** Rather than building internal CVC teams, Latin American corporates should take anchor LP positions in independent funds with contractual collaboration rights baked into the LPA. This preserves investment discipline, removes governance friction, and allows fund managers to make decisions without internal political interference.
- **Multilaterals must enforce fund-size discipline.** CAF and the IDB should condition LP commitments on rational fund sizing relative to the addressable market. A $150M fund in Mexico is not a serious investment vehicle; it is a fee vehicle. Multilaterals that keep funding these vehicles are actively distorting the market.
- **Rotate capital to new managers.** Established funds at fund three or four should be expected to raise from the private market. Multilateral anchor LP capital should be explicitly reserved for fund one and two managers to build the next generation of LatAm VC firms.
- **Smaller, more frequent fund cycles.** A $30M fund every two to three years generates better market learning, more manageable governance, and more honest return profiles than a single $75M–$100M vehicle.
- **Build exit infrastructure before deploying more capital.** The single most binding constraint in LatAm VC is exits, not deal flow or capital. Any CAF strategy that deploys LP capital without a parallel initiative on public markets, secondary markets, or cross-border M&A facilitation is incomplete.
