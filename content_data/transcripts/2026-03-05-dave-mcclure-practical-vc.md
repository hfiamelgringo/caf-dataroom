## Executive Summary
Dave McClure made a case for a structural rethinking of how development banks like CAF deploy venture capital in Latin America. Rather than concentrating capital in large, late-stage vehicles, he argued for a fund-of-funds approach anchored in small checks to small funds at seed and Series A — distributing risk, broadening wealth creation, and building a genuine top-of-funnel ecosystem. He warned that the SoftBank era (2017–2019) over-capitalized the market and left many strong companies stranded without liquidity options, creating a gap that secondary market mechanisms could fill.

McClure's most actionable recommendations centered on three levers: (1) tax policy that credits losses and ties benefits to domestic reinvestment; (2) government-sponsored or private secondary market vehicles that provide partial liquidity to angels and seed funds before IPO timelines; and (3) development banks behaving like faster, lighter LPs rather than bureaucratic gatekeepers. He flagged that CAF's current processes can take two to three years to deploy even a $10M fund commitment — a pace that is incompatible with early-stage venture dynamics. His current private secondary strategy, a $10M test portfolio targeting $100M+ revenue companies via asset swaps, illustrates what nimble capital can look like in practice.

## Stakeholder Background
Dave McClure is the founder of Practical VC and was previously the founding partner of 500 Startups, one of the world's most prolific early-stage accelerator-funds. He has made hundreds of seed investments across emerging markets and has long advocated for democratized, high-volume, small-check venture models. His current work focuses on secondary market liquidity in Latin American venture, giving him a distinctive vantage point on both the ecosystem's structural gaps and the mechanics of recycling early-investor capital.

## Key Findings

### Fund-of-Funds at Scale Is the Right Architecture for LatAm Venture
Rather than deploying billions through a single large vehicle, McClure recommended distributing capital through many smaller funds via a fund-of-funds structure. This approach avoids concentration in elite networks, spreads wealth creation more broadly, and allows for experimentation at lower downside risk. Targeting seed and Series A — rather than companies already at $10–30M in revenue — is essential to developing genuine top-of-funnel deal flow across the region.

### Tax Credits on Losses Are the Single Most Powerful Ecosystem Policy Tool
McClure identified loss tax credits for angel investors and early-stage funds as the highest-leverage policy intervention available. The credits should be conditioned on domestic reinvestment — capital only receives the benefit if it stays in the local market — preventing exits from simply enriching investors who relocate to Miami rather than recycling gains into the local ecosystem. Multiple policy designs are available depending on each country's government structure.

### Secondary Market Liquidity Is the Missing Infrastructure in LatAm Venture
With IPO timelines extending well beyond a decade, angels and seed funds have no practical exit path. McClure proposed a government-sponsored entity that provides 20–50% liquidity to early investors at Series B/C market pricing, allowing exits within a 5–10 year window. He noted there are very few players currently offering secondary liquidity in Latin American venture — making this a largely uncontested and high-impact space for a development bank to enter.

### McClure's Own Asset-Swap Model Demonstrates Practical Secondary Mechanics
His current secondary strategy involves a $10M test portfolio across 8–10 companies generating $100M or more in revenue. He buys $1–2M positions from existing investors, employees, and angels, offering an asset-swap structure: $1 of equity converts to $0.20 in cash plus $0.80 in a diversified portfolio. This gives sellers partial liquidity while preserving upside exposure, and gives the fund access to late-stage companies at favorable entry points.

### SoftBank's 2017–2019 Surge Left a Distorted Market Still Seeking Resolution
SoftBank's excessive capital deployment between 2017 and 2019 over-funded many strong Latin American companies at inflated valuations. Those companies are now mature but stuck — too big for traditional venture follow-on, not yet ready for public markets, and held by early investors who need liquidity. This distortion created precisely the secondary opportunity McClure is targeting and highlights why capital discipline and phased deployment matter more than headline fund size.

### Startup Chile Proved Ecosystem Catalysts Work Even With Modest Capital
McClure described Startup Chile as having "punched above its weight" — generating outsized ecosystem impact despite relatively light actual capital deployment. He also noted that Chilean fund-of-funds vehicles are now hitting their 10-year repayment walls, creating a fresh wave of secondary market opportunities as LPs need distributions. Both dynamics — the program's legacy and the current liquidity pressure — are directly relevant for CAF's regional strategy design.

### Development Banks Are Too Slow and Too Heavy to Be Effective Early-Stage LPs
McClure was direct that CAF and peer institutions need to become better LPs by moving faster and writing smaller checks. Current processes take two to three years to deploy commitments as small as $10M, and heavy reporting requirements create disproportionate friction for small fund managers. He acknowledged a positive signal — CAF's new $20M impact fund indicates movement toward fund-of-funds thinking — but argued the institutional pace and compliance burden must change for the model to work.

## Regulatory & Structural Observations
McClure did not address specific national regulatory frameworks in detail, but framed tax policy as a structural design choice with multiple implementation pathways depending on each country's government architecture. His core principle: tax incentives for venture should be conditioned on domestic capital recycling, not simply on the act of investment. Without that reinvestment tether, tax benefits subsidize capital flight rather than ecosystem building. He also implicitly flagged that the absence of secondary market infrastructure is itself a structural regulatory gap — one that a government-sponsored liquidity vehicle could fill without requiring private market actors to solve it first.

## Recommendations & Insights
- **Restructure deployment as a fund-of-funds:** Distribute capital across many small funds at seed and Series A rather than concentrating in large late-stage vehicles.
- **Implement loss tax credits tied to domestic reinvestment:** Design policy so benefits are conditional on capital staying in-market, with reinvestment triggers at exit.
- **Create a government-sponsored secondary liquidity facility:** Target 20–50% liquidity for early investors at Series B/C pricing to normalize 5–10 year venture timelines.
- **Reduce LP bureaucracy dramatically:** Streamline approval and reporting processes so that $10M fund commitments do not take two to three years to close.
- **Study the Chilean fund-of-funds repayment cycle:** The current 10-year wall creates actionable secondary opportunities that a regional vehicle could capture now.
- **Use McClure's asset-swap model as a template:** The $1 equity → $0.20 cash + $0.80 diversified portfolio structure is replicable at institutional scale and addresses both liquidity and concentration risk simultaneously.
