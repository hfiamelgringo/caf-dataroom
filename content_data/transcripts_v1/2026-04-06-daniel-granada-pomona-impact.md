## Executive Summary

Daniel Granada, a Honduran-born fund manager at Pomona Impact, offers a frank, ground-level assessment of Central America's investment ecosystem. His core argument is that the region is showing early but fragile signs of momentum — driven by a handful of engaged families and the rise of the Caricaco Summit as a serious investor convening — but remains structurally hobbled by regulatory fragmentation across borders, a near-complete absence of viable exit pathways, and a local capital market where wealthy families rationally prefer to send money offshore rather than back regional funds. Pomona's own experience trying to raise from Central American families for Fund II was instructive: two years of effort across Panama, Guatemala, and El Salvador yielded roughly $400,000 out of a $33 million raise.

For CAF, Granada's prescription is to act as a capital multiplier for first-time fund managers — a two-to-one or three-to-one matching mechanism that reduces first-close risk and gives nascent teams what he calls "a hunting license." He also identifies the pipeline problem as equally important: the companies that could absorb $2–6 million debt tickets often don't know the funds exist, and reaching them requires active outreach rather than ecosystem events. Ecosystem-building investment readiness programs, like those run by the Pomona Impact Foundation in agro-processing and green tech, are productive but must be paired with fund formation support to complete the capital stack.

## Stakeholder Background

Daniel Granada is a Honduran national who spent eight years at the Inter-American Development Bank (IDB) deploying $5–20 million tickets into Central American companies before joining Pomona Impact, a US-based not-for-profit impact fund focused on scale-ups across Latin America. Pomona's current fund (Fund II) closed at $33 million and deploys 80% debt / 20% equity in agribusiness, basic services, and digital economy sectors, with average ticket sizes of approximately $2.1–2.2 million per company. His IDB background and regional family ties give him an unusually candid vantage point on both DFI strategy and local capital dynamics.

## Key Findings

### Finding 1: The Caricaco Summit has replaced FLACSO-style events as the region's premier investor convening
The annual Caricaco Summit, organized by the Kidos family's El Trueno family office, now attracts global fund managers with genuine Latin America investment mandates — something the older FLACSO Central America event has failed to do as it has shifted toward NGO and impact-first audiences. Granada calls it "the best investment event in the region by far" and recommends it as the single most efficient venue for mapping who is actively deploying capital in Central America. Andres Lang, head of El Trueno, was flagged as a key introductory contact.

### Finding 2: Regulatory fragmentation makes cross-border investing structurally expensive and deal-by-deal
Expanding a fintech from El Salvador — which has purpose-built fintech legislation — into Guatemala, which has none, effectively requires building a second company from scratch. Acqui-hiring a dormant local licensed entity is often the fastest workaround, but it is costly and idiosyncratic. Granada notes that holding-company structures in Caribbean or US jurisdictions do not resolve the underlying problem of capital flow, collateral assignment, and licensing requirements, which must be negotiated country by country on every deal.

### Finding 3: Local family office fundraising yields negligible capital for regional funds
Pomona spent two years organizing dinners, roadshows, and third-party validation events across Panama, Guatemala, and El Salvador to raise from regional families for Fund II. The result was approximately $400,000 out of a $33 million total raise. The structural reason: dominant family conglomerates (cement, beer, telecoms) earn quasi-monopoly returns well above 12% net, making a regional fund's target return uncompetitive. Smaller wealthy families treat a fund investment as a management relationship, not a passive LP position, creating a governance mismatch.

### Finding 4: DFIs are a more efficient fundraising counterpart than family offices
Granada contrasts the DFI engagement experience favorably: institutions like IFC and DFC issue a clear no early if the thesis does not fit, whereas family office processes can consume four years and still produce nothing. For Pomona, the DFI side letters also come with standardized exclusion lists (no oil and gas, no extractives), which effectively defines the fund's impact guardrails without requiring a separate impact framework. Fund III will be 100% debt, partly because the equity story is undermined by the absence of exit pathways.

### Finding 5: Exit pathways are essentially nonexistent in Central America, pushing Pomona to pure debt
Granada identifies exits as the single largest structural barrier to equity venture investing in the region. There are no functioning secondary markets, no active strategic acquirer pipeline comparable to what exists in Mexico or Colombia, and no public listing venues suited to small regional companies. His solution for Fund III is to abandon equity entirely. He notes that secondary market ideas (e.g., London Stock Exchange listings) have been discussed but not implemented, and that the lack of exits makes the equity return story nearly impossible to tell to LPs.

### Finding 6: The funded VC fund universe in Central America is extremely thin — fewer than five active vehicles
When pressed, Granada could identify only Alterna and IDC (smaller Guatemalan vehicles), KASAIFF, Echo Enterprise Fund, and Pomona — but characterizes Pomona, KASAIFF, and Echo as debt funds, not VC. Caricaco is a family office, not a fund. Balam Capital (Honduras) may have raised for a specific transaction but does not appear to operate a traditional LP fund structure. The implication is that the Central American VC ecosystem, despite surface-level activity, has almost no professionally managed, fully funded equity vehicles.

### Finding 7: Pipeline generation, not investment readiness coaching, is the binding constraint at the scale-up tier
For companies capable of repaying $2–4 million loans — Pomona's target market — the problem is not that they are unready; it is that they are invisible to funds. These companies have only ever dealt with banks, do not attend startup events, and often assume impact investing is for technology startups rather than 30-year-old family businesses. The Pomona Impact Foundation addresses earlier-stage readiness through acceleration programs in agro-processing and green tech (Costa Rica), having helped companies raise millions in grant and debt capital. But Granada argues that active deal sourcing and sector-specific outreach unlocks more investment potential than readiness programs at his ticket size.

## Regulatory & Structural Observations

The region's regulatory landscape is heterogeneous at a granular operational level. El Salvador has enacted explicit fintech legislation; Guatemala has none. This asymmetry means that a company optimized for one country's regulatory environment cannot port its operating model across the border — it must re-license, recapitalize, and often re-partner with a local institution. Similar dynamics apply in Honduras, where political risk compounds regulatory uncertainty. Granada notes that Nicaragua is effectively uninvestable under current conditions. Structuring a multi-country debt investment — such as Pomona's current engagement with a logistics company operating in 29 countries but seeking Central America-focused capital — requires resolving collateral assignment and tranche structure on a country-by-country basis, with no pan-regional legal framework to simplify the process.

## Recommendations & Insights

Granada's primary recommendation for CAF is to act as a capital multiplier for first-time fund managers through matching mechanisms — analogous to Peru's reported two-to-one match program — that reduce first-close risk and allow nascent teams to credibly approach other LPs. He frames this as giving fund managers "a hunting license": a funded team with a thesis can generate pipeline and make investments; an unfunded team cannot, regardless of ecosystem quality. He explicitly calls out that work is needed on both sides of the market simultaneously — fund formation and company pipeline — because neither alone is sufficient. He also recommends that any ecosystem-building investment by CAF distinguish between early-stage readiness programs (valuable for agro and green tech) and the distinct challenge of reaching established, bank-only companies that are already scale-up candidates but unaware of alternative capital sources.
