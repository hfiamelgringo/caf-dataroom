## Executive Summary

Andy Tsao, who leads SVB's international banking division and has nearly thirty years at the bank, offers a practitioner's view of what financial infrastructure the Latin American startup ecosystem lacks and where SVB has attempted to fill gaps. His core message is that banking for innovation-economy companies requires deep pattern recognition about startup burn rates and loss cycles — something traditional banks in the region lack entirely — and that applying conventional covenants to venture-backed companies can actively destroy them. SVB built its Latin American business on this differentiation starting roughly fifteen years ago, when major players like Nubank and Monashiz were just forming.

For CAF's purposes, Tsao's most actionable insights concern two areas: the structural anatomy of cross-border startup financing (offshore Cayman/Delaware holding companies over local operating entities), and the unfulfilled promise of venture debt in the region. SVB co-created the Latin Growth Lending Fund with IDB Invest and Partners for Growth — one of the first regional venture debt vehicles — but Tsao acknowledges the instrument has not scaled as expected, citing cultural resistance to debt financing and prohibitive documentation costs for sub-$5 million loans spanning multiple jurisdictions. He expressed openness to a follow-up conversation about potential partnership structures with CAF.

## Stakeholder Background

Andy Tsao has spent twenty-nine years at Silicon Valley Bank, the last twenty focused on international expansion and the last fifteen running the Global Gateway division, which serves tech startups, scale-ups, and venture capital firms outside the United States. He oversees seven international regions — Asia (largest), Europe, Israel, India, Latin America, Middle East/North Africa, and Australia/New Zealand — and maintains a dedicated Miami-based team for Latin America. His perspective is grounded in longitudinal observation of the Latin American ecosystem from its nascent stage through the current contraction.

## Key Findings

### Finding 1: SVB entered Latin America early and built brand equity before competition existed
SVB began working in Latin America approximately fifteen years ago, when the ecosystem was tiny and firms like Nubank and Monashiz were just starting up. The bank's chief executive joined an immersion trip to the region, which catalyzed a sustained commitment. At the time, no major bank was willing to engage startups meaningfully, and SVB's forty-year focus on the innovation economy — with specialized products, pattern recognition, and investor network — gave it a decisive first-mover advantage.

### Finding 2: Latin American venture-backed startups require offshore structures that SVB is uniquely positioned to serve
Because international venture capital investors insist on familiar legal structures, Latin American startups typically incorporate a Cayman Islands holding company at the top, a Delaware entity in the middle, and a local operating company at the bottom. SVB banks the offshore entities and holds the dollar-denominated capital raised — it cannot provide local peso or real banking. This niche is highly valuable because startups need dedicated accounts to receive VC funding, manage dollar reserves safely, and onshore cash systematically.

### Finding 3: Venture debt requires specialist underwriting that conventional regional banks cannot replicate
SVB pioneered venture debt in the US — typically allowing startups to borrow up to 30% of equity raised on top of their round — using underwriting logic that inverts traditional credit analysis: instead of profitability ratios, it asks whether the company has sufficient runway and investor backing to reach the next milestone. Traditional banks that apply profit-based covenants to venture-backed companies risk triggering defaults and, in severe cases, collapsing companies that would otherwise have been viable.

### Finding 4: The Latin Growth Lending Fund is the region's first venture debt vehicle but has underperformed expectations
SVB partnered with IDB Invest and US-based Partners for Growth to create the Latin Growth Lending Fund, one of the first dedicated venture debt vehicles for Latin America, which remains operational today. Despite this innovation, Tsao says the product has not taken off as hoped, citing two structural obstacles: cultural discomfort with debt financing among Latin American founders and investors, and disproportionately high legal and documentation costs for loans typically under $5 million that span multiple national jurisdictions.

### Finding 5: Capital call financing is SVB's largest fund-banking product but remains US-centric post-acquisition
SVB's fund banking business — which may now exceed the startup banking business in scale — centers on capital call credit lines that allow VC funds to borrow ahead of LP capital calls, enabling just-in-time capital management and rapid deal execution. Larger PE clients also use these lines for IRR enhancement by delaying capital calls. Following the First Citizens acquisition, this and related fund products (GP financing, partner buy-in loans) are primarily US-focused; SVB lost ground internationally during its crisis period and is actively rebuilding trust and market share.

### Finding 6: SVB's Central American client base is small but anchored in micro-VC and family-office-linked funds
In the smaller Central American markets, SVB does not bank many startups directly but does maintain relationships with micro-VC investors, typically funds under $50 million — often in the $20–30 million range — that are linked to family offices. These relationships are valued both intrinsically and as referral channels to portfolio companies. Tsao frames this as a realistic baseline: SVB's presence in the sub-region is thin but not absent.

### Finding 7: Multi-country loan complexity is a structural barrier to scaling venture debt in Central America
A loan of less than $5 million — considered large for the Latin American venture context — that involves a company operating across multiple Central American countries generates legal documentation costs that are disproportionate to the deal size. This cost-to-loan ratio problem has prevented SVB and its partners from cracking the code on sub-regional venture debt, and Tsao flags it as a key design challenge for any CAF-sponsored instrument in this space.

## Regulatory & Structural Observations

Tsao does not engage directly with specific national regulations but makes a structural observation with clear regulatory implications: the entire architecture of US-dollar venture capital in Latin America depends on offshore holding companies (Cayman Islands and Delaware) precisely because local legal and tax frameworks are unsuitable for institutional VC investment. This is not a failure of individual countries so much as a systemic mismatch between how international venture capital operates and what local corporate and securities law offers. Any effort to deepen venture capital in Central America must grapple with whether to work within this offshore structure or attempt to create domestically viable alternatives.

## Recommendations & Insights

- CAF should explore a formal partnership conversation with SVB modeled on the IDB Invest–SVB Latin Growth Lending Fund structure; Tsao explicitly noted openness to a follow-up and offered to share lessons from the IDB experience.
- Any venture debt instrument designed for Central America must address the documentation cost problem for sub-$5 million, multi-country loans — possibly through standardized regional legal frameworks or a shared legal facility underwritten by CAF.
- Cultural resistance to debt financing among founders and investors needs to be addressed through education and ecosystem norm-building before a debt product can scale; launching the instrument alone is insufficient.
- Evaluating any lender or financing agency entering the venture space on whether they genuinely understand startup loss cycles is essential — institutions applying traditional profitability covenants to venture-backed companies are a risk to the ecosystem, not a support.
- SVB's fund-banking products (capital call lines, GP financing) could be valuable to early Central American fund managers if SVB's international business expands again; CAF could potentially serve as an anchor LP or guarantor to make those relationships viable at small fund sizes.
