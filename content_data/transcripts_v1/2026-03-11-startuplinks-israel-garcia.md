## Executive Summary

Israel Garcia, founder of Startup Links, offers a practitioner's view of the Latin American venture capital ecosystem built from six years of community building, event organizing, and data aggregation across Spanish-speaking LATAM. His central diagnosis is structural: the region suffers not from a lack of founders or activity, but from a chronic absence of exits and liquidity. Without returns flowing back to LPs, the asset class cannot mature into a self-sustaining flywheel. His own ecosystem confidence report quantifies this divide starkly — investors are pessimistic while founders remain highly optimistic, a sentiment gap that reflects different time horizons and incentive structures.

For small countries specifically, Garcia argues the primary bottleneck is understanding: policymakers, potential LPs, corporates, and founders each need ecosystem education calibrated to their own objectives, and the most effective delivery mechanism is importing experienced practitioners — people who have sold companies, run accelerators, or deployed capital — rather than expecting local actors to independently discover the asset class.

His platform ambition — to become a Crunchbase/PitchBook equivalent for Spanish-speaking LATAM — is directly relevant to CAF's data and mapping needs, and his pending roundtable of four Central American VCs could be a high-value channel for the project.

## Stakeholder Background

Israel Garcia founded Startup Links six years ago with a broad mandate to support founders; the platform has since evolved into a three-pillar operation covering founder education, startup-investor matchmaking, and ecosystem data/reporting. He co-organized the Mexican edition of a multi-country Tech Week series and recently launched a LATAM VC ecosystem report covering exit activity, investment volume, and a confidence index segmented by founders and investors. He is building a SaaS product to formalize the platform's data layer and has a personal network encompassing approximately 300 VCs via a curated WhatsApp channel. His perspective is regionally grounded, operationally current, and quantitatively informed by his own primary research.

## Key Findings

### Finding 1: Exits and liquidity are the ecosystem's defining bottleneck
The single biggest structural problem across Latin America — including more mature markets like Mexico, Colombia, and Argentina — is the absence of exits. Garcia's report catalogued secondary sales, M&A transactions, and IPOs and found the average time from company founding to exit runs approximately 15–20 years. Without founders liquidating positions and investors returning capital to LPs, venture capital cannot function as an asset class; it operates only as a technology support system.

### Finding 2: A sharp sentiment divide separates founders from investors
Garcia's ecosystem confidence index reveals a striking asymmetry: investors are broadly pessimistic about the current investment environment and near-term returns, while founders remain highly optimistic and are willing to bootstrap with their own resources when venture funding is unavailable. This divergence has practical implications for CAF — interventions aimed at deploying capital may face skeptical LPs even as demand from founders remains robust.

### Finding 3: Small-country ecosystems need imported expertise, not just capital
For early-stage ecosystems in small countries, the primary constraint is comprehension of the asset class itself. Each stakeholder group — government, potential LPs, corporates, founders — needs ecosystem education framed around their own specific objectives. Garcia's prescription is to bring in practitioners who have lived the cycle (founders who exited, fund managers who returned capital) because independent discovery through travel to Silicon Valley or Israel is realistic only for those who already know those reference points exist.

### Finding 4: Fintech remains dominant and Garcia views this as a problem
Fintech continues to capture the largest share of investment volume, transaction count, and active companies in the region. Garcia specifically names Clara, Clip, and similar card-issuance businesses as emblematic. He expresses a clear preference for seeing capital shift toward energy, climate tech, or other sectors in 2026, viewing ongoing fintech concentration as a sign of ecosystem immaturity rather than strength.

### Finding 5: AI adoption in LATAM is use-case-driven, not foundational
LATAM startups are not building foundational AI models; they are fine-tuning and applying models from Google, OpenAI, Anthropic, and others. Garcia views this pragmatically — finding proprietary application layers (he cites a company called Bad Tech working with Google as an example) can still constitute a defensible advantage. The most tractable near-term AI verticals he identifies are voice agents for call centers and customer-success automation, where corporate adoption is already accelerating.

### Finding 6: Tech Week events are a proven mechanism for ecosystem density
Startup Links co-organized Mexico Tech Week and Colombia Tech Week, and Garcia characterizes multi-day, city-wide tech events as one of the most efficient tools for relationship density across the ecosystem. Colombia's model — aligning Colombia Tech Week with the existing GoFest in Bogotá, then extending to Medellín and Barranquilla — demonstrates how event architecture can be adapted to local conditions. Venezuela Tech Week is planned for May 2026 as a centralized event given the smaller ecosystem scale.

### Finding 7: Startup Links is building the data infrastructure CAF lacks for LATAM
The platform's medium-term roadmap is to become a Crunchbase/PitchBook equivalent for Spanish-speaking Latin America, with live dashboards covering who is building what, investor activity, accelerator pipelines, and ecosystem mapping. Garcia is actively developing a SaaS product to formalize this. He also flagged a pending roundtable of four Central American VCs, scheduled for the following week, as a potential resource for the CAF project team.

## Regulatory & Structural Observations

Garcia did not engage directly with regulatory frameworks, but his structural diagnosis implicitly points to a regulatory gap: the absence of secondary market infrastructure and M&A facilitation mechanisms that would enable exits at earlier stages than the 15–20 year timelines his data currently shows. He also noted that cross-border payment innovation — peer-to-peer transfers analogous to Venmo or Brazil's Pix — has stalled in Mexico despite evident demand, suggesting regulatory or interoperability barriers he did not fully diagnose.

## Recommendations & Insights

- **Prioritize exit infrastructure over entry**: CAF and ecosystem partners should focus less on seeding more early-stage companies and more on creating pathways for liquidity — secondary markets, M&A facilitation, and structured exits — since the data shows 15–20 year average timelines are unsustainable for LP retention.
- **Bring practitioners, not only programs**: In small countries, ecosystem education delivered by people who have personally exited companies or returned capital to LPs is more credible and effective than government programs or foreign consultants.
- **Leverage Startup Links' Central American VC roundtable**: Garcia had already scheduled a roundtable with four Central American VCs for the week following this interview, representing a direct pipeline to regional practitioners.
- **Diversify sector focus beyond fintech**: Ecosystem health will improve when capital flows into climate, energy, and other verticals. CAF's investment thesis could explicitly target non-fintech sectors to counterbalance the current concentration.
- **Use Tech Week-style events as ecosystem activation tools**: For small countries, co-designing or sponsoring a centralized tech week event (as was done in Venezuela) could accelerate relationship density and LP education at relatively low cost.
