## Executive Summary

Agostina Martino offered a practitioner's view of the Latin American climate tech startup ecosystem, drawing on her work with Geometría Argentina, a founding-member network of climate-focused startups. She described a handful of revenue-generating companies working on water credits, carbon credits from regenerative ranching, early wildfire detection, drone-based reforestation, and beehive-managed crop productivity — all of which she sees as approaching Series A readiness. Her central concern is that investor understanding of climate tech as a viable business opportunity remains thin across the region: climate tech captures a far smaller share of venture capital than fintech or biotech, and that gap is structural rather than deal-flow-driven.

She also flagged two reference resources — a 2024 Climate Kick report mapping Latin American climate funds, and the Anthem fund newsletter — as more reliable for current fund counts than her own recollection. On a secondary topic, she described Sandex's "AI League for Good" and its associated academy, which runs free, scholarship-based online programs across Latin America bridging junior and semi-senior IT talent gaps, with a new sustainability-in-software track planned for 2025. Both threads are relevant to CAF's interest in ecosystem-building in Central America.

## Stakeholder Background

Agostina Martino is an ecosystem builder based in Argentina with roots in the World Economic Forum's Global Shapers network. She has been active in the Latin American climate tech space through Geometría Argentina, a network of founding-member climate startups, and more recently shifted toward workforce and AI education initiatives through her role with Sandex's nonprofit arm, AI League for Good. Her dual vantage point — investor-facing climate portfolio work and tech-talent development — makes her a useful connector across both verticals.

## Key Findings

### Finding 1: A small cluster of revenue-stage climate startups is approaching Series A in Latin America
Two Geometría Argentina founding members — Killy (water reduction for agriculture) and Morrut (regenerative ranching with carbon credits) — are already generating revenue and approaching Series A scale. Several earlier-stage companies also exist across wildfire detection, drone reforestation, and biodiversity management. Morrut recently received validation from Verra, one of the world's leading carbon credit certifiers, a significant de-risking milestone.

### Finding 2: Water-credit and carbon-credit circular models are the dominant monetization architecture
Killy converts agricultural water savings into tradeable water credits that large corporate water consumers can purchase to offset their footprint, creating a closed incentive loop for farmers. Morrut operates a near-identical structure using regenerative grazing to generate carbon credits. Both models depend on credible third-party validation — Verra's recent certification of Morrut is cited as a key proof point for the model's commercial viability.

### Finding 3: Early wildfire detection and drone reforestation represent adjacent climate tech niches with subscription revenue
A company called Settlements on Fire offers early wildfire detection on a subscription basis, while another startup uses drones to reforest land damaged by fires — with a model more closely tied to the timber production industry than to agriculture. Both represent climate tech sub-sectors that generate recurring or contracted revenue rather than relying on credit markets, which Martino views as a meaningful diversification within the ecosystem.

### Finding 4: Eiru's beehive-management model ties biodiversity to crop productivity in high-value agriculture
Eiru manages beehive networks to improve pollination efficiency for intensive crops — specifically fruits and grapes. The model monetizes biodiversity services by linking measurable productivity gains in high-value crops to managed ecological inputs. Martino described this as one of the more distinctive portfolio cases within Geometría Argentina's membership, bridging biodiversity and agri-tech.

### Finding 5: Investor illiteracy about climate tech as a business opportunity is the primary capital barrier
Martino identified the core fundraising obstacle as investors failing to recognize climate tech as a scalable business opportunity, not a shortage of companies. Climate tech receives a structurally smaller share of Latin American venture capital than fintech or biotech. Longer validation timelines — especially for biotech-adjacent climate solutions — compound the problem. She noted that the 2024 Climate Kick report is the most reliable current source for fund counts in the region.

### Finding 6: Referral to Anthem fund and Fundación Avina as key regional connectors
Martino suggested two specific referrals for CAF's Central America work: Rubén Allman at Anthem, a fund with a broad Latin American portfolio and a regular ecosystem-mapping newsletter, and Fundación Avina, which has multiple active workstreams with agricultural producers operating under regenerative logic. She offered to make introductions to both.

### Finding 7: Sandex's AI academy bridges junior-to-senior IT talent gaps with free online programs across Latin America
The AI League for Good academy, Sandex's nonprofit arm, runs fully online, scholarship-based programs in Spanish for learners across Latin America — from career-changers with no tech background to junior developers needing industry mentorship. Sandex employees serve as mentors, and programs now include AI tool training to accelerate participants to semi-senior level more quickly. A new sustainability-in-software track is planned for 2025, with free access for Spanish-speaking learners globally.

## Regulatory & Structural Observations

Martino did not discuss specific regulations. However, she flagged the structural tension around impact measurement and "impact washing" in Latin American VC — a concern that investors and funds make unsubstantiated impact claims, particularly around climate. She noted active debate in the regional ecosystem about how to set and declare impact metrics rigorously, and endorsed sharing conceptual frameworks for measuring climate impact as a priority for any serious ecosystem-development initiative.

## Recommendations & Insights

- CAF should consult the Climate Kick 2024 report for a current mapping of climate-focused funds in Latin America, and monitor the Anthem fund newsletter for ongoing ecosystem snapshots.
- Introductions to Rubén Allman (Anthem) and Fundación Avina are recommended as starting points for identifying investable climate and agri-tech companies with a Central American or regional focus.
- Any climate tech initiative in Central America should take seriously the coffee harvest vulnerability — Martino cited the collapse of Guatemalan coffee yields as a documented driver of mass migration northward, making agricultural climate resilience both a business opportunity and a humanitarian imperative.
- Investor education is as important as deal sourcing: the capital gap in climate tech is as much a function of investor understanding as it is of company quality or pipeline depth.
- The Sandex / AI League for Good model — free, online, mentorship-backed, Spanish-language IT training — may be a relevant workforce development reference or potential partner for CAF's innovation ecosystem work in Central America, given Sandex's existing collaborators in Guatemala and Peru.
