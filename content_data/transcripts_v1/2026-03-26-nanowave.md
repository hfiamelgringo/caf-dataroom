## Executive Summary
Javier Berrocal is the CEO of Nanowave, a Costa Rican deep-tech startup developing a proprietary plasma-based process to extract cellulose from agricultural biomass — without the sulfite chemicals used in conventional pulp mills — while also producing liquid fertilizer as a byproduct. The company targets three major biomass streams: African palm waste, sugarcane, and pineapple crop residues, the last of which is especially relevant given Costa Rica's position as the world's leading exporter of fresh pineapple.

Berrocal offers a candid and technically grounded view of the structural mismatches between deep-tech, CapEx-intensive ventures and the capital available in the Costa Rican and broader Central American ecosystem. His company raised $582K from local investors and Deep Science Ventures (UK), but is now navigating a nine-month-plus due-diligence process with the Subnational Climate Fund (Switzerland) for a ticket of $5–75M. He regrets incorporating in Delaware, viewing it as a mismatch for a company whose operations, investors, and value chain are entirely regional.

For CAF, Nanowave illustrates a recurring gap: the region produces technically sophisticated, climate-relevant ventures that cannot access European or global climate funds (which focus on Africa and Asia) and are too capital-intensive for typical Latin American VC funds. This company represents a potential investable asset for a patient, infrastructure-tolerant regional fund of the type CAF is scoping.

## Stakeholder Background
Javier Berrocal D. is an agronomist with an MBA in finance. His prior experience spans agriculture, agro-industry, and financial market infrastructure — he was a co-founder of the Costa Rica Commerce Exchange. He also helped establish SIMS, a think tank focused on sustainable markets in Central America. His co-founder, Andrés del Alcázar, is a biotechnology engineer. The company runs a pilot laboratory and pilot plant in Colombia for logistical and cost reasons, while maintaining its operating subsidiary, Nanowave CR, in Costa Rica. His dual background in agronomy and capital markets makes him an unusually well-positioned voice on both the technology and the financing constraints facing climate-tech ventures in the region.

## Key Findings

### Finding 1: Proprietary Plasma Technology Offers a Chemical-Free Alternative to Conventional Pulp Processing
Nanowave has developed a physical (not chemical) plasma-based process to separate cellulose from agricultural biomass using electrical energy, eliminating the sulfite-based chemicals that make conventional pulp mills highly polluting. The process produces two outputs: commodity-grade cellulose and liquid fertilizer. The technology is in the second phase of the US patent process, giving the company meaningful IP protection in its primary export market.

### Finding 2: Three Biomass Feedstocks Target a $130B Global Market Growing at 9% Per Year
The company's primary biomass targets are African palm empty fruit bunches (the largest addressable market), sugarcane residues (relevant across a north-south corridor from the United States to Brazil), and pineapple crop residues — where Costa Rica's status as the world's top fresh pineapple exporter creates a natural feedstock advantage. The global cellulose market is valued at $130B annually with a compound annual growth rate of 9%, providing a large and expanding demand base.

### Finding 3: $582K Raised Entirely from Costa Rican Investors and One UK Venture Studio
The seed-prior raise of $582K came 100% from Costa Rican sources — described as "fools, friends, and family" — plus $100K from Deep Science Ventures, a London-based venture studio that co-originated the project alongside CRUSA, a $60M Costa Rican trust fund created when USAID exited Costa Rica. The principal investor is re-investing in the next round. The company now needs $2.5M for a seed round to build one or two modular 50-ton-per-day model plants.

### Finding 4: Nine-Month-Plus Diligence with Swiss Climate Fund Signals Slow but Potentially Large Capital Event
Nanowave has been in process with the Subnational Climate Fund — a Pegasus-affiliated Swiss vehicle — for over nine months, with a ticket range of $5–75M. The process involves an environmental assessment by Futuris (Costa Rica) and financial due diligence by a Latin American consultancy. The proposal has since been expanded to $8M for a larger demonstration plant. The timeline illustrates the friction deep-tech companies face when accessing international climate finance.

### Finding 5: Delaware Incorporation Viewed in Retrospect as a Strategic Mistake
Berrocal stated plainly that incorporating the parent company in Delaware was a decision he now considers wrong for a company whose investors, customers, operations, and value chain are entirely in Latin America. The 100% subsidiary structure (Nanowave CR in Costa Rica) adds legal and administrative complexity without providing meaningful benefits for regional fundraising or operations. This is a cautionary data point for ecosystem builders advising early-stage regional founders on corporate structure.

### Finding 6: European Climate Funds Systematically Exclude Latin America, Leaving a Capital Gap for CapEx-Heavy Ventures
Berrocal identified a structural market failure: European climate and impact funds concentrate their mandates on Africa and Asia, effectively bypassing Latin America. At the same time, the typical Latin American VC fund is sized and structured for asset-light software businesses, not for capital-intensive manufacturing or processing ventures like Nanowave. The result is a funding desert for deep-tech and climate-tech companies in the region that require physical infrastructure.

### Finding 7: African Palm Feedstock Faces ESG Perception Headwinds Despite Waste-Stream Logic
Despite African palm empty fruit bunches being post-harvest agricultural waste — not requiring new land conversion — Berrocal noted that investor resistance to anything associated with palm oil is a recurring obstacle in fundraising conversations. This perception gap between the sustainability reality of the waste-stream business model and the reputational baggage of the palm sector creates unnecessary friction and may require dedicated investor education or third-party certification to overcome.

## Regulatory & Structural Observations
The business model — modular plants co-located alongside existing agro-industrial facilities, operating as a franchisor — avoids the need for greenfield site development but still requires significant CapEx, which sits outside the comfort zone of most regional venture investors. The pilot plant is located in Colombia rather than Costa Rica for logistical and economic reasons, which may complicate narrative coherence for Costa Rica-focused investors or grant programs. No specific regulatory barriers were cited, but the company's cross-border structure (Delaware parent, Costa Rican subsidiary, Colombian operations, Swiss investor process) adds jurisdictional complexity that is common among regionally ambitious but resource-constrained deep-tech ventures.

## Recommendations & Insights
Berrocal implicitly argues that the region needs patient, infrastructure-tolerant capital — whether from development banks like CAF, dedicated climate funds, or hybrid grant-equity vehicles — that can bridge the gap between early proof-of-concept and commercial-scale demonstration plants. He sees CRUSA as an important but limited local resource. His experience with the Subnational Climate Fund suggests that international climate finance is theoretically accessible but operationally slow and geographically biased. For CAF specifically, his case supports designing fund instruments that can accommodate CapEx-heavy ventures, longer commercialization timelines, and multi-country operational footprints — rather than replicating software-VC structures ill-suited to the region's most distinctive innovation assets.
