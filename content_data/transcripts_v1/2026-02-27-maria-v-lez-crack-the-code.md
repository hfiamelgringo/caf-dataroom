## Executive Summary

María Vélez is the founder of Crack the Code, a for-profit social impact company that embeds AI, data, and programming curricula directly into mandatory public high school schedules across Colombia — funded primarily through municipal government contracts. The company has reached 23,000 active students across five major city contracts, with a target of 30,000 by year-end. Its model resolves a persistent government execution failure: municipalities consistently budget for digital-skills training but cannot spend it because schools lack computers and internet. Crack the Code fills that gap by fundraising privately for hardware and connectivity, then charging governments roughly $130 per student per year for its instructional services.

The model is financially structured as a hybrid: a for-profit company in Peru and Colombia holds all software IP and collects licensing fees, while a Colombian foundation serves as the contracting vehicle for municipal procurement and receives corporate grants earmarked for hardware donations. This architecture gives the organization the procurement credentials ("idoneidad") required to win competitive government tenders while keeping technology assets in the private entity. Seventy percent of graduates placed in formal employment are the first formal workers in their households — a statistic Vélez uses as the central impact anchor.

For CAF and PFA, Crack the Code represents a replicable public-private model for Central America and the Dominican Republic. Vélez is already in active discussions with Bolivia (a 3,000-student pilot with potential CAF co-funding) and El Salvador, and is explicitly seeking bold municipal leaders, corporate donors willing to fund hardware, and a credit facility to bridge government payment delays.

## Stakeholder Background

María Vélez is a Costa Rican–Colombian entrepreneur who founded Crack the Code roughly eight to nine years ago, beginning operations in Peru before expanding into Colombia approximately three years ago. She built the company's legal and contractual architecture deliberately — making the Peruvian entity the majority owner of the Colombian subsidiary so that accumulated years of experience and contract volume could transfer, satisfying the procurement eligibility requirements Colombian municipalities require. She previously connected with PFA predecessor organization Partnership for Central America through Mark Lopes. Her perspective is grounded in years of operational execution at the intersection of government procurement, corporate philanthropy, and EdTech product development.

## Key Findings

### Finding 1: Government-contracted mandatory curriculum is the core scaling mechanism
Crack the Code does not sell to schools as individual buyers; it signs multi-year commercial contracts or collaboration agreements directly with city-level secretariats of education, making its two-hour-per-week AI and digital-skills course a mandatory curriculum slot. This approach unlocks scale unavailable to optional or extracurricular EdTech products and is enabled specifically by Colombia's decentralized education governance, where municipal principals and secretariats retain curricular flexibility independent of the national Ministry of Education. The company has executed contracts with five large city governments and expects to reach 30,000 active students by end of 2026.

### Finding 2: $130 per student per year is the current unit economics anchor, with a path to ~$65
The all-in cost to government is approximately $130 per student per year, which covers live virtual instruction delivered by Crack the Code's own teachers. As the company rolls out its AI Copilot tool — built on an proprietary large language model trained on over 80,000 hours of recorded classes — and progressively transfers teaching responsibility to trained public school teachers, the per-student cost is expected to drop by roughly half. The long-term goal is to reduce cost sufficiently that each government can expand to 100% coverage of its public high school population.

### Finding 3: Hardware and connectivity are the unlocking constraint — and the fundraising lever
Nearly every municipal government that approaches Crack the Code has digital-skills line items in its official governance plan but cannot execute them because schools lack computers and internet. Crack the Code resolves this by fundraising from large regional corporates — including Fundación Santo Domingo, Argos, Bavaria, and others — and using those grants to purchase and donate equipment. The company currently pays the monthly internet bill for 53 public schools in Colombia. This hardware-subsidy offer is what converts a waiting list of 75 schools into committed program participants.

### Finding 4: The for-profit/foundation hybrid is a deliberate procurement architecture, not a transitional structure
The company operates two legal entities simultaneously. The Colombian and Peruvian for-profit companies own all software, IP, and employ developers; the Colombian foundation signs municipal contracts when procurement rules require it and receives hardware-earmarked corporate grants. The foundation pays a per-student licensing fee back to the for-profit for use of the platform — described as roughly $50 of the $130 per-student contract value. This structure was designed specifically to satisfy the legal categories for direct contracting under Colombia's Science and Technology Law and for foundation-eligible competitive tenders, while keeping the revenue-generating technology assets in the private entity.

### Finding 5: 70% of placed graduates are first-generation formal workers — the core social mobility metric
Of high school students that Crack the Code graduates and places into formal employment, 70% are the first person in their household to hold formal employment. Vélez frames this as the central argument against the traditional four-year university pathway for this population: given Latin America's average 50% informal economy share and high secondary-school dropout rates, equipping students with AI generalist skills before graduation and connecting them to internships, technical degrees, or boot camps creates a more reliable path to formalization than conventional college enrollment.

### Finding 6: Bolivia and El Salvador are the active near-term expansion targets, with CAF already in the picture
Vélez is in active negotiations with Bolivia's Minister of Education for a 3,000-student pilot expected to launch within approximately six weeks of the interview date. The Bolivian government is separately in conversation with CAF about funding the program. She also met with El Salvador's consulate in Washington two weeks before the interview, citing the country's experimentation with AI copilots for students as a signal of receptiveness. Both conversations suggest an opening for CAF-affiliated organizations to serve as regional facilitators or funders.

### Finding 7: The company's capital needs are a credit line and corporate hardware donors — not equity
Crack the Code is not seeking equity investment at this stage. Its two stated capital needs are: (1) ongoing grants from foundations or multinationals to fund computer and internet donations to schools, and (2) a revolving credit facility or bridge loan to manage the two-to-three-month payment delays common in municipal government contracting. Vélez cited a specific example of a December 2025 invoice from a large project that took two months to be paid. A line of credit tied to government receivables would directly enable faster expansion without diluting ownership.

## Regulatory & Structural Observations

Colombia's Science and Technology Law enables a mechanism called direct contracting that allows municipalities to hire private companies or foundations for educational services without a full open tender — significantly reducing the procurement cycle time. For competitive tenders, a separate foundation category called "collaboration agreement" allows foundations to bid with a co-investment requirement of at least 30% of contract value; Crack the Code meets this through its hardware donations. The eligibility requirements for either contract type include minimum years of operation, number of previously executed contracts, and total dollar value of prior contracts — criteria the company satisfies by structuring its Colombian subsidiary as a majority-owned subsidiary of the older Peruvian entity, allowing inherited credentials. This legal architecture is country-specific; Vélez noted that Peru and other markets operate differently and require case-by-case analysis.

## Recommendations & Insights

Vélez's implicit recommendations for partners like CAF and PFA are: (1) identify bold, innovation-oriented municipal executives — the Barranquilla mayor was her example of the profile that makes rapid deployment possible — because the political will of a single leader is the primary bottleneck; (2) connect Crack the Code with regional corporate foundations willing to fund hardware as a philanthropic contribution, since this is the supply-side constraint that unlocks government budget execution; (3) explore a credit facility or receivables-based bridge financing product, since government payment delays are the main cash-flow risk to scaling; and (4) use CAF's existing Bolivia relationship as an entry point for co-funding a pilot that is already structurally ready to launch. She also suggested that a "fear of missing out" dynamic among mayors — where one visionary leader's success creates pressure on peers — is a replicable demand-generation strategy.
