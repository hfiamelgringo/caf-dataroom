## Executive Summary
Bruno Kuntz of Maya Capital traces Brazil's venture capital ecosystem from its origins in the early 2000s — anchored by the Buscapé exit and the founding of Kaszek Ventures by Mercado Livre's founders — through the 2017–2021 low-interest-rate boom that flooded the market with capital and produced a generation of unicorns, to the post-2022 correction and maturation. He identifies three interlocking catalysts: a landmark early exit that recycled entrepreneur capital back into the ecosystem, the standardization of SAFE instruments that reduced legal friction for early-stage deals, and university-based talent pipelines that produced the engineers and operators needed to build scalable companies. Today Brazil has at least 30 pre-seed and seed funds, with Kaszek alone managing roughly $1 billion.

For CAF and development banks looking to replicate this trajectory in smaller, nascent markets, Kuntz recommends two concrete interventions: partnering with local universities to deploy early capital and build credibility for entrepreneurship as a career path, and funding scholarship programs that send high-potential young people abroad — noting that most of Brazil's top founders hold US graduate degrees and returned home to build companies. His underlying thesis is that without talent and without a first credible exit to anchor the flywheel, no amount of institutional capital will be sufficient.

## Stakeholder Background
Bruno Kuntz spent approximately seven years working directly with startups after an earlier career in banking and consulting. He served as Head of Finance and CFO at multiple startups before joining Maya Capital, a São Paulo-based early-stage fund currently raising its third fund. At Maya Capital he focuses on fund modeling, portfolio support, and deal evaluation. The fund deploys roughly 70% of its capital in Brazil, with the remainder in Mexico, Argentina, and Colombia. His perspective bridges the operator and investor sides of the early-stage market, and his firsthand experience with Brazil's full cycle — from pioneer years through boom and correction — makes him a direct reference point for ecosystem-building strategy.

## Key Findings

### Finding 1: Brazil's VC ecosystem was seeded by a single landmark exit that recycled entrepreneur capital
The first major catalyst was Buscapé, a price-comparison marketplace built inside a University of São Paulo lab in the early 2000s. Its founder used proceeds from that exit to launch one of Brazil's first true venture funds, One She's Capital, in the mid-2000s. This established the flywheel logic: without a credible first exit, there is no recycled capital, no role models, and no incentive for talented people to take the entrepreneurial path.

### Finding 2: Kaszek Ventures, founded by Mercado Livre's co-founders, became the anchor institution of Brazil's VC market
Kaszek was established by the founding team of Mercado Livre and grew to become the most respected fund in Brazil. Its most celebrated bet was leading the seed round of Nubank when it was a two-person operation in a small São Paulo apartment. That outcome allowed Kaszek to raise successive funds; its current vehicle is approximately $1 billion, enabling it to lead seed rounds and participate in growth-stage investments.

### Finding 3: The 2017–2021 low-interest-rate environment triggered a capital flood and ecosystem acceleration
Under the Temer government around 2017–2018, falling interest rates drove institutional and retail money into venture capital for the first time at scale. Startups raised pre-seed rounds on PowerPoint decks alone, valuations inflated, and unicorns including Gympass (now Wellhub) and Loft emerged. Crucially, startup employer branding hit an all-time high, pulling talented professionals away from banks and consulting firms toward the startup career path.

### Finding 4: SAFE instruments dramatically reduced legal friction and accelerated early deal velocity
The adoption of Simple Agreements for Future Equity (SAFEs) — a structure originally developed by Y Combinator roughly a decade ago — became a near-universal standard for pre-seed and seed rounds in Brazil. SAFEs allow founders to defer expensive legal work, cap investor liability to the amount invested, and convert to priced equity only at a Series A or B. This standardization, legally accepted in Brazil, is credited by Kuntz as a significant structural catalyst for deal flow.

### Finding 5: The "Cayman sandwich" is the standard legal structure for fundable Brazilian startups
The typical structure for startups raising institutional rounds is a Cayman Islands holding entity that owns a Delaware LLC, which in turn owns the Brazilian operating company. For startups raising below roughly $200,000, a local convertible note instrument is used first as a cost-saving measure, eventually converting into a SAFE as the company grows. This legal standardization has reduced friction for cross-border investment.

### Finding 6: Senior operational talent remains Brazil's most persistent ecosystem gap
Despite having 30+ active pre-seed and seed funds and a robust pipeline of early-stage engineers, Brazil struggles to find executives capable of scaling companies from 1,000 to 10,000 employees. Kuntz attributes this to the limited number of companies that have reached that scale domestically, forcing funds and late-stage startups to recruit from the United States and other markets. He identifies this as the single change he would make with a "magic wand."

### Finding 7: Most of Brazil's high-profile founders hold US graduate degrees — scholarship programs are a proven talent lever
Kuntz notes a consistent pattern: the founders behind Brazil's breakout companies typically studied at Harvard, Stanford, or Berkeley before returning home to build. He frames internationally funded scholarship programs as a practical development-bank tool — not just for technical skill, but for expanding ambition and exposure to what is possible at global scale.

## Regulatory & Structural Observations
Kuntz highlights the legal acceptance of SAFE instruments in Brazil as a meaningful policy-level enabler. Because the instrument is recognized by Brazilian law and does not immediately create cap-table or liability obligations, it lowered the cost and complexity of early investing substantially. He implicitly contrasts this with markets where the absence of such standardized, legally recognized instruments forces founders and investors into expensive bespoke legal work, adding friction that discourages deal activity at the pre-seed stage.

## Recommendations & Insights
For a development bank like CAF seeking to build a venture ecosystem in a small, nascent market, Kuntz offers two prioritized recommendations:

1. **Partner with local universities and deploy early capital through them.** Universities are the natural source of first-generation technical talent and the institutional home for early experimentation. Funding university labs and startup programs signals that entrepreneurship is a legitimate career path and creates the conditions for a Buscapé-type origin story.

2. **Fund scholarship programs that send high-potential young people abroad.** The demonstrated correlation between US graduate education and high-profile Brazilian founder outcomes suggests that exposure to global ecosystems, networks, and ambition levels is a formative input — not just a nice-to-have. A structured scholarship-to-return program could compress the timeline for producing ecosystem-ready founders.

His broader framing is that capital alone is insufficient. The sequencing matters: talent formation and credible early exits must precede institutional fund formation. Development banks that deploy capital before talent and role models exist are likely to see poor returns and limited ecosystem catalysis.
