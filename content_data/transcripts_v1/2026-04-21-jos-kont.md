## Executive Summary
José Kont, a Central American venture ecosystem analyst at Quantico, provides a nuanced assessment of the regulatory and structural barriers facing venture capital in Guatemala and the broader region. He is broadly supportive of startup legislation but primarily for its public-awareness and agenda-setting value rather than as a direct capital-deployment mechanism — warning that poorly designed laws will be politicized. His most urgent structural concerns center on the absence of legal recognition for SAFE instruments in Latin America's civil-law system, the lack of fintech regulatory sandboxes in markets where fintech attracted 70% of regional VC capital in Q1 2026, and the fragmented country-by-country licensing burden that penalizes cross-border scaling in small markets.

On pension funds as a potential LP base, Kont is cautiously supportive but identifies a layered set of barriers: Guatemala's pay-as-you-go structure eliminates the portfolio-optimization incentive, regional VC funds are too small to absorb pension-sized tickets, and pension managers categorize venture under a generic "alternatives" bucket alongside private equity and real estate without understanding the asset class. He points to El Salvador's full tax exemption for tech-based startups and fund managers as the region's most ambitious and successful fiscal incentive model, and recommends CAF study it directly through the Cornest accelerator's leadership.

## Stakeholder Background
José Kont is a co-founder and ecosystem analyst at Quantico, a Guatemala-based research and advisory firm that publishes annual and thematic reports on Latin American venture capital ecosystems. He has deep familiarity with Central American startup dynamics, regulatory frameworks, and cross-border investment flows, and maintains active relationships with ecosystem actors across El Salvador, Guatemala, Honduras, and Colombia. His vantage point bridges empirical data — Quantico publishes quarterly regional VC statistics — and on-the-ground ecosystem participation through event support and stakeholder convening.

## Key Findings

### Finding 1: Startup Laws Are Worth Pursuing, But Primarily for Their Public Awareness Effect — Not Capital Deployment
Kont supports startup legislation but qualifies it sharply: its primary value is the public dialogue it generates, not the incentives it creates. He argues that none of the four target countries has an institution whose mandate includes explaining high-impact entrepreneurship, how it is financed, or why startups are not bankable. A well-designed law with a built-in outreach office — modeled on Mexico's National Entrepreneurship Institute, which he credits with building a generation of fund managers — could create durable "muscle memory" in the entrepreneurial culture and help regulators understand the asset class they are expected to attract.

### Finding 2: Politicization Is the Primary Risk in Startup Law Design — Internal Qualification Criteria Are the Safeguard
Kont warns that any government-administered seed capital program risks being captured for political patronage, citing Guatemala's National Secretariat of Innovation, which currently runs an Instagram campaign offering seed grants of $20,000–$50,000 and receives very few serious applicants. The safeguard, in his view, is embedding eligibility criteria directly into the law's text rather than delegating them to future regulations — this removes bureaucratic discretion and prevents post-enactment manipulation by legislators or officials seeking to distribute favors.

### Finding 3: Absence of SAFE Recognition in Civil-Law Systems Is a Foundational Gap
Kont identifies the lack of legal standing for SAFE instruments as a core structural flaw affecting all of Latin America, not just Central America. Under the region's civil-law framework, signing a SAFE is functionally equivalent to sending an email — it carries no enforceable guarantees for either founder or investor. The result is a compulsory structural detour: companies form domestically, raise early capital, and then execute a "flip" to Delaware when they seek institutional investment, adding cost and complexity at a critical growth stage.

### Finding 4: Fintech Absorbs 70% of Regional VC Capital but Faces Near-Bank-Level Regulatory Burdens
As of Q1 2026, fintech attracted 70% of all venture capital deployed across Latin America, yet no Central American country has a functioning regulatory sandbox for the sector. Kont cites Mexican fintech Plata — which raised nearly $400 million — as still awaiting a banking license thirteen years after beginning the application process. He argues that sector-specific regulatory harmonization across Central America, brokered institution-to-institution between supervisory bodies such as Honduras's National Commission for Banks and Insurance and Guatemala's Banking Superintendency, is more politically feasible than broad multilateral agreements and would have an outsized impact given fintech's capital concentration.

### Finding 5: Guatemala's Pay-As-You-Go Pension Structure Eliminates the Incentive to Optimize Investment Portfolios
Guatemala's Social Security Institute operates a pay-as-you-go system in which contributions are pooled and distributed, offering contributors no choice of manager and the institution no competitive pressure to generate superior returns. Kont contrasts this with individual-account systems like Chile's and Mexico's, where fund managers compete for contributors. Without that competition, there is structurally zero incentive for a Guatemalan pension fund to explore venture capital or any higher-yielding alternative — the institution is indifferent to performance optimization.

### Finding 6: Regional VC Funds Are Too Small to Accept Pension-Sized Tickets — A Fund-Size Mismatch Blocks the LP Pathway
Even if pension funds were motivated to invest in venture, the mechanics do not work: 80% of Latin American VC funds are capitalized at $10–15 million, while even a small pension fund's minimum meaningful ticket would be approximately $50 million. Fewer than a handful of VC funds in all of Latin America exceed $400 million. Kont's proposed solution is a fund-of-funds structure, where pension funds allocate a slice of their alternatives budget to a diversified vehicle investing across multiple VC portfolios — reducing risk through vintage diversification and enabling tickets sized for institutional deployment.

### Finding 7: El Salvador's Full Tax Exemption for Tech Startups and Fund Managers Is the Region's Only Comprehensive Fiscal Incentive Model
El Salvador exempts qualifying tech-based startups and venture fund managers from income tax and capital gains tax on investments made in-country — a policy Kont describes as uniquely ambitious in the region. Qualification requires incorporation as a new entity under a designated tech-company category, followed by government verification of eligibility. The incentive is time-limited and graduated, beginning at 100% exemption and stepping down over a five-year period, conditioned on attracting foreign investment and creating employment. Kont also notes that the Cornest accelerator has a match-fund agreement with the Salvadoran government: for each startup accelerated through their program, Cornest contributes $100,000 and the government matches with an additional $100,000.

## Regulatory & Structural Observations

The standard 10-year closed-end fund structure that governs most global VC vehicles — a model dating to the 1950s — is now creating significant problems because most startups require more than a decade to reach scale-up status. Kont notes that changing this standard is extremely difficult precisely because of its global entrenchment and the unwillingness of most fund managers to challenge it. He frames this as a structural advantage for Central American ecosystems: because no rigid framework is yet in place, CAF has an opportunity to build foundational infrastructure from scratch — establishing norms that can be extended and refined rather than dismantled and rebuilt.

Kont also identifies a classification problem that distorts pension fund behavior globally: venture capital is lumped under a generic "alternatives" bucket alongside private equity and real estate. Because pension managers already tick the "alternatives" box through real estate or buyout allocations, they believe they are already exposed to the asset class. Closing this gap, he argues, requires structured education sessions between VC managers and pension fund managers so each side understands what the other actually needs.

## Recommendations & Insights

- **Design startup laws with built-in qualification criteria** — not delegated to future regulations — to prevent politicization. Include a statutory office responsible for public education on high-impact entrepreneurship.
- **Pursue sector-specific regulatory harmonization for fintech** at the Central American level, brokered through direct dialogue between existing supervisory bodies. This is more achievable than broad multilateral agreements and addresses the asset class receiving the most capital.
- **Introduce legal recognition of SAFE instruments** or develop a functionally equivalent civil-law mechanism with enforceable minimum guarantees for both founders and investors.
- **Replicate El Salvador's tax exemption model** — or adapt elements of it — in Guatemala, Honduras, Costa Rica, and the Dominican Republic. CAF should commission a detailed study of the Salvadoran framework, starting with the Cornest accelerator's José Rodríguez as a primary source.
- **Pilot a fund-of-funds structure** to bridge the ticket-size mismatch between pension funds and regional VC vehicles, targeting the slice of pension alternatives allocations currently flowing to passive instruments.
- **Create structured knowledge-exchange forums** between VC fund managers and pension fund managers so that pension institutions understand venture capital as a distinct asset class before any allocation policy discussions can proceed.
