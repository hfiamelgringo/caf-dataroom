## Executive Summary

Jorge Vargas, Michael, and Carolle of Venture.do — an early-stage B2B SaaS-focused fund operating in the Dominican Republic — offer a frank assessment of one of the region's least-developed venture ecosystems. Their core argument is that the DR suffers from a triple constraint: a small domestic market that cannot support exponential startup growth, a legal and financial infrastructure not built for equity investment, and a cultural aversion to startup-style risk. The result is a consistent pattern of diaspora founders: Dominican entrepreneurs raise money and build companies offshore — in the U.S., Mexico, or Spain — because the local environment makes it structurally irrational to stay.

Despite this, the partners identify meaningful opportunity in tourism, fintech, real estate technology, consumer goods, and diaspora-linked financial services. With over 80% mobile penetration and only 50% banking coverage, fintech addressing the unbanked population is already gaining traction. The fund itself recently closed a $700,000 Mastercard contract for their portfolio company Nipi in Santo Domingo, with 37 additional cities as potential expansion targets. For CAF, this interview is a critical baseline on what is missing — in regulation, coordination, and capital infrastructure — and who the key institutional actors are to engage.

## Stakeholder Background

Jorge Vargas is a software engineer by training who built one of the Dominican Republic's earliest tech communities, co-founding the Python and Linux user groups and launching the country's first Hackers and Founders chapter. He has co-founded and shut down multiple startups, and three years ago put $2 million of personal capital into a seed-stage investment vehicle alongside Michael, who previously created the first investment fund at Banco de Reservas (the state-owned commercial bank). Their current vehicle, Venture.do, is raising its first formal institutional fund focused on B2B SaaS across Latin America. Carolle manages operations and portfolio support. Their combined perspective spans community building, public sector fund creation, and active early-stage investing.

## Key Findings

### Finding 1: The DR has fewer than five functioning VC funds — and most are informal or misclassified
By the partners' count, only three entities formally operate as venture capital funds in the Dominican Republic: Boost (which presents itself as a VC but is structured as an accelerator), an angel syndicate that functions like an SPV, and Venture.do itself. Two earlier funds — Poe and United Partners — made investments but are no longer actively deploying. Several family offices and private equity vehicles exist but are not exclusively VC-focused.

### Finding 2: Tech startups that have raised more than $1 million are nearly impossible to count — because they didn't incorporate locally
The partners estimate fewer than ten Silicon Valley-style tech startups have raised over $1 million while remaining based in the DR. The more common pattern is Dominican founders incorporating in Delaware, raising in the U.S. or Mexico, and building teams split between countries. One example: a three-Dominican, one-Colombian founding team incorporated in Tampa, operating on blockchain, with zero Dominican revenue. Venture.do's own fund does not legally exist in the Dominican Republic.

### Finding 3: Founders leave because local fundraising is slow, investor expectations are misaligned, and the market is too small
Jean-Pierre, a founder backed by Michael's Banco de Reservas fund, spent nearly a year raising $300,000 locally while investors pressured him for returns inconsistent with venture norms. He later applied to Y Combinator, was accepted, and raised $300,000 rapidly. He now runs Pana Credito, a cross-border stablecoin finance platform. The pattern repeats: local capital is slow, expensive in equity terms, and held by investors who do not understand startup risk profiles.

### Finding 4: Legal infrastructure is fundamentally incompatible with venture investment
The DR has no SAFE instrument recognized under local law. Incorporating a company takes two to four weeks. Preferred shares and common shares require tax payments that make standard equity structures prohibitively expensive. E-commerce is hampered because the tax authority taxes gross transaction value rather than net commissions, making marketplace models financially unworkable. Digital signatures are not legally valid under the U.S. framework, forcing real estate buyers to physically fly in to sign documents.

### Finding 5: Tourism, fintech, real estate tech, and diaspora services are the highest-opportunity sectors
The current government has reached 10 million annual tourists — a national milestone — making anything linked to tourism and short-term real estate a priority investment area. On the fintech side, 80%+ mobile penetration combined with only 50% banking coverage creates a massive unbanked population addressable via mobile lending and payments. Consumer goods targeting lower-income buyers and diaspora-linked services (remittances, cross-border logistics) round out the top opportunities. Venture.do has a portfolio company — Nipi — with a $700,000 Mastercard contract in Santo Domingo and a path to 37 additional Dominican cities.

### Finding 6: Government policy discontinuity has dismantled ecosystem infrastructure built over a decade
When the ruling party changed six years ago, the new administration systematically dismantled predecessor programs — not because they were ineffective, but as a political signal. The partners describe a predictable four-year cycle: year one is demolition, year two is rebuilding, year three is realizing what was destroyed had value, and year four is elections. The national entrepreneurship network, once a coordination hub, has become dysfunctional. The Ministry of Industry and Commerce has recently changed ministers again, resetting institutional relationships.

### Finding 7: Ecosystem fragmentation and ego-driven community silos are replacing earlier collaborative culture
Both Jorge and Michael independently cite a collapse in ecosystem collaboration. Where early events drew broad, cross-sector participation, communities have splintered into personality-branded groups. Jorge describes the shift from "the Python community" to "Pineda's Python community" — where individual ownership has replaced collective identity. Michael adds that the early culture of shared momentum ("let's all do this together") has given way to individualism and visible lack of results.

## Regulatory & Structural Observations

The Dominican Republic lacks the foundational legal instruments that make venture investment efficient. There is no local equivalent to the SAFE note. Company formation timelines of two to four weeks are a barrier for fast-moving deals. The tax treatment of preferred shares creates a punitive cost for establishing standard VC-compatible equity structures. The tax authority's treatment of gross transaction value rather than net commissions makes e-commerce and marketplace business models economically unviable. Digital signature law follows the European model and is not recognized under U.S. legal frameworks, creating friction specifically in real estate transactions where foreign buyers must physically appear to close. Michael frames these compounding issues as making the DR structurally hostile to startup formation at scale, regardless of talent availability or market interest.

## Recommendations & Insights

The partners recommend CAF engage three specific institutions as priority ecosystem informants: (1) Guillermo, current director of the Banco de Reservas innovation program that Michael originally created; (2) Inicia, the family office of one of the DR's oldest wealthy families, which has made fintech investments at Series A/B across Latin America and placed tickets into international VC funds — a potential anchor LP or co-investor; and (3) Katherine, director of the Dominican Fintech Association, which the partners consider the best-organized ecosystem body in the country, with clear member categories across fintechs, startups, and allied organizations. Michael additionally recommends engaging the Ministry of Industry and Commerce's entrepreneurship and SME promotion directorates, the national entrepreneurship network for a diagnostic view of active actors, and the angel investor network.

On regulatory reform, the partners would prioritize: legalizing and standardizing SAFE instruments, creating a fast-track company formation process for startups, clarifying tax treatment for marketplace and commission-based business models, and harmonizing digital signature law with international standards to unlock remote real estate transactions.

## Notable Quotes

> "Here, if your startup fails, you can't open another company — because everyone says, don't give him money, he stole it. Even though everyone knows it was risk capital. That's the mentality that nobody talks about out loud." — Michael, on cultural stigma around startup failure

> "Whoever doesn't think in terms of regional or global scale doesn't raise money. They get stuck giving away half their company." — Jorge, on why local market focus is a fundraising liability

> "The government's unspoken rule when a new party takes power is: nothing from before works, everything must be destroyed. They spend a full year tearing down what was there." — Jorge, on political discontinuity and ecosystem regression

> "It's easier for me to buy something on Amazon US and have it arrive at my house than to buy from a Dominican e-commerce site." — Jorge, illustrating the infrastructure and logistics gap that creates opportunity for cross-border commerce startups

> "We banned the word 'SME' from the entire event. The more observant people noticed — nobody talked about small businesses or traditional entrepreneurship. Everything was about high impact, dynamic entrepreneurship, disruption. That's when perception started to shift." — Michael, on reframing the Dominican startup narrative in the early 2010s
