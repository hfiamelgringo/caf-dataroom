## Executive Summary

Mak Gutierrez, a veteran ecosystem builder who helped grow Guadalajara's startup scene from near zero, offers a frank practitioner's perspective on what development institutions get wrong — and right — when trying to catalyze innovation ecosystems in Latin America. His central argument is that impact is better achieved by building scalable commercial companies (many of which become impact businesses by accident, particularly in fintech) than through grant-based impact programs that rarely move the needle. The most structural barriers he identifies are not capital shortages per se, but ecosystem cultural gaps: universities that extract fees rather than deploy capital, governments that measure new business creation but make it nearly impossible to close a company, and a near-total absence of M&A culture that leaves failed startups with real assets unable to find buyers.

His most novel recommendation for CAF is to consider a dedicated debt facility specifically designed to finance acquisitions of distressed or transitioning startups — a mechanism analogous to the U.S. Small Business Administration's acquisition lending program. This would simultaneously create exits (liquidity for founders and investors) and safety nets (soft landings for teams and assets), unlocking two of the ecosystem's most persistent missing layers. He also argues that investor education programs should deliberately send local angels to invest in mature ecosystems abroad before asking them to invest at home, where deal quality is too nascent to teach good judgment.

## Stakeholder Background

Mak Gutierrez built his ecosystem credentials by co-founding and scaling the Hackers and Founders community in Guadalajara — a metro of roughly seven million people — at a time when the city hosted fewer than one or two startup events per week. He led the development of a local founders accelerator, ran investor education programs training high-net-worth individuals in angel investing, and subsequently joined Magma Partners as an operations partner, giving him a portfolio-level view of founder behavior and capital dynamics across Mexico and broader Latin America.

## Key Findings

### Finding 1: Commercial Startups Deliver More Impact Than Dedicated Impact Programs
Gutierrez is skeptical of grant-based impact investing, arguing that building large commercial companies — particularly in fintech — generates more durable social change than most purpose-built impact vehicles. He cites credit cards for unbanked consumers, easy account opening, and accessible retail investment platforms as examples of fintech products that were never labeled "impact" yet fundamentally expand economic participation. He views the convergence of for-profit scale and social outcome as the more reliable model.

### Finding 2: Community Leaders, Not Government Programs, Are the Real Ecosystem Ignition Key
The first and hardest task in ecosystem building is identifying founders and organizers who are motivated by mission rather than compensation. Gutierrez describes his own early years in Guadalajara as driven entirely by how many people could be mobilized to build with technology, with money as a distant secondary concern. He warns that government partnerships tend to misalign on objectives — officials measure new business registrations rather than outcomes, and often want unicorns without investing in the thousands of smaller companies that must exist beneath them in the return-distribution pyramid.

### Finding 3: Universities Drain Ecosystems Instead of Funding Them
Latin American universities almost universally run incubator and accelerator programs that charge companies or seek government grants rather than taking equity stakes or making seed investments. Gutierrez contrasts this with U.S. university endowments, which actively finance venture funds and make direct investments. He argues that shifting even a fraction of Latin American university program budgets toward seed capital deployment would be a high-leverage use of development-bank capacity-building funds, because universities already have the deal flow — they just lack the investment mindset.

### Finding 4: Closing a Company Is Legally and Culturally Broken in Latin America
Mexico (and by implication much of Latin America) makes it extremely difficult to wind down a company, with no clear regulatory or tax pathway for formal closure. This creates zombie companies and raises the personal cost of entrepreneurial failure dramatically. Gutierrez also notes that the absence of acqui-hire culture means that even companies with real assets — he describes a portfolio company with roughly $70,000 in annual recurring revenue across subscription and recruiting products — cannot find buyers willing to pay cents on the dollar for customers, code, or staff, as would routinely happen in the U.S.

### Finding 5: A Development-Bank M&A Debt Facility Would Unlock Exits and Safety Nets Simultaneously
Gutierrez's most original structural recommendation is a dedicated credit line to finance acquisitions of distressed or transitioning startups — explicitly earmarked for M&A activity among SMEs and startups. He notes the U.S. SBA already offers acquisition financing at 80% loan-to-value at roughly 6–7% interest, yet no equivalent exists in Latin America. Such a facility would generate exits on the upside and soft landings on the downside, creating the liquidity layer the ecosystem currently lacks entirely.

### Finding 6: Investor Education Must Include Deployment Into Mature Ecosystems, Not Just Local Deals
Angel investor training programs across Latin America typically ask nascent investors to put money into local, early-stage companies — precisely the worst environment in which to learn good investment judgment. Gutierrez argues that investors should be educated by deploying capital into established ecosystems (including Silicon Valley funds) first, so they can observe what a high-quality company looks like, before returning to invest locally. He sees hyper-local angel clubs in cities without sufficient deal maturity as a structural mismatch that produces bad outcomes and discourages repeat participation.

### Finding 7: AI Represents a Rare Re-evangelization Moment for Central America
Gutierrez views the conversational accessibility of current AI tools as a genuine discontinuity for regions where prior technology waves required programming skills to participate. He attended hackathons where non-technical participants built commercially viable products using AI tools alone. He cites El Salvador's national distribution of ChatGPT access as a meaningful unlock, and argues this moment calls for a new wave of community evangelization — analogous to the early Hackers and Founders meetup era — focused on helping people understand they can now build things regardless of their technical background.

## Regulatory & Structural Observations

The regulatory environment for business closure in Mexico is described as a fundamental drag on entrepreneurship: there is no streamlined fiscal or corporate pathway to wind down a company, partners cannot easily absorb and declare losses, and the process is slow and uncertain enough that founders simply leave companies as legal shells rather than formally closing them. Gutierrez contrasts this with the U.S. system, where acquisition (acqui-hire) functions as an implicit safety net — the worst-case outcome for a startup employee is employment at the acquiring company. This backstop does not exist in Latin America, making the subjective risk of joining or founding a startup significantly higher, which in turn suppresses talent mobility into the sector.

## Recommendations & Insights

- **Prioritize community organizer identification** over program design: find the people who will move mountains for mission, fund them lightly, and get out of the way.
- **Redirect university engagement** from advisory/training programs toward catalyzing seed investment by university endowments or program offices.
- **Advocate for regulatory reform** on company closure and loss-recognition, framing it as a prerequisite for healthy startup culture rather than a peripheral business-environment issue.
- **Design a dedicated M&A debt facility** — even a modest pilot — to finance startup acquisitions, modeled loosely on SBA acquisition lending. This could be structured as a sub-facility within a larger fund-of-funds vehicle.
- **Reframe investor education** to include cross-border deployment into mature venture ecosystems before asking angels to invest locally.
- **Use AI as the new ecosystem evangelization hook** in Central America, funding hackathons and community events that show non-technical participants they can build products today.
- **Avoid over-indexing on unicorn targets** at the program design level; instead build the pyramid from the base — thousands of $10M companies produce the conditions for $100M companies, which produce the conditions for outliers.

## Notable Quotes

> "Governments measure how many new businesses are created, but they make it impossible to close a business. We want people to take the leap, but we give them no help when they fail — and we know they're going to fail." — on the structural mismatch in government startup policy

> "Universities in Latin America see themselves as there to extract capital from companies, not inject it. When you suggest they should be investing instead of charging fees, it genuinely breaks their mental model." — on university endowment culture

> "What if you earmarked funds specifically to encourage acquisitions? When it goes well, you're generating exits. When it goes badly, you're generating a safety net. You get both outcomes from one instrument." — on a hypothetical M&A debt facility for CAF
