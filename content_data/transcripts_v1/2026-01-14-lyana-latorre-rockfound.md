## Executive Summary

Andrea Acevedo, head of the Rockefeller Foundation's Latin America and Caribbean regional office (operating for nearly one year at the time of this conversation), outlined a focused, collaborative funding strategy built around three thematic pillars — energy access, climate and health, and food systems — with school meals as the Foundation's flagship global commitment. She identified Central America as an underserved priority subregion where Rockefeller has historically lacked deep engagement, and flagged climate-driven migration along the dry corridor as a distinctly innovative focus area that no other Rockefeller regional office is pursuing.

Acevedo was direct that the Foundation does not issue open calls and is not interested in single-NGO partnerships. Instead, it actively seeks to join or scale existing multi-stakeholder initiatives, with a strong preference for private-sector involvement as genuine co-investors rather than philanthropic recipients. She proposed a focused, technical convening in Guatemala — likely in May 2026 — on climate migration with a regenerative or resilient agriculture component, aimed at mapping actors and catalyzing multi-organization commitments. A follow-up working session was scheduled for February 11, 2026.

## Stakeholder Background

Andrea Acevedo leads the Rockefeller Foundation's Latin America and Caribbean regional office, based in Bogotá, Colombia, which launched approximately one year prior to this conversation. She oversees subregional programming across Mexico, Brazil, Colombia, the Caribbean, Central America, and South America. Her work reflects a deliberate pivot toward collaborative, ecosystem-level philanthropy rather than direct grantmaking, drawing on a listening study of 70+ business and civil society leaders across 13 countries to define the office's five strategic agendas.

## Key Findings

### Finding 1: Central America Is a Strategic Priority Precisely Because Rockefeller Has Been Absent
Acevedo identified Central America as one of the subregions where the Foundation has never had deep programmatic engagement — only scattered past grants. The new regional office explicitly frames this as an opportunity and a responsibility. Current Phase 1 focus is on Colombia and Brazil, but Central America is the next target, with climate migration and the dry corridor identified as the differentiated entry point compared to what other regional offices (Africa, Asia) are doing.

### Finding 2: The School Meals "Big Bet" Carries a $100 Million Global Commitment
The Foundation made a global commitment in 2024 to reach 100 million children through school meals programming, backed by $100 million in funding. Rockefeller does not pay for meal provision itself — that is treated as a government obligation — but funds everything surrounding it: local sourcing from small farmers, regenerative agriculture, school gardens, and supply-chain development. A related grant in Honduras, implemented with FAO and the World Food Programme, is already concluding and represents the Foundation's most concrete prior engagement in the subregion.

### Finding 3: Climate Migration Along the Dry Corridor Is the Differentiating Central America Agenda
Acevedo described climate-driven migration as a topic no other Rockefeller office — not Africa, not Asia — is pursuing as a programmatic theme. For the Latin America office, the Central American dry corridor (spanning Guatemala, Honduras, El Salvador) represents the clearest geographic case: droughts destroy subsistence agriculture, triggering family and labor migration. The Foundation is explicitly looking for existing initiatives to join rather than launching projects from scratch, and sees a convening as the right first step to map the actor landscape.

### Finding 4: The Foundation Is Already Running a Climate-Health Project in Central American Sugar Mills
One active Rockefeller-funded project is already underway in Central America: a health intervention targeting sugarcane workers at mills, aimed at mitigating the effects of extreme heat on worker health. Acevedo framed this as a proof-of-concept for a private-sector partnership model — working with the mill operators to design and implement the health protocol — and noted replication potential across El Salvador and other dry-corridor countries facing similar occupational heat exposure.

### Finding 5: No Open Calls — The Foundation Seeks Subregional Partners Who Can Map and Convene
Acevedo was explicit: the Foundation does not issue open grant calls. Instead, it proactively identifies organizations with deep subregional knowledge and networks to serve as local partners. The listening report it published at the office's launch — based on interviews with 70+ CEOs, family business leaders, and civil society figures across 13 countries — produced five strategic agendas that now serve as the office's programming roadmap. Grant-seeking organizations should understand that relationship-building and demonstrated local expertise are the only pathway in.

### Finding 6: Private Sector Is a Non-Negotiable Partner in Latin America — Unlike Other Rockefeller Geographies
Acevedo emphasized that the Latin America office consciously departs from Rockefeller's standard model in the U.S. and Africa, where government is the primary counterpart. In Latin America, political volatility and government instability in coming election cycles make the private sector a more reliable and neutral co-investor. The Foundation's goal is to engage companies as genuine business partners with mutual benefit — not as philanthropic donors — and is particularly interested in agribusiness groups that already have social investment arms and understand impact.

### Finding 7: The Proposed Guatemala Convening Would Follow a Proven Model from Brazil, Colombia, and Mexico
The Foundation has already run convenings in Brazil, Colombia, and New York that produced two concrete outputs: (1) an actor map shared publicly with the ecosystem, and (2) multi-organization collaboration commitments — typically three to four organizations agreeing to pursue a joint project. Acevedo proposed replicating this model in Guatemala in Q2 2026 (targeting May), centered on climate migration with a resilient or sustainable agriculture component, deliberately avoiding the term "regenerative agriculture" because it carries financial and technical intimidation for regional agribusiness audiences.

## Regulatory & Structural Observations

Acevedo noted that the Foundation's innovative finance team — which had been a third pillar of the regional strategy alongside grants and convenings — is currently undergoing internal restructuring and is effectively paused. This means that blended finance instruments or catalytic capital mechanisms are not currently on offer from the Latin America office, though they remain a longer-term organizational direction.

## Recommendations & Insights

- A focused, technically deep (not mass) convening in Guatemala on climate migration and sustainable agriculture should be organized in Q2 2026 (May target), with an explicit design goal of producing both an actor map and a multi-stakeholder collaboration commitment.
- The framing should use "sustainable" or "resilient" agriculture rather than "regenerative agriculture" to lower the barrier to private-sector participation in the Central American context.
- Any proposal for Rockefeller engagement should position a local partner as the convener and ecosystem navigator — not Rockefeller itself — with the Foundation playing a scaling and legitimizing role.
- Honduras is a natural anchor given the existing FAO/World Food Programme school meals grant and relationships with the Secretariat of Agriculture and Livestock and the Honduran Private Enterprise Council; Guatemala is the preferred convening location given political context and existing civil-private-government dialogue experience.
- Proposals should emphasize joining or scaling something already underway, not launching new initiatives. Track record and momentum are decisive.

## Notable Quotes

> "We don't do open calls — and the reason we make calls like this one is precisely because we don't. If we opened applications, we'd be overwhelmed. What we're looking for are people and organizations with real experience and subregional knowledge who can walk alongside us." — on the Foundation's partner selection approach

> "We don't want to start anything from scratch. We believe this is not the moment to launch new things. It's more about pushing what's already moving — giving a boost to things that have a track record but lost their funding in this current context." — on the Foundation's philosophy of scaling over origination

> "Climate migration is a differentiator for us. It's not what the team in New York does, it's not what Africa does, it's not what Asia does. Central America is the place for us to bring something innovative to Rockefeller globally." — on the strategic positioning of the Central America agenda

> "In Latin America the private sector has to be in the room — I'm one hundred percent convinced of that, and I think you understand it too. We want to bring the private sector in as a partner, not as charity. A win for them, a win for the community, and a demonstration of a different way of working together." — on the private-sector partnership model

> "What came out of our convenings in Colombia was a commitment from three or four organizations to do something jointly. That's exactly what Rockefeller wants — not a single grantee, but a merge of good things. That's what I'd want to see come out of a Central America convening." — on expected convening outcomes
