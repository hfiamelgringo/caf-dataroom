## Executive Summary

Dave McClure outlines a multi-pillar framework for engineering venture ecosystems in small emerging markets, drawing on precedents from Israel (Yozma), Saudi Arabia (SVC and JADA fund-of-funds), Singapore, the UK, and the US SBIC/QSBS programs. The core innovation he proposes is a government-backed secondary market floor anchored to primary round prices — allowing early investors and employees to sell equity at a defined discount (roughly 20% for preferred, 50% for common) within a set time window, without the government needing to set prices itself. He argues this addresses the chronic liquidity problem that stunts emerging market VC more than any other single factor.

McClure is equally emphatic about the employee equity dimension: the reason Silicon Valley compounds is not professional investors alone, but the wave of employee option holders — from PayPal, Skype, Mercado Libre, Nubank — who convert modest exits into angel capital and then into new ventures. He argues small countries must engineer this flywheel explicitly or risk having the few winners simply relocate to Miami. The simplest near-term recommendation he offers is a secondary fund-of-funds, which requires far less government machinery than a direct buyback program and could be outsourced to an experienced private fund manager.

For CAF specifically, McClure sees the multilateral's institutional permanence as a strategic asset: a well-designed program brokered by CAF can lock in commitments across political cycles lasting 10–20 years, removing ecosystem development from the volatility of 4–5 year government terms.

## Stakeholder Background

Dave McClure is the founder of 500 Global (formerly 500 Startups), one of the most prolific early-stage global VC firms, having deployed into hundreds of startups across emerging markets including Latin America. He taught venture at Stanford, where one of his students later ran the Saudi Venture Capital fund-of-funds. More recently operating under the Practical VC banner, he is active in secondary market investing and has direct experience acquiring fund positions at discounts exceeding 50% post-2022. His perspective bridges operator, investor, policy designer, and educator roles.

## Key Findings

### Finding 1: A Government-Backed Secondary Market Floor Can Solve Liquidity Without Setting Prices
McClure's central proposal is for governments to offer a price backstop anchored to primary round valuations set by financial (non-strategic) investors. The government would purchase equity at roughly a 20% discount for preferred shares and 50% for common, within a three-to-twelve-month window after the primary round closes. This allows early investors and employees to achieve partial liquidity without waiting for an IPO or acquisition, without the government distorting market pricing.

### Finding 2: Saudi Arabia and Israel Provide the Most Relevant Precedents
The Saudi Venture Capital company and JADA fund-of-funds — both launched roughly seven to eight years ago, each deploying over $750 million into 40–50 VC funds of $50–100 million each — are the closest analogues to what McClure envisions for Latin America. Israel's Yozma program added the twist of allowing private investors to buy out the government's position after five to ten years. The US SBIC program has operated on a similar matching model (3:1 leverage, 7% buyback rate) for roughly forty years. Singapore's version used 4–5x leverage at modest interest rates.

### Finding 3: The Secondary Fund-of-Funds Is the Simplest Entry Point
Rather than engineering a complex government buyback mechanism, McClure recommends the most tractable first step: a fund-of-funds that invests exclusively in private secondary funds, outsourced to an experienced private fund manager. This requires minimal government machinery, leverages existing market participants' deal-selection expertise, and still channels liquidity toward the ecosystem. The trade-off is that coverage may be incomplete — secondary funds will only serve companies and positions that meet their own return thresholds.

### Finding 4: Employee Equity Is the Ecosystem Multiplier Small Countries Are Missing
McClure argues that the compounding engine of Silicon Valley is not professional VCs but employee stock option holders who convert liquidity events into angel capital. He traces this through PayPal (his own case), Skype (whose employees became the Baltic VC community), Mercado Libre (which spawned Kavak and others), and Nubank. Small countries that do not build employee equity norms — stock options, liquidity pathways — will generate a handful of rich founders who move to Miami rather than a distributed network of locally reinvesting angels.

### Finding 5: Legal Infrastructure Varies but US Standards Are Increasingly Dominant
Most emerging market startups are now incorporating in the US or in jurisdictions following US or UK legal standards (Singapore and Mauritius in Southeast Asia). A key structural gap for Latin American countries is the absence of a clear legal distinction between preferred and common stock, and the near-total absence of startup employee equity culture until roughly the last ten years. The EU was reportedly eight weeks from launching a unified European corporate structure with standardized employee stock options at time of interview, though McClure noted uncertainty about its progress.

### Finding 6: Transparency Requirements Will Be a Hard Sell to Founders
McClure flags that his proposed secondary market mechanism requires participating companies to disclose financial data — a condition most startup CEOs will resist because they see opacity as a competitive advantage and do not feel near-term pressure for liquidity the way their investors do. The practical workaround would be for VCs to pressure portfolio companies to participate as a condition of investor-level liquidity access, but this is not guaranteed to work at scale.

### Finding 7: CAF's Institutional Permanence Is Its Comparative Advantage
Venture ecosystems take 10–20 years to mature; governments typically serve 4–5 year terms. McClure identifies CAF's role as brokering a sufficiently complex, well-designed commitment that participating governments effectively take ecosystem development off their political agenda for a decade or more. Once signed, the program becomes self-sustaining and politically too complex for successors to unwind — which he frames as a feature, not a bug, of multilateral institutional design.

## Regulatory & Structural Observations

The distinction between preferred and common stock — foundational to McClure's secondary market pricing mechanism — does not exist in all Latin American legal systems, creating a structural prerequisite for regulatory reform before the model can be implemented directly. Employee stock options in startups have only become common in the region over the last ten years, and standardized documents (US-style SAFEs, Delaware incorporation) are increasingly used to bypass local legal limitations, though this workaround does not resolve liquidity pathways for employees whose options are held through local entities. McClure recommends that any regulatory agenda prioritize formalizing the preferred/common distinction and establishing startup employee equity standards as foundational infrastructure, before layering on secondary market mechanisms.

## Recommendations & Insights

- **Start with a secondary fund-of-funds**: This is the lowest-complexity, highest-credibility entry point — familiar to development banks, outsourceable to private managers, and does not require novel government machinery.
- **Build the employee equity layer explicitly**: Do not assume the angel flywheel will emerge organically. Design programs that include employee option liquidity as a first-class objective alongside investor liquidity.
- **Use international precedents strategically**: Frame the program around Israel, Saudi Arabia, Singapore, and the Nordics — not just the US — to reduce political resistance in contexts where American comparisons carry ideological baggage.
- **Outsource execution to private fund managers**: Government agencies should set the framework and provide capital, but fund selection, pricing, and portfolio management should rest with experienced private secondary market operators.
- **Anchor government pricing to primary market rounds**: Avoid having governments set valuations. Let institutional financial investors (not corporate/strategic investors) set the primary price; the government buys at a defined discount within a defined time window.
- **Design for a 10–20 year horizon**: Structure CAF's commitment so that the program survives multiple electoral cycles without requiring active political re-endorsement.

## Notable Quotes

> "The slight innovation I was trying to propose was a way to use the primary round market as a price anchor for a secondary floor that the government provides — so the government is not setting price, but using the primary market's price as an anchor and then buying at a discount to that." — explaining the core secondary market mechanism

> "In the US we now have an active secondary market that's as sizable as the amount of capital for IPOs and M&A. That's a fairly recent phenomenon. In most other parts of the world, secondary market liquidity is hardly existent at all." — on the gap between US and emerging market exit infrastructure

> "It's not the tech bros who become oligarchs and end up in Miami buying Ferraris. It's the employee stock options — making sure you have dozens or hundreds of relatively wealthy people who then say, 'I want to do that too. I can do this myself.'" — on why employee equity is the real ecosystem multiplier

> "Ecosystems take ten, fifteen, twenty years to build. Governments are usually in power for four or five. That's where CAF comes in — if they can broker the deal, it allows all the governments to take it off their political agenda for ten or fifteen years, because they've already signed on the dotted line." — on CAF's institutional role

> "Choose your medium-sized unicorn of note in any emerging market, wait five to ten years, and that group of ex-employees who made money will probably be starting and investing in their local ecosystems again." — on the compounding effect of regional exits like Mercado Libre and Nubank
