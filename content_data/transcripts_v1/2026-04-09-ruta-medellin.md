## Executive Summary

Santiago Henao leads the Medellín Venture Capital initiative at Ruta N, a public innovation agency backed by the city of Medellín. His central argument is that Medellín's startup ecosystem is sufficiently mature — sixteen years old — but is being starved of local capital: 97% of the approximately $50–60 million raised by local startups in 2024 came from outside the city, meaning any eventual exits would drain wealth rather than recycle it. To address this, Ruta N is executing a three-pillar strategy — investor education, community-building, and a fund-of-funds — that has already mobilized $46 million in confirmed public commitments and made a $1.7 million anchor investment into the national development bank's fund-of-funds vehicle that unlocked $13 million for micro-VCs.

For CAF, this conversation is directly relevant: Ruta N has explicitly modeled its fund-of-funds on international public vehicles (Israel's Yozma program, Mexico's fund-of-funds, Peru's Cofide, Chile's Corfo), has already identified CAF as a potential co-investor in its capitalization table, and is actively in dialogue with over 250 funds across Central and South America. Henao's data-driven approach — including open-source ecosystem tools (Startia and the "Inversómetro") — and his frank diagnosis of ecosystem fragmentation provide a rigorous, practitioner-level view of what it takes to build a local capital flywheel in a mid-sized Latin American city.

## Stakeholder Background

Santiago Henao has approximately fifteen years of experience in Medellín's innovation and entrepreneurship ecosystem, spanning founder, angel investor, corporate innovation, and ecosystem-builder roles — including work with Georgia Tech. He joined Ruta N roughly two years before this interview, recruited by Director Carolina Londoño following a change in city administration in 2024. He leads the Medellín Venture Capital program, Ruta N's flagship initiative to stimulate local venture investment.

## Key Findings

### Finding 1: Local capital is almost entirely absent from Medellín's startup ecosystem
In 2024, Medellín startups raised approximately $50–60 million — already a weak year by regional comparisons — but only 3% of that capital came from local investors. The remaining 97% was foreign. Henao frames this as a structural trap: without local exits that recycle capital domestically, Medellín cannot replicate the self-reinforcing flywheel that made Silicon Valley work. Local investors remain concentrated in real estate, cattle, and traditional commerce.

### Finding 2: Ruta N's Medellín Venture Capital program rests on three interlocking pillars
Launched in late 2024, Medellín Venture Capital operates across formation (training investors), community (connecting angels, family offices, CVCs, and funds), and a fund-of-funds vehicle. Since late 2024, more than 300 individuals with investment capacity have been trained through specialized courses, an AI-assisted virtual curriculum, and a cohort traveling to an overseas university program. Twenty of the first 200 graduates self-organized into a syndicate called M Capital to co-invest in startups at ticket sizes they could not reach individually.

### Finding 3: A $1.7 million anchor unlocked $13 million through the national development bank's fund-of-funds
Ruta N invested $1.7 million into a compartment within the national development finance institution's fund-of-funds vehicle, which in turn unlocked $13 million earmarked for four micro-VCs — two from Medellín, two from the rest of Colombia. The first investee, Sima Fintech Plus, already had a $5 million commitment before Ruta N's participation; that compartment is now approaching $10 million and targets 30–35 fintech startups across the region. Additional micro-VCs focused on agtech and proptech are in the pipeline.

### Finding 4: The city-level fund-of-funds has $46 million confirmed and explicitly lists CAF as a target co-investor
The Medellín Venture Capital fund-of-funds has confirmed approximately $46–50 million in public commitments from the city and EPM (the city's public utility). The program's capitalization roadmap explicitly includes multilaterals (with CAF named), domestic pension funds, and international private capital as the next tiers. The stated model is to invest in regional funds — including Central and South American vehicles — that in turn deploy into startups with impact in Medellín, not exclusively local startups.

### Finding 5: International funds cite absence of local co-investors as the primary reason for limiting Colombia exposure
Henao relayed a candid exchange with a New York-based managing partner at K50 Ventures, who explained that their Colombia allocation is capped at three startups — not by portfolio theory but because their partners see no local investors who know the market co-investing alongside them. The partner's logic: "If Colombians who are there and know what's happening aren't investing in Colombia, why would we?" Henao argues this dynamic applies across Latin America and that domestic capital mobilization is the precondition for attracting international capital.

### Finding 6: Ruta N built two open-source data tools to ground ecosystem strategy in evidence
Rather than launch programs without evidence, Henao led development of two public tools: the "Inversómetro," a five-year dashboard of VC deals, investment volumes, sectoral distribution, capital sources, and exits in Medellín and Colombia; and Startia, an open-access platform described as the "brain" of the city's startup and investment ecosystem, where all investors and startups are encouraged to register. Both tools are publicly available. Henao noted that fifteen exits have been identified in Medellín over the past five years — largely invisible in public perception — and plans to develop case studies to change the exit narrative.

### Finding 7: Ecosystem fragmentation and territorial data-hoarding remain the primary cultural obstacles
Henao's sharpest frustration is that ecosystem actors — universities, chambers of commerce, and even startups themselves — treat their data and deal flow as proprietary assets rather than shared infrastructure. Some founders objected to Startia on the grounds that startup data was their commercial property. He sees this "ego-system versus ecosystem" dynamic as the single biggest drag on collective growth, and has built reciprocity requirements into Medellín Next (a program for higher-revenue startups): graduates must mentor earlier-stage founders and engage with schools and universities.

## Regulatory & Structural Observations

Henao did not focus on regulatory frameworks directly, but noted structural references to international public fund-of-funds models that have shaped Medellín Venture Capital's design: Israel's Yozma program, Mexico's national fund-of-funds, Peru's Cofide, and Chile's Corfo. He also referenced secondary market infrastructure — specifically a domestic initiative called Petra focused on secondary markets — and expressed interest in revenue-based financing as a non-dilutive instrument for startups that cannot or should not raise equity.

## Recommendations & Insights

- **Prioritize local capital mobilization over grant programs.** Ruta N deliberately moved away from seed grants toward instruments that train investors, build syndicates, and anchor fund-of-funds vehicles. Henao believes responsible capital — not "smoke" — is what elevates ecosystem quality.
- **Use a fund-of-funds to invest regionally, not just locally.** Ruta N's model requires investee funds to deploy into startups solving local problems, but those startups can be based anywhere in Latin America (including the Caribbean and Central America). This is a deliberate design choice to connect Medellín to broader regional deal flow.
- **CAF should consider co-anchoring the Medellín Venture Capital fund-of-funds.** Henao explicitly named CAF on the capitalization roadmap and expressed openness to partnership. An anchor commitment from CAF could catalyze the transition from public-only to blended capital.
- **Exits need narrative as much as capital.** Henao is building case studies around Medellín's fifteen identified exits specifically to change investor perception — the exits exist but are invisible. CAF could support similar data and narrative infrastructure across the four target countries.
- **Reciprocity should be built into ecosystem programs.** Programs like Medellín Next require beneficiaries to give back (mentorship, school visits) as a condition of participation — a design principle worth replicating in CAF-supported ecosystems.
