## Executive Summary

Five active Central American and Panama-based venture investors convened to diagnose the structural failures holding back the regional innovation ecosystem. Their core message: the ecosystem suffers from a compounding set of gaps — insufficient early-stage capital, near-total absence of Series A and B follow-on funding, weak founder preparation, and family offices oriented toward outside-region returns — that together prevent the value-creation flywheel from ever spinning up. Liquidity was identified as the master constraint: without exits, there is no proof of concept for local investment, no recycled capital, and no learning loop for entrepreneurs.

The group converged on several structural fixes: government-backed LP matching programs modeled on Chile's Corfo and Colombia's Bancoldex, which have successfully mandated in-country deployment while making fund economics viable; coordinated technical assistance and investor education before capital deployment; and a more sophisticated blended-capital stack that moves beyond pure VC toward redeemable equity and recoverable grants to serve the "missing middle" of companies that cannot promise traditional VC exits. Several participants flagged the sudden collapse of USAID and DFC-style concessionary capital as an acute and immediate gap that risks reversing recent ecosystem progress.

## Stakeholder Background

**Gallot** is a serial entrepreneur turned first-time fund manager based in San José, Costa Rica, running an agtech-focused fund that backs Latin American science-based startups and scales them into the US market. **Abigail ("Abi")** is a Mexico-born, Costa Rica-based cofounder of Ara Impact Capital, a $2 million impact-first fund investing in the "missing middle" of Central America and southern Mexico across food security, circular economy, and climate, using a hybrid nonprofit/recoverable-grant structure. **Charles** is her Alabama-born cofounder, a ten-year Central America resident who designed the fund's blended-finance nonprofit vehicle. **Guillermo** is a Panamanian partner at Amador, an early-stage pan-Latin American VC firm founded in Panama that backed Hugo, Central America's first notable VC exit. **Rodrigo** is a general partner at Intelligent Capital (El Salvador), a $15 million fund on its third vehicle, focused on the Northern Triangle, backed by IDB Lab and DFC, with six portfolio companies.

## Key Findings

### Finding 1: The Exit Gap Is the Master Constraint — Everything Else Is Downstream

Without Series A and B investors willing to enter Central America, early-stage investors cannot exit, entrepreneurs cannot learn from success, and family offices have no local proof of return. Guillermo framed this precisely: "I think most of those problems are solved with economic value creation — and that means what happens after we invest. Who is the next one to create value after me?" Hugo's exit catalyzed a wave of returnee founders in El Salvador, demonstrating that a single exit changes the entire cultural and capital dynamic.

### Finding 2: Corfo and Bancoldex Models Are the Template for Government Action

Guillermo provided the most concrete policy prescription of the session: Chile's Corfo matches LP investments across all funds, effectively giving every LP a 1.5x MOIC floor while doubling fund deployment capacity — all conditioned on investing in Chile-domiciled companies. Colombia's Bancoldex invests as an LP requiring a 2x in-country investment match (a $2M commitment requires $4M deployed in Colombia). Guillermo argued a Central American equivalent — a regional development institution acting as a mandated LP — is the single highest-leverage intervention available.

### Finding 3: Founder Awareness of VC Is a Structural Market Failure

Rodrigo reported that Intelligent Capital repeatedly encounters high-quality founders who have never heard of venture capital and therefore never seek it out. "We find an excellent founder who didn't know VC was available in Central America — there's a missing matchmaking problem." Gallot corroborated this, noting that science-based founders lack the entrepreneurship mindset to translate research into companies, and that government prototype grants in Costa Rica are capped at roughly $15,000 — far too small to be meaningful — leaving startups "almost dead at the beginning."

### Finding 4: Ara Impact Capital's Blended Structure Reveals the Missing-Middle Problem

Ara Impact Capital operates as a nonprofit managing a $2 million pool of recoverable grants, deploying ticket sizes of $50,000–$150,000, primarily using redeemable equity (equity that the company buys back over one to five years). The structure was designed because the standard 2/20 VC model requires at least a $12 million fund to be operationally viable at that ticket size — which would take a decade to deploy at their thesis scale. After reviewing 400 companies in eighteen months, they made nine investments (seven initial, two follow-ons), and every investee required pre-investment technical assistance on basic capital structure questions before a deal could close.

### Finding 5: Central American Family Offices Are Focused Outward and Are Missing from the Local Stack

Charles and Abigail identified the absence of local high-net-worth capital as a critical gap. Most family offices in the region either focus outside Central America entirely or bifurcate into pure philanthropy versus maximum-return mandates, with nothing in between. Abigail added that international investors — including US-based funds — consistently decline Central American deal flow on reputational grounds alone, often conflating the region with Nicaragua's political situation, without engaging with country-specific data even when local fund managers provide it directly.

### Finding 6: Existing Accelerators Are Widely Viewed as Ineffective and Disconnected

Costa Rica nominally has seven or eight national acceleration programs, but participants uniformly described them as low-quality, more akin to incubators, and disconnected from both target markets and investors. Abigail rated the idea of a new "Startup Central America" program closer to 1 out of 10, specifically because of historical execution failures: "The acceleration programs already out there are really not connected with the market for the companies they work with, nor with investors. That's supposed to be one of their main jobs." Guillermo concurred that the $50,000 grant level is not the binding constraint — follow-on capital access is.

### Finding 7: The USAID/DFC Collapse Has Left an Acute Concessionary Capital Void

Charles flagged that the pullback of USAID and DFC-style programming has created an immediate funding gap that the current private ecosystem cannot absorb. "There's a huge void that USAID left — not just on the LP side, but on the philanthropic and technical assistance side as well. There's been a complete collapse of that funding." The group noted that smaller funds like Intelligent Capital ($15 million) and Ara ($2 million) were only able to raise institutional tickets at all because IDB Lab and DFC ran programs specifically designed for sub-$20 million vehicles — programs that are now at risk.

### Finding 8: International Cooperation and Cross-Border Scale Are Prerequisites

Gallot argued that Central America's small country sizes make purely national ecosystem-building futile: "Costa Rica cannot stay enclosed in Costa Rica." She is actively negotiating to bring an Argentine venture builder to Costa Rica, with an explicit condition that the program must connect across at least several Central American countries to achieve sufficient deal flow mass. She estimated it will take three to five years of this kind of preparation before the region is ready to produce investable startups at scale.

## Regulatory & Structural Observations

Abigail noted that regulatory fragmentation across Central American countries is a compounding barrier — different legal contexts for each jurisdiction mean regional funds face multiple compliance regimes, making it structurally easier for capital to flow to Brazil, Argentina, or Mexico. Her own founding experience included being forced to shut down a biotech company operating in Panama and Guatemala because customs processing times destroyed biological inputs, rendering the unit economics unworkable. She cited this as emblematic of the non-tariff regulatory burdens that kill companies before investment becomes relevant.

Abigail also raised the problem of onerous and non-standardized due diligence and reporting requirements from development finance institutions. As a nonprofit fund manager taking no management fees, she found IDB Lab's standard LP reporting requirements designed for larger commercial funds — creating significant administrative burden. She recommended that CAF and co-investing MDBs move toward shared, standardized due diligence protocols across institutions, as co-investing funds already share diligence materials with each other informally.

## Recommendations & Insights

**Guillermo:** Create a Central American equivalent of Corfo or Bancoldex — a government or MDB vehicle that acts as a mandated LP in regional funds and requires proportional in-country deployment. This is the single mechanism that has demonstrably worked in comparable smaller markets.

**Gallot:** Prioritize international cooperation to build ecosystem mass — bring established venture builders from more developed Latin American markets (Argentina, Brazil, Chile) into Central America with a regional mandate, not a country-specific one.

**Charles:** Mobilize local family offices into the middle of the capital spectrum — not pure philanthropy, not maximum-return VC, but blended instruments like recoverable grants and redeemable equity. Coordinate a shift in orientation from outside-region to in-region deployment.

**Abigail:** Standardize and share due diligence processes across MDBs to reduce reporting burden on small funds. Before any new acceleration program is launched, ensure it has genuine market connectivity and investor integration — the existing programs fail precisely because they lack both.

**Rodrigo:** Solve the awareness and matchmaking gap: great founders exist but do not know VC is available to them. A matchmaking and founder-education function — not necessarily a new accelerator — is the most pressing near-term need. A Silicon Valley soft-landing or bridging presence for post-seed companies seeking Series A would be high-value if quality-filtered.

**All participants:** Any CAF-supported program must prioritize execution quality above all else. A "Startup Central America" program modeled on Startup Chile scores low (1–3 out of 10) in current conditions because of the region's track record of poorly executed programs, but would score much higher if quality and regional coordination could be guaranteed.
