## Executive Summary
Vanessa Gibson of CINDE provided a candid assessment of Costa Rica's current investment climate and ecosystem vulnerabilities. With 420 multinational companies operating in the country, Costa Rica faces mounting pressure across its three core FDI pillars: shared services are being disrupted by AI and global talent competition, medical devices face potential damage from US Section 232 tariffs, and advanced manufacturing and semiconductors remain the one bright spot. A political transition in May 2026 adds uncertainty to an already fragile moment.

Gibson also flagged a structural policy gap that is directly relevant to CAF's mandate: Costa Rica has developed world-class capabilities for attracting foreign direct investment but has systematically underinvested in building domestic entrepreneurs. This asymmetry — strong FDI infrastructure, weak startup ecosystem support — represents both a systemic risk and an intervention opportunity. ProComer's recently opened Silicon Valley office, led by a former CINDE official with medical device expertise, signals growing awareness of the need to deepen international linkages, but the entrepreneurship development gap remains unaddressed at the policy level.

## Stakeholder Background
Vanessa Gibson works at CINDE, Costa Rica's primary investment promotion agency, which has been the institutional backbone of the country's successful FDI model for decades. Her perspective spans the full landscape of multinational engagement in Costa Rica and gives her direct visibility into how global shifts — technological, geopolitical, and trade-related — are registering among the 420 companies CINDE works with. Her insights are particularly relevant to CAF's interest in understanding how small countries can leverage FDI as a platform for broader innovation ecosystem development.

## Key Findings

### Finding 1: Costa Rica's 420 Multinationals Are Collectively Anxious About the Future
All 420 multinational companies currently operating in Costa Rica are reported to share concerns about the country's trajectory. This anxiety is not sector-specific but cuts across shared services, medical devices, and manufacturing — suggesting a systemic confidence challenge rather than isolated sectoral headwinds. The breadth of concern among established investors makes the political transition in May 2026 a particularly consequential moment for signaling policy continuity.

### Finding 2: US Section 232 Tariffs Pose a Material Threat to Costa Rica's Medical Device Sector
Costa Rica's medical device industry — one of the country's flagship FDI sectors and a major export earner — is facing direct exposure to US Section 232 trade measures. This is not a speculative risk; it is an active concern among companies in the sector. Medical devices have been a centerpiece of Costa Rica's investment promotion story, making tariff vulnerability at the US border a strategic-level threat to the country's FDI model.

### Finding 3: Advanced Manufacturing and Semiconductors Remain a Strategic Anchor
While shared services face AI-driven disruption and medical devices face tariff risk, advanced manufacturing and semiconductors remain a viable and strategically important pillar for Costa Rica's FDI agenda. Gibson identified this sector as still holding strong potential, consistent with global trends favoring nearshoring and supply chain diversification in critical technology inputs — areas where Costa Rica's established talent base and proximity to the US market are genuine advantages.

### Finding 4: ProComer Opens Silicon Valley Office to Deepen US Market Linkages
ProComer, Costa Rica's export and FDI promotion agency, recently opened an office in Santa Clara, California — one of more than 20 global offices. The office is led by Carolina Sánchez, a former CINDE official with a background in medical device investment. The Silicon Valley location signals a deliberate effort to engage the US technology and investment community directly, and represents a potential entry point for conversations about connecting Costa Rican companies to US venture capital and technology ecosystems.

### Finding 5: Costa Rica Has a Structural Gap — World-Class at Attracting FDI, Weak at Building Entrepreneurs
Gibson identified a fundamental asymmetry in Costa Rica's innovation policy: the country has built sophisticated, internationally recognized infrastructure for attracting foreign investment, but has not developed equivalent capacity to grow domestic entrepreneurs. This gap is not incidental — it reflects decades of deliberate policy prioritization. For CAF, this framing is significant: Costa Rica's FDI base could be a platform for entrepreneurship development, but institutional mechanisms to make that linkage are currently absent.

### Finding 6: May 2026 Political Transition Creates Policy Uncertainty
Costa Rica is entering a presidential transition in May 2026, with a new administration taking office. This transition introduces uncertainty about the continuity of investment promotion policies and any new entrepreneurship or innovation initiatives. The timing overlaps with the window in which CAF and partners might seek to influence policy design, making early engagement with incoming government teams strategically important.

## Regulatory & Structural Observations
The core structural observation Gibson offered is the bifurcation between Costa Rica's mature FDI attraction apparatus — embodied by CINDE and ProComer's global network — and the underdeveloped policy infrastructure for domestic entrepreneurship. This is not a regulatory gap per se, but an institutional design gap: the agencies, incentive frameworks, and public financing mechanisms that exist are oriented toward multinationals, not startups. Any effort to build a startup-enabling regulatory environment in Costa Rica will need to reckon with this institutional legacy and potentially leverage existing FDI institutions as delivery vehicles for new entrepreneurship mandates.

## Recommendations & Insights
Gibson's implicit recommendation, consistent with her framing of the FDI-entrepreneurship gap, is that Costa Rica needs dedicated policy attention to entrepreneur development — separate from and complementary to its FDI promotion machinery. The ProComer Silicon Valley office represents an asset that could be leveraged for startup-investor connectivity if its mandate were broadened beyond export and FDI promotion. The incoming administration in May 2026 represents a policy window. CAF engagement with CINDE and ProComer ahead of and during the transition could help shape how the new government thinks about linking its multinational base to a domestic innovation ecosystem.
